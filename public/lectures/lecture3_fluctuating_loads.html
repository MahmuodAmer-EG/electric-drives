Fluctuating Loads & the Flywheel

🟡 The Problem — Why Should We Care?

Picture a paper cutter in a factory. For most of each cycle it just sits there, idling. Then — slam — it drives through a thick stack of paper in a fraction of a second, demanding an enormous burst of torque. Then it's back to idling.

Now think about electric hammers, pressing machines, and punch presses. They all share the same pattern: brief, intense torque demand separated by long idle or light-load periods.

This creates two painful engineering problems:

  1. Oversized motors with poor utilization. If we pick a motor rated for the peak torque, it spends most of its time at a tiny fraction of its capacity. That's expensive hardware sitting idle — and the motor operates far from its efficient zone. We're paying for horsepower we rarely use.
  2. Grid disturbances. Every time the load slams on, the motor draws a huge current spike. Other equipment sharing the same power line sees voltage sags, flicker, and all the headaches that come with them. Your factory neighbors will not be happy.

So here's our challenge: How can we let a modest motor handle a huge intermittent load, without hammering the grid?

🔵 The Intuition — Enter the Flywheel

The solution is beautifully simple and centuries old: add a flywheel (in Arabic: الحدافة).

A flywheel is just a heavy disc — typically cast iron — with most of its mass concentrated at the outer rim. Some designs are even hollow inside. The key property is that it has a large moment of inertia \( J \).

Think of it like a mechanical battery:

  • During the light-load period: the motor doesn't need much torque, so the excess motor torque goes into speeding up the flywheel. It's charging — storing kinetic energy \(\tfrac{1}{2}J\omega^2\).
  • During the heavy-load period: the load demands far more torque than the motor can provide alone. The flywheel discharges — it slows down, converting its stored kinetic energy into the extra torque needed to meet the load.

The result? We can use a much smaller motor. The motor only needs to supply the average power over the full cycle, not the peak. The flywheel smooths out the difference.

Real-World Analogy

It's exactly like a car flywheel. In a 4-cylinder diesel engine, power strokes happen only part of each revolution. The flywheel keeps the crankshaft spinning smoothly between power strokes, preventing jerky rotation. Same idea, same physics.

🟢 Deep Dive — How the Flywheel Actually Works

Let's nail down the variables and the physics step by step.

Variables & Definitions

  • \( T_{Lh} \) — High load torque (during the working stroke)
  • \( T_{Ll} \) — Low load torque (during the idle/light period)
  • \( t_h \) — Duration of the heavy-load period
  • \( t_l \) — Duration of the light-load period
  • \( T_r \) — Rated motor torque — this should be much less than \( T_{Lh} \)
  • \( \omega_{mo} \) — No-load motor speed
  • \( \omega_{\max} \) — Maximum speed (reached at end of light-load period)
  • \( \omega_{\min} \) — Minimum speed (reached at end of heavy-load period)
  • \( T_{\max} \) — Motor torque at \(\omega_{\min}\) (highest motor torque in the cycle)
  • \( T_{\min} \) — Motor torque at \(\omega_{\max}\) (lowest motor torque in the cycle)
  • \( J_{fw} \) — Moment of inertia of the flywheel

The Fundamental Equation of Motion

At every instant, Newton's second law for rotation tells us:

Equation of Motion
\[ J_{\text{total}} \frac{d\omega}{dt} = T_{\text{motor}} - T_{\text{load}} \]

Where \( J_{\text{total}} = J_{\text{motor}} + J_{\text{flywheel}} + J_{\text{load}} \).

During the Heavy-Load Period

The load torque \( T_{Lh} \) exceeds the motor torque. So \( T_{\text{motor}} - T_{Lh} < 0 \), which means \( d\omega/dt < 0 \) — the system decelerates. The flywheel is releasing its stored energy to help the motor cope.

Speed drops from \(\omega_{\max}\) down to \(\omega_{\min}\).

During the Light-Load Period

The motor torque exceeds the light load \( T_{Ll} \). So \( T_{\text{motor}} - T_{Ll} > 0 \), and the system accelerates. The flywheel absorbs this surplus energy, speeding up from \(\omega_{\min}\) back to \(\omega_{\max}\).

Key Insight: Energy Balance

In steady-state, the energy the flywheel stores during the light period exactly equals the energy it releases during the heavy period. The speed oscillates between \(\omega_{\min}\) and \(\omega_{\max}\) every cycle, but the average speed stays constant.

Benefits of Adding a Flywheel

  1. Smaller motor: We rate the motor for average power, not peak power.
  2. Smoother current draw: No violent current spikes — the grid is happy.
  3. Speed regulation: Speed varies within a controlled band \([\omega_{\min}, \omega_{\max}]\).
  4. Lower cost: Smaller motor + smoother operation = cheaper system overall.

🟢 Deep Dive — Calculating the Flywheel Inertia Jfw

Now let's do the math. We want to find the minimum flywheel inertia \( J_{fw} \) so that the motor torque never exceeds a specified maximum \( T_{\max} \).

Assumptions

  • The motor has a linear speed-torque characteristic in the operating region.
  • The load torque switches sharply between \( T_{Lh} \) and \( T_{Ll} \).
  • We are in periodic steady state: the speed pattern repeats every cycle.

Step 1: Linear Motor Characteristic

For a motor with a linear \(T\)-\(\omega\) characteristic, we can write:

Motor Characteristic
\[ T = T_{\text{stall}} \left(1 - \frac{\omega}{\omega_{mo}}\right) \quad \text{or equivalently} \quad \omega = \omega_{mo}\left(1 - \frac{T}{T_{\text{stall}}}\right) \]

We can also express the relationship between speed and torque changes as a slope:

\[ \frac{d\omega}{dT} = -\frac{\omega_{mo}}{T_{\text{stall}}} = \text{const} \]

Step 2: Energy Balance Over the Heavy-Load Period

During the heavy-load period \( t_h \), the speed drops from \(\omega_{\max}\) to \(\omega_{\min}\). The kinetic energy released by the flywheel (and the entire rotating system) must equal the deficit between what the load demands and what the motor provides:

Energy Balance (Heavy Load)
\[ \frac{1}{2} J_{\text{total}} \left(\omega_{\max}^2 - \omega_{\min}^2\right) = \int_0^{t_h} \left(T_{Lh} - T_{\text{motor}}(t)\right) \, dt \]

Step 3: Simplification for Linear Characteristics

If we assume the motor torque varies approximately linearly between \(T_{\max}\) (at \(\omega_{\min}\)) and some intermediate value during the deceleration, we can approximate the integral. For a linear motor curve, the average motor torque during the heavy-load period is:

\[ \bar{T}_{\text{motor,h}} \approx \frac{T_{\max} + T_{\min}}{2} \]

Wait — that's not quite right for the heavy period alone. More precisely, during the heavy-load period the motor torque varies from some value at \(\omega_{\max}\) to \(T_{\max}\) at \(\omega_{\min}\). Let's use the exact energy approach instead.

Step 4: Exact Approach Using the Equation of Motion

We start from:

\[ J \frac{d\omega}{dt} = T(\omega) - T_L \]

For the linear motor characteristic \( T(\omega) = T_{\text{stall}}\!\left(1 - \frac{\omega}{\omega_{mo}}\right) \), during the heavy load:

\[ J \frac{d\omega}{dt} = T_{\text{stall}}\!\left(1 - \frac{\omega}{\omega_{mo}}\right) - T_{Lh} \]

This is a first-order linear ODE in \(\omega\). Solving it (or using the energy approach by multiplying both sides by \(\omega\) and integrating) gives us the relationship between \(J\), the speed limits, the load torque, and the time period.

The practical formula, derived from the energy balance, is:

Flywheel Inertia Formula
\[ J_{fw} = \frac{\displaystyle\int_0^{t_h} \left(T_{Lh} - T_{\text{motor}}\right) dt} {\frac{1}{2}\left(\omega_{\max}^2 - \omega_{\min}^2\right)} - J_{\text{motor}} - J_{\text{load}} \]

Or equivalently, since \(\omega_{\max}^2 - \omega_{\min}^2 = (\omega_{\max}+\omega_{\min})(\omega_{\max}-\omega_{\min})\):

\[ J_{fw} \approx \frac{2 \times E_{\text{deficit}}} {(\omega_{\max}+\omega_{\min})(\omega_{\max}-\omega_{\min})} - J_{\text{existing}} \]

Step 5: Practical Simplification

When the speed variation is small (which is usually what we want), we can approximate \(\omega_{\max} + \omega_{\min} \approx 2\bar{\omega}\) where \(\bar{\omega}\) is the average speed. Then:

Simplified Formula
\[ J_{\text{total}} \approx \frac{E_{\text{deficit}}}{\bar{\omega} \cdot \Delta\omega} \]

where \(\Delta\omega = \omega_{\max} - \omega_{\min}\) is the allowable speed drop.

Design Trade-off

A bigger flywheel (larger \(J_{fw}\)) means a smaller speed drop \(\Delta\omega\), but the flywheel itself is heavier, more expensive, and takes longer to start up. There's always a balance between smooth operation and practical constraints.

Takeaway — Fluctuating Loads
  • Intermittent loads demand huge peak torque but low average torque.
  • A flywheel acts as a mechanical energy buffer — stores energy during light periods, releases it during heavy periods.
  • This lets us use a smaller, cheaper motor rated for average (not peak) power.
  • The grid sees smooth, steady current instead of violent spikes.
  • The trade-off: speed does vary within a band \([\omega_{\min}, \omega_{\max}]\), and the flywheel adds weight and cost.
  • The required \(J_{fw}\) is determined by the energy deficit during the heavy-load period and the allowable speed drop.

Constant Power Drives (Take-up Rolls)

🟡 The Problem — Winding Material onto a Roll

Think about a machine that winds paper, plastic film, wire, or steel strip onto a roll. This is a take-up reel (or coiler).

Here's the catch: as material builds up on the roll, the radius increases. But we need the linear speed of the material (the speed at which the strip feeds in) to stay constant — otherwise the material stretches, sags, or breaks.

So as the roll gets fatter, the motor must spin slower. And since the roll is heavier and has a larger moment arm, the motor must provide more torque. This creates a unique operating profile that we need to understand.

🔵 The Intuition — Why It's "Constant Power"

Let's think about this with simple relationships:

  • The material feeds at constant linear speed \( v \).
  • The tension force \( F \) on the material is constant (set by the process).
  • Mechanical power = \( P = F \times v \). Both are constant, so power is constant.

Now let's look at what happens to the motor:

  • Linear speed: \( v = \omega_m \times r \). Since \( v \) is constant and \( r \) increases, \( \omega_m \) must decrease.
  • Motor torque: \( T = F \times r \). Since \( F \) is constant and \( r \) increases, \( T \) must increase.
  • Power: \( P = T \times \omega_m \). One goes up, the other goes down — the product stays the same!

This is why we call it a constant power drive. The motor must be rated for the maximum torque (which occurs at the largest roll radius), but it operates at constant power throughout.

Figure 4: Take-up reel drive. As material winds on, the radius grows. The motor must slow down to keep linear speed constant, while torque increases. Power = T × ω stays constant.
🟢 Deep Dive — The Mathematics of Constant Power

Fundamental Relationships

Let's define our variables clearly:

  • \( v \) — Linear speed of the material (constant)
  • \( F \) — Tension force on the material (constant)
  • \( r \) — Current radius of the roll (increases with time)
  • \( \omega_m \) — Angular speed of the roll/motor
Kinematic Constraint
\[ v = \omega_m \times r \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \omega_m = \frac{v}{r} \]

As \( r \) increases, \( \omega_m \) decreases inversely.

Torque Relationship
\[ T = F \times r \]

As \( r \) increases, torque increases linearly.

Power
\[ P = T \times \omega_m = (F \times r) \times \frac{v}{r} = F \times v = \text{constant} \]

Motor Rating

The motor must handle:

  • Maximum torque \( T_{\max} = F \times r_{\max} \) (at full roll)
  • Maximum speed \( \omega_{\max} = v / r_{\min} \) (at empty core)
  • Constant power \( P = F \times v \) throughout

The speed range is:

\[ \text{Speed range} = \frac{\omega_{\max}}{\omega_{\min}} = \frac{r_{\max}}{r_{\min}} \]

This ratio can be quite large (3:1 to 10:1 or more), which makes motor selection an important design task. The motor must maintain rated torque at the lowest speed and handle the full speed range.

Practical Note

In practice, these drives often use DC motors with field weakening or variable-frequency AC drives to achieve the wide speed range required. The drive must provide constant torque at low speeds and constant power at high speeds — or vice versa, depending on the starting condition.

Takeaway — Constant Power Drives
  • Take-up reel drives wind material at constant linear speed and constant tension.
  • Since \( P = F \times v = \) const, this is a constant power application.
  • As the roll radius grows: speed decreases, torque increases, power stays the same.
  • The motor must be rated for maximum torque at minimum speed.
  • The speed ratio \( r_{\max}/r_{\min} \) determines the required speed range of the drive.

Compressor Drives

🟡 The Problem — Keeping Things Cold

Open your kitchen — that humming box is your refrigerator. The heart of every refrigerator, air conditioner, and heat pump is a compressor.

Compressors have a very characteristic load profile: the torque they demand is proportional to speed. Run the compressor faster, and it has to work harder to compress the gas. This gives us a \( T \propto \omega \) load characteristic.

Another important feature: compressors never need to reverse direction. The gas flow is always one way through the refrigeration cycle. This simplifies the drive requirements considerably.

🔵 The Intuition — How Refrigeration Works

Before we dive into the motor characteristics, let's understand what the compressor is doing. The refrigeration cycle is elegantly simple once you see it:

  1. Compression: The compressor squeezes the refrigerant gas to high pressure. This also heats it up (think: bicycle pump getting warm).
  2. Condensation: The hot, high-pressure gas flows through the condenser (the coils on the outside/back of your fridge). It exchanges heat with the ambient air, cools down, and liquefies.
  3. Expansion: The liquid refrigerant passes through a tiny expansion valve. Pressure drops dramatically. (Like releasing an aerosol can — the escaping gas feels cold.)
  4. Evaporation: The low-pressure liquid enters the evaporator (the coils inside the fridge). Here it expands and boils, absorbing heat from the fridge interior. This is what makes things cold!
  5. Return: The refrigerant, now a gas again, returns to the compressor, and the cycle repeats.

The compressor is the engine of this cycle. Its electric motor must run reliably, efficiently, and in one direction only.

Figure 5: Simplified refrigeration cycle. The compressor drives the cycle — compressing gas, which gets cooled in the condenser, expanded through a valve, and then evaporated to absorb heat.
🟢 Deep Dive — Compressor Load Characteristic

The T ∝ ω Characteristic

For a compressor, the load torque is proportional to speed:

Compressor Load Characteristic
\[ T_L = k \cdot \omega \]

where \( k \) is a constant that depends on the compressor design and operating conditions.

The mechanical power consumed is therefore:

\[ P = T_L \times \omega = k \cdot \omega^2 \]

So power goes as the square of speed. Double the speed and power consumption quadruples. This is different from both the fluctuating load (where torque varies with time) and the constant power drive (where power is fixed).

Key Characteristics of Compressor Drives

Load Profile

  • \( T \propto \omega \) (linear)
  • \( P \propto \omega^2 \) (quadratic)
  • Starting torque can be high

Drive Requirements

  • Unidirectional — no reversal
  • Variable speed desirable for efficiency
  • Soft starting often needed

Motor Selection for Compressors

Because the load torque is proportional to speed, the motor operating point is naturally stable. If the motor speeds up slightly, the load increases, which tends to slow it back down. This is a self-regulating characteristic.

Common motor choices for compressors include:

  • Single-phase induction motors (small refrigerators, AC units)
  • Three-phase induction motors (industrial refrigeration)
  • Permanent magnet synchronous motors (modern inverter-driven compressors for energy efficiency)
Energy Efficiency

Modern inverter-driven compressors vary their speed to match the cooling demand. Instead of cycling on and off (which wastes energy at each start), they run continuously at a lower speed. Since \( P \propto \omega^2 \), running at half speed uses only one-quarter the power. This is why inverter AC units are so much more efficient.

Takeaway — Compressor Drives
  • Compressors have a \( T \propto \omega \) load characteristic (torque proportional to speed).
  • Power consumption follows \( P \propto \omega^2 \) — very sensitive to speed.
  • No direction reversal needed — simplifies the drive.
  • The self-regulating nature makes the operating point inherently stable.
  • Variable-speed drives (inverters) can dramatically improve compressor efficiency.
  • The refrigeration cycle: Compress → Condense → Expand → Evaporate → Repeat.

Practice Problems (Sheet 01)

Problem 16 — Flywheel with Wound Rotor IM

Given:

  • 6-pole, 50 Hz wound rotor induction motor
  • Flywheel with \( J = 1000 \; \text{kg} \cdot \text{m}^2 \)
  • Load: \( T_L = 1000 \; \text{N} \cdot \text{m} \) for 10 seconds, then no-load
  • Motor has a linear torque-speed characteristic in the operating region

Find:

  1. The maximum motor torque during the cycle
  2. The speed at the end of the deceleration (heavy load) period

Solution Approach

Step 1: Find the synchronous speed.

\[ n_s = \frac{120 f}{p} = \frac{120 \times 50}{6} = 1000 \; \text{rpm} \] \[ \omega_s = \frac{2\pi \times 1000}{60} = 104.72 \; \text{rad/s} \]

Step 2: Apply the equation of motion. During the heavy-load period, the motor decelerates as the flywheel gives up energy. During the no-load period, the motor accelerates back up. In steady state, the energy stored equals energy released.

Step 3: Use the energy balance and the linear motor characteristic to solve for \( T_{\max} \), \( \omega_{\min} \), and \( \omega_{\max} \). The key equations are:

\[ J(\omega_{\max} - \omega_{\min}) = (T_{Lh} - \bar{T}_{\text{motor}}) \times t_h \] \[ \frac{1}{2}J(\omega_{\max}^2 - \omega_{\min}^2) = \int_0^{t_h}(T_L - T_{\text{motor}})d t \]

The detailed numerical solution requires iterating with the linear motor characteristic or solving the first-order ODE analytically.

Problem 17 — Designing the Flywheel Inertia

Given:

  • Motor with a linear torque-speed characteristic
  • Load: \( T_L = 600 \; \text{N} \cdot \text{m} \) for 10 seconds, then light/no-load
  • Requirement: limit motor torque to a maximum of \( T_{\max} = 450 \; \text{N} \cdot \text{m} \)

Find:

  • The required moment of inertia \( J \) of the flywheel

Solution Approach

Key idea: The motor can only supply up to 450 N·m, but the load demands 600 N·m. The difference of 150 N·m must come from the flywheel's stored kinetic energy.

Step 1: Calculate the energy deficit during the heavy-load period:

\[ E_{\text{deficit}} \approx (T_L - T_{\max}) \times t_h = (600 - 450) \times 10 = 1500 \; \text{N} \cdot \text{m} \cdot \text{s} \]

(This is a simplified approximation. The actual deficit depends on how motor torque varies with speed during deceleration.)

Step 2: Determine the allowable speed drop \(\Delta\omega\) and average speed \(\bar{\omega}\) from the motor characteristic and the rated operating point.

Step 3: Apply the flywheel inertia formula:

\[ J_{fw} = \frac{E_{\text{deficit}}}{\bar{\omega} \cdot \Delta\omega} \]

The exact values depend on the specific motor parameters (stall torque, no-load speed) which determine the operating speed range.

Lecture Summary

Key Takeaways from Lecture 3
  1. Fluctuating loads (punch presses, hammers) cycle between high and low torque. A flywheel smooths this out by storing and releasing kinetic energy, allowing a smaller motor and cleaner grid current.
  2. Constant power drives (take-up reels) maintain constant linear speed and tension. As the roll radius grows, speed drops and torque rises, but \( P = T\omega = Fv = \) constant. The motor must handle the full speed range and maximum torque.
  3. Compressor drives have \( T \propto \omega \) and \( P \propto \omega^2 \). They never reverse direction. Variable-speed operation (via inverters) can dramatically improve energy efficiency.
  4. Each load type requires a different motor selection and drive strategy. Understanding the load characteristic is the first step in designing any electric drive system.
