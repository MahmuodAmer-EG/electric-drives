DC Drives — Introduction & Single-Phase Converter Drives

Understanding DC motor fundamentals, speed control strategies, and single-phase power converter topologies for driving DC machines.

1. DC Motors — Introduction

▾
The Problem / The Why

Before we can build a drive, we need to understand the machine it controls. DC motors have been the workhorse of variable-speed drives for over a century. Even though AC drives are taking over in many areas, DC drives are still everywhere — in traction systems, rolling mills, cranes, and legacy industrial plants. Understanding DC motor physics is also the single best foundation for understanding any electric drive, because the relationships between voltage, current, flux, torque, and speed are beautifully direct.

The Intuition

Think of a DC motor like a water wheel. The armature voltage is the water pressure pushing the wheel. The field flux is how big the wheel's paddles are. The back-EMF is the "pushback" the spinning wheel creates against the incoming water. The bigger the paddles (more flux) or the higher the pressure (more voltage), the faster or harder the wheel turns.

Deep Dive — DC Motor Types

DC motors are classified by how their field winding is connected relative to the armature:

TypeField ConnectionKey Feature
Separately ExcitedIndependent supply for fieldBest controllability, independent flux & armature control
ShuntField in parallel with armatureNearly constant speed (like sep. excited when supply is stiff)
SeriesField in series with armatureHigh starting torque, speed drops sharply with load (traction)
CompoundBoth shunt + series windingsIntermediate characteristics, cumulative or differential
Permanent MagnetMagnets replace field windingSimple, compact, constant flux (like sep. excited at fixed field)

For drives, the separately excited motor is the most important because we can control armature voltage and field current independently — giving us maximum flexibility.

Takeaway

DC motors translate between electrical and mechanical domains through a direct, linear relationship involving flux. The separately excited motor is the default "lab rat" of electric drives because it offers independent control knobs for voltage and field.

2. Characteristics of DC Motors

▾

a) Separately Excited Motor

The Problem / The Why

To control a motor, we need its mathematical "blueprint" — the equations that link what we can control (voltage, field current) to what we want (speed, torque). Without these, we're driving blind.

The Intuition

Imagine pushing a child on a swing. You push with a force (applied voltage \(V_a\)). Some of that effort is lost to friction at the joints (the \(I_a R_a\) drop). The rest goes into maintaining the swing's motion (the back-EMF \(E_g\)). As the swing goes faster, it "pushes back" harder, and you need to push harder just to maintain speed. That pushback is the back-EMF.

Figure 1: Separately excited DC motor circuit. The armature and field circuits have independent supplies.

Deep Dive — Governing Equations

Let's write down the three fundamental equations of the separately excited DC motor:

Armature Voltage Equation
\[ V_a = E_g + I_a R_a \]
Back-EMF
\[ E_g = K\phi \cdot \omega_m \]
Torque Equation
\[ T = K\phi \cdot I_a \]

where:

  • \(V_a\) = armature terminal voltage
  • \(E_g\) = back-EMF (generated voltage)
  • \(I_a\) = armature current
  • \(R_a\) = armature resistance (small, typically a fraction of an ohm)
  • \(K\phi\) = motor constant × flux (often written as \(K_\phi\) or simply \(K\))
  • \(\omega_m\) = mechanical angular speed (rad/s)
  • \(T\) = electromagnetic torque (N·m)

Combining these, we get the torque-speed characteristic:

Torque-Speed Relationship
\[ \omega_m = \frac{V_a}{K\phi} - \frac{R_a}{(K\phi)^2} \cdot T \]

This is a straight line with:

  • No-load speed: \(\omega_{m0} = \dfrac{V_a}{K\phi}\)   (intercept on the speed axis)
  • Speed drop: \(\Delta\omega_m = \dfrac{R_a}{(K\phi)^2} \cdot T\)   (how much speed drops under load)

Since \(R_a\) is small, the slope is gentle — the speed is nearly constant, dropping only slightly under load. This is exactly the behavior we want for most industrial applications.

Figure 2: Torque-speed characteristic of a separately excited DC motor — nearly flat, with a small speed droop.

b) Comparison of DC Motor Types

The Intuition

Different field connections give dramatically different personality traits. A shunt motor is like a steady, reliable worker — nearly constant speed regardless of load. A series motor is like a sprinter — incredible burst of torque at low speeds, but it must never be unloaded or it runs away to dangerously high speeds. A compound motor tries to blend both personalities.

Figure 3: Torque-speed characteristics of different DC motor types. Notice how the series motor speed shoots up dangerously at light loads.

Deep Dive — Torque vs. Armature Current

The relationship between torque and armature current also differs by type:

  • Separately excited / Shunt: \(T = K\phi \cdot I_a\). Since \(\phi\) is constant, \(T\) is proportional to \(I_a\) — a straight line.
  • Series: At low currents (unsaturated), \(\phi \propto I_a\), so \(T = K I_a^2\) — a parabola. At high currents (saturated), \(\phi \approx \text{const}\) and we're back to a linear relationship.
  • Cumulative compound: Slightly more than linear because both the series and shunt fields contribute.

Figure 4: Torque vs. armature current for different DC motor types.

Takeaway

The separately excited motor gives a nearly flat speed-torque curve — great for constant-speed applications. The series motor gives huge starting torque but is unstable at light loads. For drives, we almost always work with the separately excited type because its linear, decoupled behavior makes control straightforward.

3. Starting of DC Motors

▾
The Problem / The Why

Here's a nasty surprise waiting for anyone who just "switches on" a DC motor. At standstill (\(\omega_m = 0\)), the back-EMF is zero. That means the entire supply voltage appears across the tiny armature resistance. The resulting current can be 10–20 times the rated value — enough to destroy the commutator, blow fuses, or damage the power supply. We must limit the starting current.

The Intuition

It's like trying to push-start a car on a flat road. At first, the car isn't moving (no back-EMF), so you bear the full brunt of your pushing effort against just friction (small \(R_a\)). The current (your effort) is enormous. As the car picks up speed, it starts rolling on its own (back-EMF builds up), and you can ease off. That's why we need to start gently.

Deep Dive

At standstill: \(\omega_m = 0 \implies E_g = K\phi \cdot 0 = 0\)

Starting Current (no protection)
\[ I_{a,\text{start}} = \frac{V_a}{R_a} \gg I_{a,\text{rated}} \]

For example, if \(V_a = 230\text{ V}\) and \(R_a = 0.1\;\Omega\), then \(I_{a,\text{start}} = 2300\text{ A}\)! If the rated current is, say, 100 A, that's 23 times the rated current.

Solutions:

  • Starting resistance: Add a resistor \(R_{\text{start}}\) in series with the armature. As the motor speeds up and \(E_g\) rises, progressively short-circuit sections of this resistor until it's fully removed at operating speed. Simple but wastes energy.
  • Reduced voltage starting: Use a converter (like the ones we'll study shortly) to apply a low voltage initially and gradually ramp it up. This is the modern approach and wastes no energy in resistors — and it's precisely what DC drives do.
Takeaway

Never apply full voltage to a stationary DC motor. The starting current is dangerously high because there's no back-EMF to oppose it. Modern DC drives solve this elegantly by controlling the voltage from zero upward — which is one of the main reasons drives exist in the first place.

4. Speed Control Methods

▾
The Problem / The Why

Industrial processes need motors that can run at different speeds: a conveyor belt during loading vs. unloading, a crane hoisting a heavy load vs. moving an empty hook. How do we change the speed of a DC motor smoothly and efficiently? There are exactly two knobs we can turn, and each one works best in a different speed range.

Looking at the speed equation \(\omega_m = \frac{V_a}{K\phi} - \frac{R_a}{(K\phi)^2} T\), we see two independent variables we can control: \(V_a\) and \(\phi\).

Method 1: Armature Voltage Control (Below Base Speed)

The Intuition

Imagine a garden hose. If we reduce the water pressure (lower \(V_a\)) while keeping the nozzle the same size (same \(\phi\)), the water comes out slower. The motor slows down. Since we haven't changed the "size of the nozzle" (the flux), the motor can still produce the same maximum force (torque) at any speed — it just can't go as fast.

Deep Dive

When we reduce \(V_a\) while keeping \(\phi\) constant (field at rated value):

  • The no-load speed \(\omega_{m0} = V_a / (K\phi)\) decreases proportionally with \(V_a\).
  • The slope of the T-ω curve \(\frac{R_a}{(K\phi)^2}\) stays the same — the curves shift down in parallel.
  • Torque capability: \(T_{\max} = K\phi \cdot I_{a,\text{max}}\) is unchanged (constant flux, same current limit).
  • Power capability: \(P = T \cdot \omega_m\) decreases with speed. Less speed = less power, even at full torque.
Armature Voltage Control Region
\[ \text{For } \omega_m \leq \omega_r: \quad T_{\max} = \text{const}, \quad P_{\max} \propto \omega_m \]

This is called the constant torque region.

Method 2: Field Control / Field Weakening (Above Base Speed)

The Intuition

Now we're at full voltage and want to go even faster. We can't increase \(V_a\) beyond rated. But we can reduce the field flux \(\phi\) by reducing \(I_f\). Less flux means less back-EMF per unit speed, so the motor spins faster to "catch up." The trade-off? With weaker flux, each amp of armature current produces less torque. We keep the same power (voltage × current), but trade torque for speed — like shifting to a higher gear in a car.

Deep Dive

When we reduce \(\phi\) while \(V_a\) stays at rated value:

  • The no-load speed \(\omega_{m0} = V_a/(K\phi)\) increases as \(\phi\) decreases.
  • Torque capability \(T_{\max} = K\phi \cdot I_{a,\text{max}}\) decreases proportionally.
  • Power capability \(P = V_a \cdot I_a\) remains constant (same voltage, same current limit).
Field Weakening Region
\[ \text{For } \omega_m \geq \omega_r: \quad P_{\max} = \text{const}, \quad T_{\max} \propto \frac{1}{\omega_m} \]

This is called the constant power region.

Combined Characteristic

Figure 5: Combined speed control characteristic. Below base speed: constant torque (armature voltage control). Above base speed: constant power (field weakening).

Takeaway

We have two control knobs: armature voltage (for speeds from zero to base speed, giving constant torque) and field weakening (for speeds above base speed, giving constant power). Together, they give us a wide speed range — and this is exactly the strategy used in every DC drive system.

5. DC Drive Classification

▾
The Problem / The Why

We know we need to control the armature voltage. But where does this controlled DC voltage come from? The power grid gives us AC, so we need a converter that turns AC into variable DC. The type of converter we choose determines the cost, performance, and capability of the entire drive system.

Deep Dive — Classification

DC drives are classified by their power converter topology:

CategorySourceKey Devices
Single-Phase Drives1-phase AC supplyThyristors + Diodes
Three-Phase Drives3-phase AC supplyThyristors (SCRs)
Chopper DrivesDC supply (battery, DC bus)IGBTs, MOSFETs

In this lecture, we focus on single-phase drives. These are used for small to medium power applications (up to about 15 kW). Let's explore each converter topology.

6. Single-Phase Half-Wave Converter Drive

▾
The Problem / The Why

Let's start with the simplest possible converter. We have a single-phase AC source and we want to produce a controllable DC voltage for a small motor (say, a small fan or pump under 0.5 kW). What's the minimum circuit that gets the job done?

The Intuition

A single thyristor acts like a door with a delay timer. It only lets current through during the positive half of the AC cycle, and we can choose when during that half-cycle to open the door (the firing angle \(\alpha\)). The later we open it, the less average voltage (and thus less speed) the motor gets. A freewheeling diode provides a path for the motor current to circulate when the thyristor is off, keeping the current flowing smoothly through the motor's inductance.

Figure 6: Single-phase half-wave converter drive with thyristor T1 and freewheeling diode Dm.

Deep Dive

The source voltage is \(v_s = V_m \sin(\omega t)\). The thyristor T1 is fired at angle \(\alpha\) during each positive half-cycle.

Average Output Voltage
\[ V_a = \frac{V_m}{2\pi}(1 + \cos\alpha) \]

Key properties:

  • Power range: Up to ~0.5 kW (small loads)
  • Voltage range: From \(V_m/\pi\) (at \(\alpha = 0\)) down to 0 (at \(\alpha = \pi\))
  • Quadrant operation: ONE quadrant only
  • The freewheeling diode \(D_m\) carries the motor current during the negative half-cycle when T1 turns off, preventing negative voltage across the motor

Figure 7: Half-wave converter operates in Quadrant I only (+Va, +Ia).

Takeaway

The half-wave converter is the simplest topology — one thyristor, one diode. It gives us variable DC voltage for small motors but is limited to one-quadrant operation (forward motoring only). For anything bigger or more capable, we need a bridge.

7. Single-Phase Semi-Converter (Half-Controlled Bridge)

▾
The Problem / The Why

The half-wave converter wastes half the AC cycle and can only handle tiny loads. For medium-power applications (up to 15 kW), we need a bridge rectifier. But full bridges with all thyristors are expensive. Can we get decent performance with a half-and-half approach — some thyristors, some cheaper diodes?

The Intuition

Think of a bridge with two "smart gates" (thyristors T1, T3) and two "dumb gates" (diodes D2, D4). The smart gates decide when to start conducting (firing angle \(\alpha\)), while the dumb gates just let current through whenever it wants to flow. This combo uses both halves of the AC cycle but still lets us control the average voltage. And because the diodes provide a freewheeling path inside the bridge, the output voltage is always positive.

Figure 8: Single-phase semi-converter (half-controlled bridge). T1,D4 conduct in the positive half; T3,D2 conduct in the negative half.

Deep Dive
Average Output Voltage — Semi-Converter
\[ V_a = \frac{V_m}{\pi}(1 + \cos\alpha) \]

Compare this with the half-wave: the factor out front is \(V_m/\pi\) instead of \(V_m/(2\pi)\) — double the voltage because we use both half-cycles.

Key properties:

  • Power range: Up to ~15 kW
  • Voltage range: \(V_a\) varies from \(2V_m/\pi\) (at \(\alpha = 0\)) to 0 (at \(\alpha = \pi\))
  • Output voltage is always ≥ 0 (diodes provide freewheeling, clamping voltage to zero)
  • Quadrant operation: ONE quadrant (+\(V_a\), +\(I_a\)) — Quadrant I only
  • Cannot reverse voltage, so no regenerative braking
  • Better power factor than full converter at large firing angles because of freewheeling

Figure 9: Semi-converter operates in Quadrant I only (+Va, +Ia).

Takeaway

The semi-converter doubles the half-wave's voltage capability by using both AC half-cycles, handles up to 15 kW, and is cheaper than a full bridge because it uses only 2 thyristors instead of 4. The trade-off: still limited to one-quadrant operation (forward motoring), no braking capability.

8. Single-Phase Full Converter (Fully-Controlled Bridge)

▾
The Problem / The Why

One-quadrant operation means we can only accelerate the motor in one direction. What if we need to stop it quickly? In an elevator, a crane, or a machine tool, we often need to brake the motor electrically — which requires sending energy back to the supply. For that, we need the output voltage to go negative while the current stays positive. The semi-converter can't do this. The full converter can.

The Intuition

Now all four switches in the bridge are thyristors — all four gates are "smart." When the firing angle is less than 90 degrees, we get positive average voltage (motoring, like before). But here's the magic: when we push \(\alpha\) past 90 degrees, the average voltage flips negative! The motor's back-EMF now drives current back into the supply, acting as a generator. We're braking by regeneration — recovering energy. Current still flows in the same direction through the motor, but the power flow has reversed.

Figure 10: Single-phase full converter (fully-controlled bridge). All four devices are thyristors.

Deep Dive
Average Output Voltage — Full Converter
\[ V_a = \frac{2V_m}{\pi}\cos\alpha \]

This is a cosine function! Notice what happens:

  • At \(\alpha = 0\): \(V_a = +2V_m/\pi\) (maximum positive)
  • At \(\alpha = 90°\): \(V_a = 0\)
  • At \(\alpha = 180°\): \(V_a = -2V_m/\pi\) (maximum negative)

Key properties:

  • Power range: Up to ~15 kW
  • TWO quadrant operation: \(V_a\) can be positive or negative, but \(I_a\) flows in one direction only (through thyristors)
  • Quadrant I (\(\alpha < 90°\)): +\(V_a\), +\(I_a\) → Motoring (forward)
  • Quadrant IV (\(\alpha > 90°\)): −\(V_a\), +\(I_a\) → Regenerative braking (energy returned to supply)
  • No freewheeling diode — current must always flow through thyristor pairs

Figure 11: Full converter operates in Quadrants I and IV (+Ia, ±Va).

Takeaway

The full converter is the first topology that gives us two-quadrant operation. By pushing the firing angle past 90 degrees, we reverse the voltage and enable regenerative braking — the motor becomes a generator and pumps energy back to the grid. This is critical for applications like cranes and elevators where we need controlled deceleration.

9. Single-Phase Dual Converter

▾
The Problem / The Why

The full converter gave us two quadrants — forward motoring and regenerative braking. But what about reverse? If we need the motor to run in both directions (think of a reversing rolling mill, a positioning table, or a bidirectional winch), we need all four quadrants: forward/reverse and motoring/braking in each direction. A single bridge can't reverse the current direction. So we use two bridges.

The Intuition

Picture two full converters connected "back-to-back" (anti-parallel) across the motor. Converter 1 pushes current in one direction (forward), and Converter 2 pushes current in the opposite direction (reverse). At any moment, only one converter is active. When we want to switch direction, we smoothly hand off from one converter to the other. The key constraint: if one converter has firing angle \(\alpha_1\), the other must have \(\alpha_2 = \pi - \alpha_1\) to ensure both produce the same average voltage magnitude (just with opposite signs), preventing a short circuit between them.

Figure 12: Single-phase dual converter — two full bridges connected anti-parallel to the motor. The constraint α1 + α2 = π prevents circulating currents.

Deep Dive

The dual converter consists of two full converters connected in anti-parallel. The firing angles are complementary:

Complementary Firing Constraint
\[ \alpha_1 + \alpha_2 = \pi \]

This ensures both converters produce the same magnitude of average voltage:

\[ V_{a1} = \frac{2V_m}{\pi}\cos\alpha_1, \quad V_{a2} = \frac{2V_m}{\pi}\cos\alpha_2 = -V_{a1} \]

Four-quadrant operation:

QuadrantVaIaOperationActive Converter
I++Forward motoringConverter 1 (α1 < 90°)
II−+Forward regen. brakingConverter 1 (α1 > 90°)
III−−Reverse motoringConverter 2 (α2 < 90°)
IV+−Reverse regen. brakingConverter 2 (α2 > 90°)

Key properties:

  • Power range: Up to ~15 kW
  • FOUR quadrant operation: Full speed and direction control with regeneration
  • Most expensive single-phase topology (8 thyristors total)
  • Requires careful coordination to prevent circulating current between converters

Figure 13: Dual converter operates in ALL four quadrants — full bidirectional speed and torque control.

Takeaway

The dual converter is the "full package" — four-quadrant operation means we can run the motor in any direction and brake regeneratively from any direction. The price is complexity and cost (two full bridges). For applications demanding full reversing capability up to 15 kW on a single-phase supply, this is the go-to topology.

10. Converter Comparison Summary

▾
Deep Dive — Complete Comparison
Topology Va Formula Quadrants Power Devices
Half-Wave \(\frac{V_m}{2\pi}(1+\cos\alpha)\) I only ≤ 0.5 kW 1 SCR + 1 Diode

The pattern: As we add more thyristors, we gain more quadrants of operation. Each step up in capability comes with more cost and complexity. The choice depends on what the application demands.

Takeaway

Think of it as a progression: half-wave (simplest, one quadrant) → semi-converter (two devices more, same one quadrant but higher power and better waveform) → full converter (all thyristors, two quadrants with regen braking) → dual converter (two bridges, all four quadrants). Each step trades cost for capability.

11. Worked Example

▾
Worked Example

Problem: A separately excited DC motor rated at 230 V drives a constant load torque below full load. It draws 100 A at 500 rpm. The armature resistance is \(R_a = 0.1\;\Omega\). Find:

  1. The back-EMF at this operating point
  2. The motor constant \(K\phi\)
  3. The electromagnetic torque

Step 1: Find the back-EMF

From the armature voltage equation:

\[ V_a = E_g + I_a R_a \] \[ 230 = E_g + 100 \times 0.1 \] \[ E_g = 230 - 10 = 220 \text{ V} \]

Step 2: Find \(K\phi\)

Convert speed to rad/s: \(\omega_m = \frac{2\pi \times 500}{60} = 52.36\) rad/s

From the back-EMF equation:

\[ E_g = K\phi \cdot \omega_m \] \[ 220 = K\phi \times 52.36 \] \[ K\phi = \frac{220}{52.36} = 4.2 \text{ V·s/rad} \]

Step 3: Find the torque

\[ T = K\phi \cdot I_a = 4.2 \times 100 = 420 \text{ N·m} \]
Note

Notice how small the \(I_a R_a\) voltage drop is (only 10 V out of 230 V). This confirms that most of the applied voltage goes into producing back-EMF, and the speed is primarily determined by \(V_a/(K\phi)\) as we discussed. The motor is working well below its full-load capacity since the torque is below rated.

12. Practice Problems (Sheet 3)

▾
Problem 1

A separately excited DC motor runs at 1000 rpm with \(V_a = 200\) V, \(I_a = 100\) A, and \(R_a = 0.1\;\Omega\). The load torque is proportional to speed squared (\(T \propto \omega^2\)). The armature voltage is reduced to 100 V. Find the new steady-state speed and armature current.

Step 1: Find \(K\phi\) from initial conditions

\(\omega_1 = \frac{2\pi \times 1000}{60} = 104.72\) rad/s

Back-EMF: \(E_{g1} = V_a - I_a R_a = 200 - 100(0.1) = 190\) V

\(K\phi = E_{g1}/\omega_1 = 190/104.72 = 1.814\) V·s/rad

Step 2: Find initial torque

\(T_1 = K\phi \cdot I_a = 1.814 \times 100 = 181.4\) N·m

Step 3: Use the load characteristic

Since \(T \propto \omega^2\): \(T_1 = k_L \omega_1^2\)

\(k_L = 181.4 / 104.72^2 = 0.01654\)

At the new speed: \(T_2 = k_L \omega_2^2 = 0.01654\;\omega_2^2\)

Also: \(T_2 = K\phi \cdot I_{a2} = 1.814\;I_{a2}\)

And: \(V_{a2} = E_{g2} + I_{a2} R_a = K\phi\;\omega_2 + I_{a2}(0.1)\)

\(100 = 1.814\;\omega_2 + 0.1\;I_{a2}\)

Step 4: Solve simultaneously

From the torque relationship: \(I_{a2} = \frac{0.01654\;\omega_2^2}{1.814} = 0.009117\;\omega_2^2\)

Substituting into the voltage equation:

\(100 = 1.814\;\omega_2 + 0.1 \times 0.009117\;\omega_2^2\)

\(100 = 1.814\;\omega_2 + 0.0009117\;\omega_2^2\)

Since the \(R_a\) term is very small, we can approximate:

\(\omega_2 \approx 100/1.814 = 55.13\) rad/s \(\approx 526\) rpm

\(I_{a2} = 0.009117 \times 55.13^2 = 27.7\) A

Verification

Check: \(V_a = 1.814 \times 55.13 + 27.7 \times 0.1 = 99.8 + 2.77 = 102.6\) V. Close to 100 V (the approximation introduced a small error). Iterating would refine this, but the answer is approximately 526 rpm and 27.7 A.

Problem 3

A separately excited DC motor runs at 500 rpm with \(V_a = 220\) V, \(I_a = 20\) A, and \(R_a = 1\;\Omega\). The load torque is proportional to speed (\(T \propto \omega\)). Find the resistance to add in series with the armature to reduce speed to 250 rpm.

Step 1: Find \(K\phi\) from initial conditions

\(\omega_1 = 2\pi \times 500/60 = 52.36\) rad/s

\(E_{g1} = 220 - 20(1) = 200\) V

\(K\phi = 200/52.36 = 3.819\) V·s/rad

Step 2: Load relationship

\(T_1 = K\phi \cdot I_a = 3.819 \times 20 = 76.39\) N·m

Since \(T \propto \omega\): \(k_L = T_1/\omega_1 = 76.39/52.36 = 1.459\)

Step 3: New operating point at 250 rpm

\(\omega_2 = 2\pi \times 250/60 = 26.18\) rad/s

\(T_2 = k_L \omega_2 = 1.459 \times 26.18 = 38.19\) N·m

\(I_{a2} = T_2/(K\phi) = 38.19/3.819 = 10\) A

\(E_{g2} = K\phi \cdot \omega_2 = 3.819 \times 26.18 = 100\) V

Step 4: Find the total resistance

\(V_a = E_{g2} + I_{a2}(R_a + R_{\text{ext}})\)

\(220 = 100 + 10(1 + R_{\text{ext}})\)

\(120 = 10 + 10 R_{\text{ext}}\)

\(R_{\text{ext}} = 110/10 = \mathbf{11\;\Omega}\)

Note

Adding 11 Ω in series is a lot — and it wastes \(I_{a2}^2 R_{\text{ext}} = 100 \times 11 = 1100\) W as heat! This illustrates exactly why armature resistance control is inefficient and why power electronic converters (which adjust \(V_a\) directly) are preferred in modern drives.

Problem 4

A DC shunt motor runs at 1000 rpm with \(I_a = 100\) A and a constant load torque. The source voltage is halved. Find the new speed and armature current. Assume \(R_a\) is small enough to neglect.

Key insight for a shunt motor: When we halve the supply voltage, both the armature voltage and the field voltage are halved (since they share the same supply). This means \(\phi\) also drops (though not exactly by half due to the nonlinear B-H curve — but let's assume linear operation for simplicity, so \(\phi \propto V_f \propto V_s\)).

Analysis (neglecting \(R_a\)):

Speed: \(\omega_m \approx V_a/(K\phi)\)

If both \(V_a\) and \(\phi\) are halved: \(\omega_2 = (V_a/2)/(K \cdot \phi/2) = V_a/(K\phi) = \omega_1\)

So the speed stays approximately the same: \(\omega_2 \approx 1000\) rpm

For the current: Since torque is constant and \(T = K\phi \cdot I_a\):

\(T_1 = K\phi \cdot 100\)

\(T_2 = K(\phi/2) \cdot I_{a2} = T_1\)

\(I_{a2} = \frac{K\phi \cdot 100}{K \cdot \phi/2} = 200\) A

New armature current: 200 A (doubles!)

Warning

This is a dangerous situation! The current doubled because the flux halved — the motor needs more current to maintain the same torque with weaker flux. If the motor was already running near rated current, halving the voltage on a shunt motor with constant torque load could cause overcurrent. This is why shunt motors need careful protection and why separately excited motors (where we control \(V_a\) and \(\phi\) independently) are preferred for drives.

Big Picture

In this lecture, we built up the complete picture of DC drives from the ground up:

  1. DC motor fundamentals: Three simple equations (\(V_a = E_g + I_aR_a\), \(E_g = K\phi\omega_m\), \(T = K\phi I_a\)) govern everything.
  2. Starting problem: Never apply full voltage to a stopped motor — current will be dangerously high.
  3. Speed control: Two methods — armature voltage (constant torque, below base speed) and field weakening (constant power, above base speed).
  4. Single-phase converters: A family of topologies offering progressively more capability:
    • Half-wave: simplest, one quadrant, small loads
    • Semi-converter: one quadrant, medium power, better waveform
    • Full converter: two quadrants with regeneration
    • Dual converter: four quadrants, full reversing capability

Next lecture, we'll extend these ideas to three-phase converters, which handle much higher power levels and form the backbone of industrial DC drive systems.
