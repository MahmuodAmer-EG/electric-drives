0 Introduction & Overview

💡 The Problem — Why do we care about "dynamics"? ▼

Imagine you're designing an elevator system. The motor is rated at 10 kW and can spin at 1500 rpm. Great. But here's the question that keeps engineers awake at night: how long will it take to accelerate a fully loaded cabin from standstill to full speed?

If it takes 2 seconds, passengers feel comfortable. If it takes 15 seconds, they complain. If the motor can't accelerate the load at all... well, nobody goes anywhere.

The steady-state torque-speed characteristic (which we studied in Lecture 1) tells us where the motor will eventually settle. But it says nothing about how we get there. That's the realm of dynamics.

🧠 The Big Picture ▼

In this lecture, we'll build up a single, powerful equation — Newton's second law, but for rotating things — and then learn how to use it for real motor-load systems. Here's the roadmap:

  1. The Fundamental Torque Equation — the "F = ma" of electrical drives
  2. Equivalent Parameters — how to "refer" all moving parts back to the motor shaft
  3. Load Torque Components — friction, windage, and the actual useful work
  4. Measuring Inertia — a practical lab technique
  5. Worked Problems — real calculations from Sheet 01

Let's dive in.

2.1 Fundamental Torque Equation

💡 The Problem — What makes a motor speed up or slow down? ▼

Think about pushing a heavy shopping cart. If you push harder than friction resists, the cart accelerates. If you push exactly as hard as friction, it moves at constant speed. If you stop pushing, friction slows it down.

What would happen if we just connected a motor to a heavy load and turned it on? Would the motor instantly reach full speed? Would it stall? Something in between? The answer depends on the balance between what the motor provides (torque) and what the load demands (load torque + the torque needed to accelerate everything).

🧠 The Intuition — Newton's Second Law, but Rotational ▼

We all know F = ma for linear motion. There's a direct rotational analog:

Linear (Translational) Rotational
Force \(F\)Torque \(T\)
Mass \(m\)Moment of Inertia \(J\)
Velocity \(v\)Angular velocity \(\omega\)
Acceleration \(a = dv/dt\)Angular acceleration \(\alpha = d\omega/dt\)
\(F_{\text{net}} = m \cdot a\)\(T_{\text{net}} = J \cdot \alpha\)

The "net torque" on the motor shaft is the difference between what the motor produces and what the load consumes. That leftover torque (positive or negative) is what causes acceleration or deceleration. Simple as that.

📚 The Deep Dive — Deriving the Fundamental Equation ▼

Consider a motor connected to a mechanical load through a shaft. We define:

  • \(T\) = motor developed torque (N·m)
  • \(T_L\) = equivalent load torque, referred to the motor shaft (N·m)
  • \(J\) = equivalent moment of inertia of the entire motor-load system, referred to the motor shaft (kg·m²)
  • \(\omega_m\) = motor angular speed (rad/s)
Figure 2.1 — Motor-load system connected by a shaft. The motor produces torque T; the load resists with torque TL.

Applying Newton's second law for rotation about the motor shaft:

Fundamental Equation of Motion
\[ T - T_L = J \frac{d\omega_m}{dt} \]

The term \(J \frac{d\omega_m}{dt}\) is called the dynamic torque (or inertia torque). It represents the torque required to change the kinetic energy stored in the rotating masses.

ⓘ
Key insight: This equation is the single most important equation in electrical drives. Every control strategy, every sizing calculation, every dynamic analysis starts here.

Three Operating Conditions

The fundamental equation leads us directly to three distinct situations:

▲
Acceleration
\(T > T_L\)
\(\frac{d\omega}{dt} > 0\)
Motor produces excess torque. Kinetic energy is stored in rotating masses. Speed increases.
▶
Steady State
\(T = T_L\)
\(\frac{d\omega}{dt} = 0\)
Torque balance. No change in kinetic energy. Speed is constant.
▼
Deceleration
\(T < T_L\)
\(\frac{d\omega}{dt} < 0\)
Load exceeds motor torque. Kinetic energy is discharged. Speed decreases.
🎯 Takeaway ▼

The fundamental equation \(T - T_L = J \frac{d\omega_m}{dt}\) is the "master equation" of drive dynamics. The entire rest of this lecture is about understanding each term in this equation and learning how to calculate them for real systems.

Next, we tackle the tricky part: what happens when the motor and load aren't directly connected, but go through gears, belts, or other mechanisms?

2.2 Equivalent Values of Drive Parameters

💡 The Problem — What if the motor and load spin at different speeds? ▼

In the real world, motors are almost never bolted directly to the load. A washing machine motor spins at 1450 rpm but the drum only spins at 300 rpm — there's a belt in between. An industrial conveyor uses a gearbox. A crane's motor rotates, but the payload translates (moves in a straight line).

How do we write \(T - T_L = J \frac{d\omega}{dt}\) when different parts of the system are moving at different speeds? We need a way to "translate" everything to one common reference — the motor shaft.

🧠 The Intuition — The Currency Exchange Analogy ▼

Think of it like currency exchange. If you have dollars, euros, and yen, you can't just add them up. You need to convert everything to one currency first. The "exchange rate" in our case is the gear ratio or speed ratio.

The trick is simple: energy is our universal currency. We use the principle of energy conservation:

  • For inertia (\(J\)): total kinetic energy must be preserved
  • For torque (\(T_L\)): total power must be preserved

By matching energies and powers, we can collapse any complex multi-speed system into a single equivalent motor-load model.

The three common mechanisms we encounter are:

  1. Direct coupling — motor and load share the same shaft and speed (trivial case)
  2. Gear box — speed ratio between driving and driven shafts
  3. Belt drive / pulley system — similar concept with pulleys

2.2.1 Load with Rotational Motion

Coupling with Gear Box

Figure 2.2 — Motor-load coupling through a gear box. The driving gear (N teeth) meshes with the driven gear (N1 teeth).
📚 Deep Dive — Gear Ratio and Its Meaning ▼

The gear ratio \(a\) is defined as:

\[ a = \frac{\omega_{m1}}{\omega_m} = \frac{N}{N_1} \]

Where:

  • \(\omega_m\) = speed of the driving gear (motor side)
  • \(\omega_{m1}\) = speed of the driven gear (load side)
  • \(N\) = number of teeth on the driving gear
  • \(N_1\) = number of teeth on the driven gear
📚
Convention note: If \(a < 1\), the load spins slower than the motor (speed reduction, torque multiplication — the common case). If \(a > 1\), the load spins faster (speed increase, torque reduction).

Belt Drive / Pulley System

Figure 2.3 — Belt drive coupling. The speed ratio depends on the pulley radii.

The belt drive works on the same principle as gears: the linear velocity at the belt-contact surface must be the same for both pulleys (assuming no slippage). So \(r \cdot \omega_m = r_1 \cdot \omega_{m1}\), giving us the same type of speed ratio \(a\).

Calculating Equivalent Moment of Inertia \(J\)

📚 Deep Dive — Equivalent J from Energy Conservation ▼

The principle is beautifully simple: the kinetic energy of the equivalent system must equal the total kinetic energy of all moving parts.

Energy Conservation Principle
\[ \frac{1}{2} J \omega_m^2 = \frac{1}{2} J_m \omega_m^2 + \frac{1}{2} J_1 \omega_{m1}^2 + \frac{1}{2} J_2 \omega_{m2}^2 + \cdots \]

Since \(\omega_{m1} = a_1 \cdot \omega_m\), \(\omega_{m2} = a_2 \cdot \omega_m\), etc., we can factor out \(\frac{1}{2}\omega_m^2\):

Equivalent Moment of Inertia (Rotational Loads)
\[ J = J_m + J_1 \cdot a_1^2 + J_2 \cdot a_2^2 + \cdots \]

where \(a_i = \omega_{mi} / \omega_m\) is the speed ratio of the \(i\)-th component relative to the motor.

⚠
Watch the squared term! Even a small inertia \(J_1\) can have a large equivalent effect if the speed ratio \(a\) is large. Conversely, a large inertia behind a speed-reduction gear (\(a < 1\)) contributes much less than you might expect.

Calculating Equivalent Load Torque \(T_L\)

📚 Deep Dive — Equivalent T_L from Power Conservation ▼

For torque, we use power conservation instead of energy conservation. The total mechanical power consumed by all loads must equal the power of the equivalent load torque:

Power Balance
\[ T_L \cdot \omega_m = T_{L0} \cdot \omega_m + \frac{T_{L1} \cdot \omega_{m1}}{\eta_1} + \frac{T_{L2} \cdot \omega_{m2}}{\eta_2} + \cdots \]

Where \(\eta_i\) is the efficiency of the \(i\)-th transmission mechanism. Dividing both sides by \(\omega_m\):

Equivalent Load Torque (Rotational Loads)
\[ T_L = T_{L0} + \frac{a_1 \cdot T_{L1}}{\eta_1} + \frac{a_2 \cdot T_{L2}}{\eta_2} + \cdots \]
ⓘ
Why divide by efficiency? Because the motor must supply extra torque to overcome transmission losses. If the gearbox is 90% efficient, the motor "sees" the load torque as being 1/0.9 = 1.11 times larger than it actually is.

2.2.2 Load with Translational Motion

💡 The Problem — What about loads that don't rotate? ▼

A crane lifts a weight vertically. A conveyor moves boxes horizontally. A vehicle moves along a road. These loads undergo translational (linear) motion, not rotation. But the motor shaft rotates. How do we convert a mass moving in a straight line into an equivalent inertia on a rotating shaft?

🧠 The Intuition — Rotational-Translational Dictionary ▼

Every rotational quantity has a translational twin:

Rotational Translational Relationship
Torque \(T\) (N·m)Force \(F\) (N)\(T = F \cdot r\)
Angular velocity \(\omega\) (rad/s)Linear velocity \(V\) (m/s)\(V = \omega \cdot r\)
Moment of inertia \(J\) (kg·m²)Mass \(M\) (kg)\(J_{\text{eq}} = M \cdot r^2\)
Kinetic energy \(\frac{1}{2}J\omega^2\)Kinetic energy \(\frac{1}{2}MV^2\)Must be equal
📚 Deep Dive — Equivalent J and T_L for Translational Loads ▼

Equivalent Moment of Inertia:

Again, we equate kinetic energies:

\[ \frac{1}{2} J \omega_m^2 = \frac{1}{2} J_m \omega_m^2 + \frac{1}{2} M V^2 \]

Since \(V = \omega_m \cdot r\) (where \(r\) is the effective radius or the conversion factor between rotation and translation):

Equivalent J (Translational Load)
\[ J = J_m + M \left(\frac{V}{\omega_m}\right)^2 \]

Equivalent Load Torque:

From power balance: \(T_L \cdot \omega_m = F \cdot V\), so:

Equivalent T_L (Translational Load)
\[ T_L = F \cdot \frac{V}{\omega_m} \]
🎯 Takeaway — The Referral Recipe ▼

No matter how complex the system — multiple gears, belts, and translating masses — the recipe is always the same:

  1. For equivalent \(J\): Equate kinetic energies. Speed ratios appear squared.
  2. For equivalent \(T_L\): Equate powers. Speed ratios appear linearly. Don't forget efficiency.
  3. Everything gets referred back to the motor shaft.

2.4 Components of Load Torque

💡 The Problem — Where does the load torque actually come from? ▼

We've been writing \(T_L\) as if it's one simple number. But in reality, the "load torque" is a composite of several different physical phenomena. A lathe cutting metal faces different resistances than a fan spinning in air. Understanding the components of \(T_L\) is critical for proper motor sizing and control design.

What if your motor can handle the useful load but you forgot about friction and windage? It stalls. Let's break it all down.

🧠 The Intuition — The Three Tax Collectors ▼

Think of the motor torque as your salary. Before you get to spend it (useful work), three "tax collectors" take their share:

  1. Friction — like a flat tax. Always there, somewhat constant (with a startup surcharge).
  2. Windage — like a progressive tax. The faster you go, the more it takes (proportional to speed squared).
  3. Useful load \(T_{L}\) — the actual work you want done (cutting, lifting, pumping, etc.).
📚 Deep Dive — Friction, Windage, and Useful Load ▼

The total load torque is:

Load Torque Decomposition
\[ T_L = T_F + T_W + T_{L,\text{useful}} \]

Let's examine each component:

1. Friction Torque \(T_F\)

Friction itself has three sub-components:

  • Static friction \(T_s\) — the "breakaway" torque needed to start motion from rest. This is the highest friction component at zero speed.
  • Coulomb friction \(T_c\) — constant friction independent of speed (once moving). Like a flat fee.
  • Viscous friction \(T_v = B\omega\) — proportional to speed. \(B\) is the viscous friction coefficient (N·m·s/rad).

2. Windage Torque \(T_W\)

Air resistance on the rotating parts. Proportional to the square of speed:

\[ T_W = c \cdot \omega^2 \]

where \(c\) is the windage coefficient. This is significant at high speeds (think of fans and centrifugal pumps).

3. Useful Load Torque \(T_{L,\text{useful}}\)

The actual work: lifting weights against gravity, cutting metal, pumping fluid, etc. This depends entirely on the application.

Figure 2.4 — Components of load torque vs. speed. Notice how Ts (static friction) exists only at zero speed, while windage grows quadratically.

Complete Equation of Motion

📚 Deep Dive — The Full Picture ▼

Incorporating viscous friction explicitly into the fundamental equation (since it's always present and speed-dependent), we get the complete equation of motion:

Complete Fundamental Equation of Motion
\[ T = T_L + J\frac{d\omega}{dt} + B\omega \]

Where:

  • \(T\) = motor developed torque
  • \(T_L\) = load torque (useful work + Coulomb friction + windage)
  • \(J \frac{d\omega}{dt}\) = dynamic (inertia) torque
  • \(B\omega\) = viscous friction torque
✎
In many textbook problems, \(B\) is either given explicitly or folded into the load torque expression. Always check what's included in "\(T_L\)" in any given problem statement.
🎯 Takeaway ▼

The motor must overcome friction, windage, the useful load, AND provide extra torque for acceleration. Forgetting any of these "tax collectors" leads to undersizing the motor. The complete equation \(T = T_L + J\frac{d\omega}{dt} + B\omega\) is your go-to formula for all dynamic analysis.

Δt Calculating Dynamic Time

💡 The Problem — "How long does it take to reach operating speed?" ▼

This is perhaps the most practical question in drive dynamics. When an engineer sizes a motor for a rolling mill, elevator, or centrifuge, the first question from the customer is: "How quickly can it get up to speed?" Too slow means lost productivity. Too fast means mechanical stress or passenger discomfort.

📚 Deep Dive — Deriving the Time Formula ▼

Starting from the fundamental equation:

\[ T - T_L = J \frac{d\omega}{dt} \]

Rearranging for \(dt\):

\[ dt = \frac{J \, d\omega}{T - T_L} \]

Integrating from initial speed \(\omega_1\) to final speed \(\omega_2\):

Dynamic Time Calculation
\[ t = J \int_{\omega_1}^{\omega_2} \frac{d\omega}{T(\omega) - T_L(\omega)} \]

The challenge: Both \(T\) and \(T_L\) often depend on \(\omega\), making this integral potentially complex. There are three cases:

  1. Constant \(T - T_L\): The integral is trivial → \(t = \frac{J \cdot \Delta\omega}{T - T_L}\)
  2. Linear dependence on \(\omega\): Leads to an exponential (first-order system) response
  3. Complex dependence: May require numerical or graphical integration

2.3 Measurement of Moment of Inertia

💡 The Problem — We need J, but how do we actually measure it? ▼

For simple shapes (cylinders, discs), we can calculate \(J\) from formulas. But a real motor-load system has complex geometry — armature windings, fan blades, couplings, irregular-shaped loads. How do we experimentally determine \(J\) for a real machine?

The Retardation Test

🧠 The Intuition — Coast to a Stop and Watch ▼

Imagine you're riding a bicycle and you stop pedaling. How quickly you slow down depends on two things: your total inertia (you + bike) and the friction/air resistance. If we know the friction and measure how fast you decelerate, we can work backwards to find the inertia.

That's exactly what the retardation test does for a motor.

📚 Deep Dive — Retardation Test Procedure ▼

The test involves two parts:

Test 1: Record the Deceleration Curve

  1. Run the motor slightly above rated speed (no mechanical load connected).
  2. Suddenly disconnect the electrical supply.
  3. Record the speed vs. time curve (\(\omega\) vs. \(t\)) using a storage oscilloscope or digital recorder.

Test 2: Measure Rotational Losses

  1. Run the motor at rated speed at no load.
  2. Measure the input power \(P_{\text{rot}}\). Since there's no mechanical load, all input power goes to rotational losses (friction + windage).
Figure 2.5 — Retardation test: speed vs. time after supply disconnection. The slope at rated speed gives dω/dt.

Calculating J

After supply disconnection, the only torque acting is the rotational losses (friction + windage). So:

\[ -T_{\text{losses}} = J \frac{d\omega}{dt} \]

At rated speed, the power of rotational losses is:

\[ P_{\text{rot}} = T_{\text{losses}} \cdot \omega_r \]

Therefore:

Moment of Inertia from Retardation Test
\[ J = \frac{P_{\text{rot}}}{\omega_r \cdot \left| \frac{d\omega}{dt} \right|_{\omega = \omega_r}} \]

Where \(\left| \frac{d\omega}{dt} \right|_{\omega = \omega_r}\) is obtained from the slope of the tangent to the deceleration curve at rated speed.

🎯 Takeaway ▼

The retardation test is elegant: let the system coast, measure how fast it slows, and combine with known losses to extract \(J\). It requires only basic instruments (speed sensor + oscilloscope + power meter) and works for any motor-load system regardless of geometric complexity.

Ex Worked Examples (Sheet 01)

Let's put everything we've learned into practice with three problems from Sheet 01.

📝 Problem 3 — Motor Driving Four Loads (Rotational + Translational) ▼

Problem Statement:

A motor running at \(\omega_m = 100\) rad/s drives four loads:

  • Load 1 (rotational): \(J_1 = 5\) kg·m², connected through a gearbox with gear ratio \(a_1 = 0.5\), \(T_{L1} = 10\) N·m, efficiency \(\eta_1 = 0.9\)
  • Load 2 (rotational): \(J_2 = 2\) kg·m², connected through a gearbox with gear ratio \(a_2 = 2\), \(T_{L2} = 8\) N·m, efficiency \(\eta_2 = 0.85\)
  • Load 3 (translational): Mass \(M_3 = 500\) kg, linear velocity \(V_3 = 5\) m/s, force \(F_3 = 200\) N, efficiency \(\eta_3 = 0.95\)
  • Load 4 (translational): Mass \(M_4 = 1000\) kg, linear velocity \(V_4 = 2\) m/s, force \(F_4 = 500\) N, efficiency \(\eta_4 = 0.88\)

Motor inertia: \(J_m = 0.5\) kg·m²

Find: Equivalent \(J\) and equivalent \(T_L\) referred to the motor shaft.

Solution:

Step 1: Equivalent J

Using energy conservation, the equivalent moment of inertia is:

\[ J = J_m + J_1 a_1^2 + J_2 a_2^2 + M_3 \left(\frac{V_3}{\omega_m}\right)^2 + M_4 \left(\frac{V_4}{\omega_m}\right)^2 \]

Substituting values:

\[ J = 0.5 + 5(0.5)^2 + 2(2)^2 + 500\left(\frac{5}{100}\right)^2 + 1000\left(\frac{2}{100}\right)^2 \] \[ J = 0.5 + 1.25 + 8 + 1.25 + 0.4 \] \[ \boxed{J = 11.4 \text{ kg·m}^2} \]
Step 2: Equivalent T_L

Using power conservation:

\[ T_L = \frac{a_1 T_{L1}}{\eta_1} + \frac{a_2 T_{L2}}{\eta_2} + \frac{F_3 V_3}{\omega_m \cdot \eta_3} + \frac{F_4 V_4}{\omega_m \cdot \eta_4} \]

Substituting:

\[ T_L = \frac{0.5 \times 10}{0.9} + \frac{2 \times 8}{0.85} + \frac{200 \times 5}{100 \times 0.95} + \frac{500 \times 2}{100 \times 0.88} \] \[ T_L = 5.56 + 18.82 + 10.53 + 11.36 \] \[ \boxed{T_L = 46.27 \text{ N·m}} \]
💡
Notice: Load 2, despite having a small \(J_2 = 2\) kg·m², contributes the most to the equivalent inertia (8 kg·m²) because its gear ratio \(a_2 = 2\) gets squared. This is a common "gotcha" in drive design!
📝 Problem 4 — Weight Lifting by Winch ▼

Problem Statement:

A winch driven by a motor lifts a weight of \(M = 800\) kg at a velocity of \(V = 1.5\) m/s. The motor speed is \(N_m = 1440\) rpm. The motor inertia is \(J_m = 1.2\) kg·m². The drum (winch) inertia is \(J_d = 3\) kg·m² and operates at 240 rpm. Gearbox efficiency is \(\eta = 0.85\).

Find: (a) Equivalent \(J\), (b) Motor torque required during steady-state lifting.

Solution:

Step 1: Convert motor speed to rad/s
\[ \omega_m = \frac{2\pi N_m}{60} = \frac{2\pi \times 1440}{60} = 150.8 \text{ rad/s} \]

Drum speed:

\[ \omega_d = \frac{2\pi \times 240}{60} = 25.13 \text{ rad/s} \]

Gear ratio:

\[ a = \frac{\omega_d}{\omega_m} = \frac{25.13}{150.8} = \frac{1}{6} \]
Step 2: Equivalent J

The system has a rotating drum and a translating weight:

\[ J = J_m + J_d \cdot a^2 + M \left(\frac{V}{\omega_m}\right)^2 \] \[ J = 1.2 + 3 \times \left(\frac{1}{6}\right)^2 + 800 \times \left(\frac{1.5}{150.8}\right)^2 \] \[ J = 1.2 + 0.083 + 0.079 \] \[ \boxed{J = 1.362 \text{ kg·m}^2} \]
Step 3: Motor torque during steady-state lifting

At steady state, \(\frac{d\omega}{dt} = 0\), so the motor only needs to overcome the load torque. The load force is the weight: \(F = Mg = 800 \times 9.81 = 7848\) N.

\[ T_L = \frac{F \cdot V}{\omega_m \cdot \eta} = \frac{7848 \times 1.5}{150.8 \times 0.85} \] \[ \boxed{T_L = 91.8 \text{ N·m}} \]
ⓘ
Observation: The equivalent inertia is dominated by the motor itself (1.2 kg·m²). The drum and load contribute very little because the gear ratio \(a = 1/6\) gets squared, dramatically reducing their reflected inertia. This is one of the key benefits of gearing.
📝 Problem 11 — Calculate Starting Time ▼

Problem Statement:

A motor-load system has: \(J = 10\) kg·m², motor torque \(T = 15 + 0.5\omega\) N·m, load torque \(T_L = 5 + 0.6\omega\) N·m. Calculate the time to accelerate from standstill (\(\omega = 0\)) to a speed of \(\omega = 80\) rad/s.

Solution:

Step 1: Find the net accelerating torque
\[ T - T_L = (15 + 0.5\omega) - (5 + 0.6\omega) = 10 - 0.1\omega \]
Step 2: Apply the dynamic time formula
\[ t = J \int_0^{80} \frac{d\omega}{T - T_L} = 10 \int_0^{80} \frac{d\omega}{10 - 0.1\omega} \]
Step 3: Evaluate the integral

Let \(u = 10 - 0.1\omega\), then \(du = -0.1 \, d\omega\), so \(d\omega = -10 \, du\).

When \(\omega = 0\): \(u = 10\). When \(\omega = 80\): \(u = 10 - 8 = 2\).

\[ t = 10 \int_{10}^{2} \frac{-10 \, du}{u} = 100 \int_{2}^{10} \frac{du}{u} = 100 \left[\ln u\right]_2^{10} \] \[ t = 100 \left(\ln 10 - \ln 2\right) = 100 \ln 5 \] \[ \boxed{t = 100 \times 1.609 = 160.9 \text{ seconds}} \]
Step 4: Check — What about steady-state speed?

At steady state, \(T - T_L = 0\), which gives:

\[ 10 - 0.1\omega_{ss} = 0 \implies \omega_{ss} = 100 \text{ rad/s} \]

We're accelerating to 80 rad/s, which is 80% of the steady-state speed. The net torque at \(\omega = 80\) is only \(10 - 8 = 2\) N·m, so acceleration is very slow near the target speed. This explains why the time is relatively long.

⚠
Key insight from this problem: When the net torque \(T - T_L\) decreases with speed (as it often does), the last portion of the acceleration takes disproportionately long. In theory, reaching \(\omega_{ss}\) takes infinite time (like an exponential approach). In practice, we consider the motor "at speed" when it reaches ~95% of \(\omega_{ss}\).

Σ Lecture Summary

🎯 Summary — What We Learned Today ▼
  1. The Fundamental Equation: \(T - T_L = J \frac{d\omega_m}{dt}\). This single equation governs all transient behavior in electrical drives.
  2. Three Conditions: Acceleration (\(T > T_L\)), steady state (\(T = T_L\)), and deceleration (\(T < T_L\)).
  3. Equivalent Parameters: Use energy conservation for \(J\) (speed ratios squared) and power conservation for \(T_L\) (speed ratios linear, include efficiency).
  4. Load Torque Components: \(T_L = T_F + T_W + T_{L,\text{useful}}\) with friction (static + Coulomb + viscous) and windage (\(\propto \omega^2\)).
  5. Complete Equation: \(T = T_L + J\frac{d\omega}{dt} + B\omega\).
  6. Dynamic Time: \(t = J \int \frac{d\omega}{T - T_L}\) — can be evaluated analytically for simple cases.
  7. Measuring J: The retardation test uses coast-down data and loss measurements to experimentally determine \(J\).
