Electric Traction Drives

From diesel locomotives to subway trains — how electric motors move the world's heaviest vehicles

Electrical Engineering • Traction Systems • Drive Design

1. What Are Traction Drives?

▼
🟡 The Problem

Imagine you need to accelerate a 600-tonne passenger train from standstill to 150 km/h in under a minute, then hold that speed across varying terrain, then smoothly stop at the next station — all while keeping thousands of passengers comfortable. This is not your textbook DC motor spinning a small fan. This is traction: the most demanding application of electric drives.

What makes it so hard? One word: inertia. The sheer mass of a train (or an electric bus, or a metro car) means enormous forces during acceleration and braking. Get the motor sizing wrong and you either can't reach speed in time, or you burn out the motor trying.

🔵 The Intuition

A "traction load" simply means: the thing being driven is a vehicle, and the motor is electric. That's it. The vehicle could be a train, a tram, a trolley bus, a subway car, or an electric car. What they all share is:

  • Very high inertia — the mass is huge, so starting and stopping requires serious torque.
  • Friction-type load — the resistive force comes from friction (wheels on rails or tires on road), air drag (windage), and gravity (hills).
  • Variable operating cycle — the motor doesn't just spin at one speed; it accelerates, cruises, and brakes repeatedly.
🟢 Deep Dive — Nature of Traction Loads

Let's break down what happens to friction as the vehicle moves:

  • At standstill: There's a static friction component — you need a "break-away" torque just to get rolling. Think of trying to push a heavy cart from rest.
  • As speed increases: Static friction decreases (the wheels are now rolling, not sliding), but viscous friction (proportional to speed) and windage (proportional to speed squared) start growing.

So the total resistance looks something like:

General Friction Model \[ F_{\text{resistance}} = F_{\text{static}} + b \cdot v + c \cdot v^2 \] where \(b\) = viscous friction coefficient, \(c\) = aerodynamic drag coefficient

At low speeds, static friction dominates. At high speeds, windage (\(v^2\) term) dominates. This shapes everything about how we design the motor and the drive cycle.

🚂

Trains & Locomotives

Main line trains: up to 6000 HP using 6 motors of ~1000 HP each, mounted on the locomotive's axles.

🚇

Metro & Subway

Metro (above ground) and subway (underground). Motors often distributed across multiple cars, not just the front.

🚋

Trams & Trolley Buses

Grid-powered via overhead wires. Lighter vehicles, frequent stops, urban operation with tight curves.

🚗

Electric Vehicles

Battery-powered traction. Same physics, smaller scale. High torque at standstill is a natural advantage of electric motors.

📝 Takeaway

Traction = electric motor + vehicle. The defining challenge is high inertia requiring enormous accelerating/braking forces. The load is friction-type, changing character from static-dominated at standstill to windage-dominated at high speed.

2. Types of Electric Traction

▼
🟡 The Problem

Not every rail route has overhead power lines. Long-haul freight routes through remote areas can't justify the cost of electrifying thousands of kilometres of track. So how do you still get the benefits of electric motor drive (smooth torque, regeneration, controllability) without a grid connection?

Answer: you carry your own generator on board. That's the diesel-electric approach. Meanwhile, city transit systems (trams, metro, subway) do have grid power available, so they use it directly. Let's map the whole landscape.

Figure 2.1 — Classification of electric traction drives by power source
🔵 The Intuition

Think of it this way: diesel-electric is like carrying a portable charger for your phone. You have a diesel engine on board that spins a generator, which feeds the electric motors. The diesel engine never directly turns the wheels — electricity is always the intermediary.

Grid-powered traction is like plugging your phone into the wall. The power comes from the electrical grid via overhead wires (catenary) or a third rail. This is simpler, more efficient, and cheaper to operate — but requires expensive infrastructure.

🟢 Deep Dive — Diesel-Electric Variants

All three diesel-electric options start with: Diesel Engine → Generator → Motor. The difference is in the generator/motor pairing:

VariantGeneratorIntermediateMotorNotes
A Separately-excited DC — DC Series Classic approach, simple control
B Synchronous (AC) Rectifier DC Series AC generator is more robust
C Synchronous (AC) Inverter Induction (AC) Modern approach, best efficiency

Why is the DC series motor so popular in traction? Because its natural torque-speed characteristic is perfect: high torque at low speed (exactly what you need to get a heavy train moving), and the torque drops as speed increases (which is what you want for constant-power cruising).

Modern systems (Option C) use induction motors with variable-frequency inverters, which give even better efficiency and need less maintenance (no brushes or commutator).

Bogie System & Motor Placement

🔵 The Intuition

A bogie (or "truck" in American English) is the wheeled frame underneath a rail car. Each bogie typically has 2 or 3 axles, and each axle can have a motor. The motors are mounted directly on the bogie frame, connected to the wheel axles through a gearbox.

Figure 2.2 — Typical main-line locomotive: 2 bogies, 3 axles each, 6 motors total (~1000 HP per motor)
ⓘ
Main-line trains vs. Subway trains In a main-line train, all motors are in the locomotive (first car). In a subway/metro, motors are often distributed across multiple cars — several cars are "motorized" and others are just passenger trailers. This gives better adhesion (more driving wheels) and distributes the weight.
📝 Takeaway

Two families: Diesel-electric (self-contained power) and grid-powered (external supply). The DC series motor dominated historically due to its ideal traction torque curve, but modern systems increasingly use AC induction motors with inverters. Motors are mounted on bogies, connected to wheel axles through gearboxes.

3. Duty Cycle of the Traction Drive

▼
🟡 The Problem

A traction motor doesn't just "run." Between two stations, it goes through a violent acceleration, a calm cruise, and then a forceful braking — then sits at rest while passengers get on and off — and then does it all again. How do we describe this repeating pattern? And how does it affect motor sizing?

If we design the motor only for cruising, it will overheat during acceleration. If we design it only for acceleration, it'll be oversized and wasteful during cruising. We need to understand the full duty cycle.

🔵 The Intuition

Think of a city bus journey between two bus stops:

  1. Gun it — accelerate hard from rest to cruising speed. The motor works at maximum torque.
  2. Cruise — hold a steady speed. The motor only needs to overcome friction and wind resistance.
  3. Brake — slow down and stop at the next station. The motor may help (regenerative braking) or mechanical brakes take over.
  4. Wait — doors open, passengers move, motor is off.

This four-phase pattern repeats for every station-to-station segment. We call it the duty cycle, and we can draw it as a speed-vs-time diagram.

Figure 3.1 — Trapezoidal speed-time diagram. The shaded area under the curve equals the distance D between stations.
🟢 Deep Dive — Duty Cycle Phases

Let's describe each phase precisely:

PhaseDurationSpeedMotor TorqueMotor Power
Acceleration \(t_1\) \(0 \to V_m\) \(T = T_{\max}\) (constant) \(P\) increases linearly with \(\omega\)
Free running \(t_2\) \(V_m\) (constant) \(T_{\text{motor}} = T_{\text{load}}\) \(P =\) constant at rated
Braking \(t_3\) \(V_m \to 0\) Negative (regenerative) or zero (mech. brakes) Zero or negative
Stop \(t_{\text{stop}}\) 0 0 0

Key relationships:

Speed-Time Geometry \[ V_m = \alpha \cdot t_1 = \beta \cdot t_3 \] \[ D = \tfrac{1}{2} V_m \, t_1 + V_m \, t_2 + \tfrac{1}{2} V_m \, t_3 \] \[ V_{\text{av}} = \frac{D}{T} \quad \text{where } T = t_1 + t_2 + t_3 + t_{\text{stop}} \]

Here \(\alpha\) is the acceleration rate (km/h/s) and \(\beta\) is the deceleration rate (km/h/s).

3.1 Ideal Traction Drive Characteristics

🟡 The Problem

We want maximum acceleration from standstill. But the motor has a power limit. How do we get the most out of it across the entire speed range? This leads us to the "ideal" torque-speed and power-speed curves for traction.

🔵 The Intuition

At low speed, the motor can push with maximum torque (the current limit). As speed increases, the back-EMF rises and eventually the motor hits its voltage limit — so it can't maintain that torque anymore. From that point on, it runs at constant power (torque falls as \(1/\omega\)).

So the ideal traction drive has two distinct regions:

  1. Constant Torque (low speed): \(T = T_{\max},\; P = T_{\max} \cdot \omega\) (power rises linearly)
  2. Constant Power (high speed): \(P = P_{\max},\; T = P_{\max}/\omega\) (torque falls hyperbolically)

The transition speed is the base speed \(\omega_b\) (or rated speed), where the motor first reaches its rated power.

Figure 3.2 — Ideal traction drive: constant torque up to base speed, then constant power
🟢 Deep Dive
Constant Torque Region (ω < ωb) \[ T = T_{\max} = \text{const}, \quad P = T_{\max} \cdot \omega \]
Constant Power Region (ω > ωb) \[ P = P_{\max} = \text{const}, \quad T = \frac{P_{\max}}{\omega} \]

Why is this "ideal"? Because it extracts the maximum possible acceleration at every speed. At low speed, you're limited by motor current (torque). At high speed, you're limited by motor voltage (power). The ideal drive hits both limits simultaneously at the base speed.

3.2 Coasting — The Energy Saver

🟡 The Problem

Braking wastes energy (unless you have regenerative braking). Is there a way to reduce energy consumption between stations without slowing down the service too much?

🔵 The Intuition

Yes! Instead of keeping the motor powered all the way to the braking point, we can cut the power early and let the train coast. During coasting, the supply is disconnected and the train gradually slows down due to friction and wind resistance alone. Then, when the speed has dropped enough, we apply mechanical brakes to come to a complete stop.

Coasting trades a slightly longer travel time for significant energy savings. In metro systems with frequent stops, this is a major operational technique.

🟢 Deep Dive

With coasting, the duty cycle becomes five phases:

  1. Acceleration (\(t_1\)): 0 to \(V_m\)
  2. Free running (\(t_2\)): constant at \(V_m\)
  3. Coasting (\(t_3\)): supply off, speed decreases gradually under friction
  4. Braking (\(t_4\)): mechanical brakes to standstill
  5. Stop (\(t_{\text{stop}}\)): stationary at station

The speed-time diagram is no longer a clean trapezoid — the coasting phase has a gentle curve as friction decelerates the train naturally (much slower than mechanical braking).

💡
Energy Savings from Coasting Coasting is standard practice in metro/subway operations. The driver (or automatic control system) cuts power a calculated distance before the station. The energy saved can be 10-30% compared to full-power cruising followed by hard braking.
📝 Takeaway

The traction duty cycle is a repeating pattern of accelerate, cruise, decelerate, stop. The ideal motor characteristic is constant torque (low speed) then constant power (high speed). Coasting — cutting power before braking — saves significant energy in urban transit.

4. Calculation of Traction Drive Rating

▼
🟡 The Problem

You're an engineer designing the electric drive for a new metro line. The civil engineers tell you: "Stations are 2 km apart. The train must average at least 40 km/h including stop time. The train weighs 400 tonnes." Your job: figure out how much force the motors need to produce, and therefore how powerful they need to be. If you get it wrong, the train either can't keep schedule (underdesigned) or costs millions more than necessary (overdesigned).

🔵 The Intuition

We use the trapezoidal speed-time diagram as our model. From it, we can calculate:

  • The required acceleration rate \(\alpha\)
  • The free-running speed \(V_m\)
  • The total distance \(D\) as the area under the curve
  • The average speed \(V_{\text{av}} = D/T\)

Then, knowing the acceleration rate and the train mass, we can calculate the tractive effort (force) needed at the wheel rims.

🟢 Deep Dive — Speed-Time Relationships

From the trapezoidal profile (Figure 3.1), with the speed \(V_m\) in km/h:

Acceleration & Braking Time \[ t_1 = \frac{V_m}{\alpha}, \quad t_3 = \frac{V_m}{\beta} \]
Distance (area under speed-time curve) \[ D = \frac{V_m}{2} \cdot t_1 + V_m \cdot t_2 + \frac{V_m}{2} \cdot t_3 \]

Note: When \(V_m\) is in km/h and time in seconds, we need conversion factors. The distance \(D\) in km is:

\[ D = \frac{V_m}{3600} \left( \frac{t_1}{2} + t_2 + \frac{t_3}{2} \right) \quad [\text{km}] \]

The average speed and scheduled speed are defined as:

Average Speed vs. Scheduled Speed \[ V_{\text{av}} = \frac{D}{t_1 + t_2 + t_3} \quad \text{(excludes stop time)} \] \[ V_{\text{sch}} = \frac{D}{T} = \frac{D}{t_1 + t_2 + t_3 + t_{\text{stop}}} \quad \text{(includes stop time)} \]

4.1 Tractive Effort (Force)

🟡 The Problem

The motors don't exist in a vacuum. The force at the wheel rims — the tractive effort \(F_t\) — must overcome several things simultaneously: the inertia of the train (to accelerate it), gravity (on slopes), and friction (always present). Missing any component means your motor sizing is wrong.

🔵 The Intuition

The total force the motors must push with is the sum of three components:

  1. Accelerating force \(F_a\) — to speed up the train's mass (including the spinning wheels, gears, and motor rotors)
  2. Gradient force \(F_g\) — to climb hills (gravity pulling the train backward)
  3. Resistance force \(F_r\) — to overcome friction (wheel-rail contact, bearings, air drag)
Figure 4.1 — Tractive effort is the sum of forces needed for acceleration, gradient climbing, and friction resistance
🟢 Deep Dive — Accelerating Force Fa

In traction, there are two types of motion happening simultaneously:

  • Translational: the whole train body moving along the track
  • Rotational: wheels, axles, gearboxes, and motor rotors spinning

To account for both, we define an effective mass \(M_e\) that is larger than the actual train mass \(M\):

Effective Mass \[ M_e = (1.08 \;\text{to}\; 1.15) \times M \]

The factor 1.08 to 1.15 accounts for the rotational inertia of all spinning parts. For a train with heavy motor rotors, use the higher end; for a lighter vehicle, the lower end.

The accelerating force is then:

Accelerating Force \[ F_a = 277.8 \times M_e \times \alpha \quad \text{[N]} \]

Where does 277.8 come from? It's a unit conversion factor:

Unit Conversion \[ F = M \cdot a \] \[ \alpha \;\text{is in km/h/s} = \frac{1000}{3600}\;\text{m/s}^2 = \frac{1}{3.6}\;\text{m/s}^2 \] \[ M \;\text{is in tonnes} = 1000\;\text{kg} \] \[ \therefore\; F = 1000 \times \frac{1000}{3600} \times M \times \alpha = 277.8 \times M \times \alpha \;\text{[N]} \]

4.2 Gradient Force Fg

🟡 The Problem

Not all tracks are flat. When a train climbs a hill, gravity pulls it backward. How much extra force do the motors need to produce to maintain speed on a slope?

Figure 4.2 — Forces on a train climbing a gradient. The component of gravity along the slope opposes motion.
🟢 Deep Dive

The gradient \(G\) is defined as the vertical rise in metres per kilometre of track distance:

Gradient Definition \[ G = \frac{Y}{S} \times 1000 \]

where \(Y\) = vertical rise (m) and \(S\) = track distance (m). For small angles (which railway gradients always are), \(\sin\theta \approx \tan\theta \approx Y/S\).

The force needed to overcome the gradient is simply the component of the train's weight along the slope:

Gradient Force \[ F_g = M \cdot g \cdot \sin\theta \approx M \cdot g \cdot \frac{G}{1000} = 9.81 \times M \times G \quad \text{[N]} \]

(Here \(M\) is in tonnes, so we get: \(F_g = 9.81 \times 1000 \times M_{\text{kg}} \times G/1000 = 9.81 \times M \times G\) Newtons. The factors of 1000 cancel beautifully.)

4.3 Train Resistance Fr

🟢 Deep Dive

The train resistance force is often expressed using a specific resistance \(r\) in Newtons per tonne of train mass:

Resistance Force \[ F_r = r \times M \quad \text{[N]} \]

where \(r\) (N/tonne) accounts for:

  • Rolling friction between wheels and rails
  • Bearing friction in axle bearings
  • Aerodynamic drag (windage) — which increases with speed
  • Track curvature resistance

In practice, \(r\) is speed-dependent (increases with velocity), but for initial calculations it's often taken as a constant given value (e.g., 30 N/tonne).

4.4 The Complete Tractive Effort Equation

🟢 Deep Dive — Master Equation

Putting it all together, the total tractive effort during acceleration on a gradient is:

Total Tractive Effort (during acceleration) \[ \boxed{F_t = 277.8 \times M_e \times \alpha \;+\; 9.81 \times M \times G \;+\; r \times M \quad \text{[N]}} \]

Where:

SymbolQuantityUnits
\(M_e\)Effective mass (includes rotational inertia)tonnes
\(\alpha\)Acceleration ratekm/h/s
\(M\)Total train masstonnes
\(G\)Gradient (rise per km of track)m/km (or ‰)
\(r\)Specific train resistanceN/tonne

During free running (constant speed, no gradient): \(F_t = r \times M\) (only resistance).

During free running on a gradient: \(F_t = 9.81 \times M \times G + r \times M\).

📝 Takeaway

The tractive effort has three components: acceleration (dominant during starting), gradient (depends on terrain), and resistance (always present). The 277.8 factor converts from "tonnes × km/h/s" to Newtons. The effective mass \(M_e = 1.08\text{-}1.15 \times M\) accounts for spinning parts.

5. Maximum Tractive Effort & Wheel Slip

▼
🟡 The Problem

There's a hard physical limit to how much force you can apply at the wheels: if you push too hard, the wheels slip. The driving wheels lose traction and spin uselessly on the rails. This is dangerous (you lose control) and damaging (wheel flats, rail damage). Every traction engineer must know this limit.

🔵 The Intuition

It's exactly like your car on a wet road. If you floor the accelerator, the tires spin. The maximum force you can apply depends on two things:

  1. How much weight is on the driving wheels — more weight = more grip
  2. The friction coefficient between wheel and rail — dry rail is better than wet rail

This limit exists regardless of how powerful your motor is. A 10,000 HP motor is useless if the wheels can't grip the rail.

🟢 Deep Dive

The maximum tractive effort before wheel slip is:

Maximum Tractive Effort (adhesion limit) \[ F_{t,\max} = \mu \cdot M_d \cdot g = 9.81 \times \mu \times M_d \quad \text{[N]} \]

Where:

  • \(\mu\) = adhesion coefficient (friction between wheel and rail). Typically 0.15–0.35 depending on conditions (wet, dry, sandy, etc.).
  • \(M_d\) = adhesive weight = mass resting on the driving wheels (tonnes). Not the whole train mass — only the weight on wheels that are motor-driven.
⚠️
Design Constraint The calculated tractive effort \(F_t\) must always be less than \(F_{t,\max}\). If it isn't, you need either:
  • More weight on the driving wheels (heavier locomotive or distributed motors)
  • Lower acceleration rate
  • Sand application to increase \(\mu\)
  • Slip detection and torque reduction (modern electronic control)
📝 Takeaway

No matter how powerful the motor, the wheel-rail adhesion sets an absolute ceiling on tractive effort. \(F_{t,\max} = 9.81 \times \mu \times M_d\). Modern systems use slip detection and multi-motor distribution to get close to this limit without exceeding it.

6. Motor Coupling Torque

▼
🟡 The Problem

We've calculated the force at the wheel rims (\(F_t\)). But motors produce torque, not force. And there's a gearbox between the motor and the wheels. How do we translate the required wheel-rim force into the required motor shaft torque?

🔵 The Intuition

The force at the wheel rim, the wheel radius, the gear ratio, and the gearbox efficiency connect the motor torque to the tractive effort. If the gear ratio is small (motor spins much faster than the wheel), the motor needs less torque but runs faster. It's the classic torque-speed tradeoff through a gear.

🟢 Deep Dive

The relationship between motor coupling torque \(T_{\text{motor}}\) and tractive effort per motor \(F_t\) is:

Motor Coupling Torque \[ T_{\text{motor}} = \frac{F_t \times R}{n \times \eta} \times a \]

Wait — let's be more careful. If there are \(n\) motors sharing the total tractive effort, the force per motor at the wheel rim is \(F_t / n\). The torque at the wheel is \((F_t / n) \times R\) where \(R\) is the wheel radius. The gearbox with ratio \(a\) (defined as motor speed / wheel speed) transforms this:

Motor Coupling Torque (per motor) \[ T_{\text{motor}} = \frac{F_t}{n} \times \frac{R \times a}{\eta} \]

Here \(a = \omega_{\text{wheel}} / \omega_{\text{motor}}\) (which is less than 1 since the motor is faster), and \(\eta\) is the gearbox transmission efficiency (typically 0.92–0.98).

ⓘ
Sign convention note: Some textbooks define \(a\) as motor-to-wheel ratio (>1), others as wheel-to-motor ratio (<1). The key is: the motor sees less torque than the wheel torque, divided by the gear ratio and multiplied by 1/efficiency. Always check the convention being used.

If the gear ratio \(a\) is defined such that \(a < 1\) (as in the problem sheet, \(a = 0.25\)), then:

\[ T_{\text{motor}} = \frac{F_t}{n} \times R \times \frac{a}{\eta} \]
📝 Takeaway

The motor torque is the tractive effort per motor, multiplied by the wheel radius and gear ratio, divided by the transmission efficiency. The gearbox lets the motor run at higher speed with lower torque, which is usually more efficient and cost-effective.

7. Practice Problems

▼
✎ Problem 1 — Speed-Time Diagram Analysis Click to expand/collapse
🟡 Problem Statement

A train accelerates at \(\alpha = 5\) km/h/s for 30 seconds, then runs at constant speed for 10 minutes, then brakes at \(\beta = 5\) km/h/s. The stop time at the station is 5 minutes. Calculate:

  1. The free-running speed \(V_m\)
  2. The braking time \(t_3\)
  3. The distance \(D\) between stations
  4. The average speed \(V_{\text{av}}\)
  5. The scheduled speed \(V_{\text{sch}}\)
🟢 Solution
Step 1: Free-running speed

The train accelerates from rest:

\[ V_m = \alpha \times t_1 = 5 \times 30 = 150 \;\text{km/h} \]
Step 2: Braking time

Since \(\beta = 5\) km/h/s (same as \(\alpha\)):

\[ t_3 = \frac{V_m}{\beta} = \frac{150}{5} = 30 \;\text{s} \]
Step 3: Distance between stations

Convert the free-running time: \(t_2 = 10 \;\text{min} = 600 \;\text{s}\)

\[ D = \frac{V_m}{3600}\left(\frac{t_1}{2} + t_2 + \frac{t_3}{2}\right) \] \[ D = \frac{150}{3600}\left(\frac{30}{2} + 600 + \frac{30}{2}\right) \] \[ D = \frac{150}{3600}(15 + 600 + 15) = \frac{150}{3600} \times 630 \] \[ D = \frac{150 \times 630}{3600} = \frac{94500}{3600} = 26.25 \;\text{km} \]
Step 4: Average speed

Total running time (excluding stop):

\[ t_{\text{run}} = t_1 + t_2 + t_3 = 30 + 600 + 30 = 660 \;\text{s} = 11 \;\text{min} = \frac{11}{60}\;\text{hr} \] \[ V_{\text{av}} = \frac{D}{t_{\text{run}}} = \frac{26.25}{11/60} = \frac{26.25 \times 60}{11} = \frac{1575}{11} \approx 143.2 \;\text{km/h} \]
Step 5: Scheduled speed

Total time including stop: \(T = 660 + 300 = 960\;\text{s} = 16\;\text{min} = \frac{16}{60}\;\text{hr}\)

\[ V_{\text{sch}} = \frac{D}{T} = \frac{26.25}{16/60} = \frac{26.25 \times 60}{16} = \frac{1575}{16} \approx 98.4 \;\text{km/h} \]
📝 Result Summary
\(V_m\)150 km/h
\(t_3\)30 s
\(D\)26.25 km
\(V_{\text{av}}\)143.2 km/h
\(V_{\text{sch}}\)98.4 km/h

Notice how the scheduled speed (98.4 km/h) is much lower than the free-running speed (150 km/h) because of the 5-minute stop. This is why minimizing stop time is so important for improving transit system performance.

✎ Problem 4 — Motor Coupling Torque Calculation Click to expand/collapse
🟡 Problem Statement

A 100-tonne locomotive pulls a 500-tonne train (total mass \(M = 600\) tonnes). The effective mass factor is 1.1, so \(M_e = 1.1 \times 600 = 660\) tonnes. The train has 4 DC motors with:

  • Gear ratio: \(a = 0.25\)
  • Transmission efficiency: \(\eta = 95\% = 0.95\)
  • Wheel radius: \(R = 0.54\) m
  • Specific resistance: \(r = 30\) N/tonne

Calculate the coupling torque per motor at an acceleration of \(\alpha = 2\) km/h/s on level track.

🟢 Solution
Step 1: Calculate total tractive effort

On level track (\(G = 0\)), the total tractive effort is:

\[ F_t = 277.8 \times M_e \times \alpha + r \times M \] \[ F_t = 277.8 \times 660 \times 2 + 30 \times 600 \] \[ F_t = 366,696 + 18,000 = 384,696 \;\text{N} \]

That's approximately 384.7 kN — nearly 40 tonnes of force!

Step 2: Force per motor

With 4 motors sharing the load equally:

\[ F_{\text{per motor}} = \frac{384,696}{4} = 96,174 \;\text{N} \]
Step 3: Motor coupling torque

Using the coupling torque formula:

\[ T_{\text{motor}} = F_{\text{per motor}} \times R \times \frac{a}{\eta} \] \[ T_{\text{motor}} = 96,174 \times 0.54 \times \frac{0.25}{0.95} \] \[ T_{\text{motor}} = 96,174 \times 0.54 \times 0.2632 \] \[ T_{\text{motor}} = 96,174 \times 0.1421 \] \[ T_{\text{motor}} \approx 13,667 \;\text{N}\cdot\text{m} \approx 13.67 \;\text{kN}\cdot\text{m} \]
📝 Result Summary
Total tractive effort \(F_t\)384,696 N (384.7 kN)
Force per motor96,174 N (96.2 kN)
Motor coupling torque~13,670 N·m (13.67 kN·m)

Note how the gear ratio (\(a = 0.25\)) significantly reduces the motor torque compared to the wheel-rim torque. The motor shaft torque is about 1/4 of what it would be without the gearbox (minus efficiency losses), but the motor spins 4 times faster than the wheel. This is the whole point of the gearbox.

8. Lecture Summary

▼
📝 Complete Summary

1. Traction = Electric Motor + Vehicle

Traction drives are friction-type loads with very high inertia. The resistance transitions from static-friction-dominated at standstill to windage-dominated at high speed.

2. Two Families of Traction

Diesel-electric (onboard generation) and grid-powered (external supply via catenary or third rail). DC series motors were dominant historically; modern systems use AC induction motors with inverters.

3. The Duty Cycle

Accelerate → cruise → brake → stop. The trapezoidal speed-time diagram is our primary design tool. Coasting (cutting power before braking) saves 10-30% energy.

4. Ideal Characteristics

Constant torque below base speed, constant power above. This extracts maximum performance across the entire speed range.

5. Tractive Effort

\(F_t = 277.8 M_e \alpha + 9.81 M G + r M\). Three components: acceleration, gradient, resistance. The effective mass accounts for rotational inertia of spinning parts.

6. Limits

Adhesion sets a hard ceiling: \(F_{t,\max} = 9.81 \mu M_d\). Exceed this and wheels slip. Motor coupling torque relates to tractive effort through the gearbox ratio and efficiency.

📐

Key Equations

\(F_t = 277.8 M_e \alpha + 9.81 M G + rM\)

\(F_{t,\max} = 9.81 \mu M_d\)

\(T_{\text{motor}} = \frac{F_t}{n} \cdot \frac{R \cdot a}{\eta}\)

📊

Key Numbers

\(M_e = 1.08\text{-}1.15 \times M\)

\(\mu = 0.15\text{-}0.35\)

277.8 = unit conversion factor

🚨

Common Pitfalls

Forgetting to use \(M_e\) instead of \(M\) for acceleration. Mixing up gear ratio conventions. Forgetting to check adhesion limit.
