DC Chopper Drives

High-frequency DC-DC converters for efficient motor control: step-down and step-up choppers, motoring, regenerative braking, and multi-quadrant operation.

1. Introduction to Choppers

▾
The Problem / The Why

In the previous lectures, we used phase-controlled rectifiers (thyristor converters) to control DC motors from an AC supply. They work, but they have a fundamental limitation: the switching frequency is locked to the AC mains (50 or 60 Hz). This means the output has large voltage and current ripples, which cause torque pulsations, extra heating, and a wide region of discontinuous conduction where speed regulation is poor.

What if our DC supply is already available — from batteries (like in an electric vehicle), solar panels, or a simple diode rectifier? We need a way to chop the DC voltage into a variable average value. That is exactly what a DC chopper does, and it does so at frequencies far above 50 Hz — typically 2.5 kHz to 200 kHz — giving us dramatically smoother current and faster dynamic response.

The Intuition

Think of a chopper as a very fast light dimmer switch, but for DC. If you flip a switch ON and OFF very rapidly and adjust the fraction of time it stays ON, the average voltage seen by the load changes smoothly. The motor's inductance acts like a low-pass filter — it "averages out" the chopped waveform and sees a nearly smooth current. The faster you chop, the smoother it gets.

Deep Dive — Chopper Advantages

Compared to phase-controlled rectifiers, DC choppers offer:

  • High efficiency — power semiconductor operates as a switch (low conduction losses)
  • Flexibility — duty ratio can be varied continuously from 0 to 1
  • Light weight and small size — high switching frequency means smaller filter components
  • Quick response — response time limited only by switching speed, not mains frequency
  • Regeneration capability — can return energy to the source down to very low speeds
  • Less current ripple — higher frequency means smaller \(\Delta I_a\), reducing torque pulsations
  • Smaller discontinuous conduction region — better speed regulation and transient response

Switching Devices

Deep Dive — Device Selection

The choice of switching device depends on the application's voltage, current, and frequency requirements:

DeviceSwitching FrequencyApplication
Thyristors / GTOsUp to a few hundred HzHigh voltage, high power (MW range). Limited frequency due to slow turn-off.
Power Transistors / IGBTs> 2.5 kHz typicalMedium power. IGBTs dominate modern industrial drives.
MOSFETs> 200 kHzLow power applications. Fastest switching, lowest losses at high frequency.
Takeaway

Choppers are preferred when a DC source is already available (batteries, fuel cells, diode bridge output) or when higher performance is needed than phase-controlled converters can deliver. Higher chopping frequency directly translates to smoother motor current and better dynamic performance.

2. Chopper Types

▾
The Problem / The Why

We need the output voltage to be lower than the supply for normal motor speed control (motoring below base speed), and higher than the supply for regenerative braking (to push energy back into the source when \(E_g\) is low). These are two fundamentally different tasks requiring different circuit topologies.

The Intuition

A step-down (buck) chopper is like a faucet — it can only reduce the water pressure from the supply. A step-up (boost) chopper is like a pump — it stores energy in an inductor and releases it at a higher voltage, boosting the pressure above the supply level. Together, they cover all operating needs.

a) Step-Down Chopper (Buck / Class A)

Deep Dive — Circuit and Operation
Figure 1: Step-down (buck) chopper circuit with DC motor load

The chopper has two operating intervals in each period \(T\):

1. Duty interval (switch G ON, \(0 \le t \le \delta T\)): The supply voltage \(V\) is applied directly across the motor. The armature current flows through G and increases.

2. Freewheeling interval (switch G OFF, \(\delta T \le t \le T\)): The motor current continues to flow through the freewheeling diode D. The voltage across the motor is essentially zero (ignoring diode drop). The current decreases as the motor's stored energy dissipates in \(R_a\) and \(E_g\).

Output Voltage
\[ V_a = \begin{cases} V & \text{for } 0 \le t \le \delta T \\ 0 & \text{for } \delta T \le t \le T \end{cases} \]
Average Output Voltage
\[ V_{a(\text{avg})} = \delta \cdot V \]

where \(\delta = T_{\text{on}} / T\) is the duty ratio (0 to 1)

Figure 2: Output voltage (blue) and armature current (green) waveforms. Red dashed line is the average voltage.
Takeaway

The step-down chopper reduces the average voltage from \(V\) to \(\delta V\) by varying the duty ratio \(\delta\). It operates in quadrant I (positive voltage, positive current) — motoring in the forward direction.

b) Step-Up Chopper (Boost / Class B)

The Problem / The Why

During regenerative braking, the motor acts as a generator. Its back-EMF \(E_g\) may be lower than the DC supply voltage \(V\). But to push current back into the supply, we need the motor terminal voltage to exceed \(V\). How do we boost a lower voltage to a higher one using just a switch and an inductor?

The Intuition

Imagine pressing down on a spring (storing energy in the inductor during the ON period) and then releasing it so it bounces higher than where it started (the inductor voltage adds to the source voltage during the OFF period). The key insight: an inductor resists changes in current, and when you suddenly force the current to redirect, it generates a voltage spike that "boosts" the output.

Deep Dive — Step-Up Operation
Figure 3: Step-up (boost) chopper circuit for regenerative braking

During ON period (\(0 \le t \le \delta T\)): Switch G is closed. The source current \(i_s\) increases from \(I_{s1}\) to \(I_{s2}\), storing energy in the inductor \(L\). The diode D is reverse-biased, and the capacitor C sustains the load voltage.

During OFF period (\(\delta T \le t \le T\)): Switch G opens. The inductor current cannot change instantaneously, so the inductor's self-induced EMF adds to the source voltage, forward-biasing diode D. Current flows to the supply through D, and the capacitor charges. Current decreases from \(I_{s2}\) to \(I_{s1}\).

Step-Up Voltage Relationship
\[ V_{ab} = \frac{V_a}{1 - \delta} \]

Since in steady state the average voltage across \(L\) is zero:
\(V = V_L + V_{ab}\), and \(\bar{V}_L = 0\), so \(V_a = (1-\delta) \cdot V_{ab}\)
Rearranging: \(V_{ab} = V_a / (1-\delta)\)

As \(\delta \to 1\), the output voltage \(V_{ab} \to \infty\) in theory. In practice, losses limit the boost ratio. For \(\delta = 0.5\), the output is twice the input.

Takeaway

The step-up chopper boosts the motor's back-EMF to a level exceeding the supply voltage, enabling regenerative braking. As duty ratio \(\delta\) increases, the boost factor \(1/(1-\delta)\) increases. This topology operates in quadrant II or IV (current reversed relative to motoring).

3. Motoring Operation — Step-Down Chopper

▾
The Problem / The Why

Knowing the average voltage \(V_a = \delta V\) is great for steady-state speed calculations, but in reality the armature current is not constant — it ripples between a minimum \(I_{a1}\) and a maximum \(I_{a2}\). To properly design the chopper (select switching frequency, evaluate motor heating, determine if current becomes discontinuous), we need the exact time-domain expressions for the armature current during each interval.

The Intuition

During the duty interval, the current ramps up because the supply voltage exceeds the back-EMF plus resistive drop. During freewheeling, the current ramps down because the back-EMF and resistance "drain" the stored magnetic energy. The current waveform looks like a sawtooth riding on top of the average value, with the peak-to-peak amplitude (ripple) depending on how fast we chop and how much inductance the motor has.

Figure 4: Separately excited DC motor with step-down chopper (motoring mode)

Duty Interval (\(0 \le t \le \delta T\))

Deep Dive — KVL During ON Period

When switch G is ON, applying KVL around the armature loop:

Armature Loop Equation (Duty Interval)
\[ R_a \, i_a + L_a \frac{di_a}{dt} + E = V \]

This is a first-order linear ODE with constant coefficients. With initial condition \(i_a(0) = I_{a1}\) (the minimum current at the start of the duty interval):

Current During Duty Interval (Eq. 3.7)
\[ i_a(t) = \frac{V - E}{R_a}\left(1 - e^{-t/\tau_a}\right) + I_{a1} \, e^{-t/\tau_a} \]

where \(\tau_a = L_a / R_a\) is the armature time constant

At the end of the duty interval (\(t = \delta T\)), the current reaches its maximum value \(I_{a2}\):

Maximum Current (Eq. 3.8)
\[ I_{a2} = \frac{V - E}{R_a}\left(1 - e^{-\delta T/\tau_a}\right) + I_{a1} \, e^{-\delta T/\tau_a} \]

Freewheeling Interval (\(\delta T \le t \le T\))

Deep Dive — KVL During OFF Period

When switch G is OFF, the motor current freewheels through diode D. The supply is disconnected, so KVL gives:

Armature Loop Equation (Freewheel Interval)
\[ R_a \, i_a + L_a \frac{di_a}{dt'} + E = 0 \]

where \(t' = t - \delta T\) (reset time origin to start of freewheel)

With initial condition \(i_a(0) = I_{a2}\):

Current During Freewheel (Eq. 3.10)
\[ i_a(t') = -\frac{E}{R_a}\left(1 - e^{-t'/\tau_a}\right) + I_{a2} \, e^{-t'/\tau_a} \]

At the end of the freewheeling interval (\(t' = (1-\delta)T\)), the current drops to its minimum \(I_{a1}\):

Minimum Current (Eq. 3.11)
\[ I_{a1} = -\frac{E}{R_a}\left(1 - e^{-(1-\delta)T/\tau_a}\right) + I_{a2} \, e^{-(1-\delta)T/\tau_a} \]

Steady-State Solution

Deep Dive — Solving for \(I_{a1}\) and \(I_{a2}\)

In steady state, the current waveform is periodic: the \(I_{a1}\) at the end of one cycle equals the \(I_{a1}\) at the start of the next. We solve equations 3.8 and 3.11 simultaneously (two equations, two unknowns):

Simultaneous Equations
\[ \begin{cases} I_{a2} = \dfrac{V - E}{R_a}\left(1 - e^{-\delta T/\tau_a}\right) + I_{a1} \, e^{-\delta T/\tau_a} \\[12pt] I_{a1} = -\dfrac{E}{R_a}\left(1 - e^{-(1-\delta)T/\tau_a}\right) + I_{a2} \, e^{-(1-\delta)T/\tau_a} \end{cases} \]

Let \(x = e^{-\delta T / \tau_a}\) and \(y = e^{-(1-\delta)T / \tau_a}\). Solving:

\[ I_{a1} = \frac{V}{R_a} \cdot \frac{1 - x}{1 - xy} \cdot y - \frac{E}{R_a} \] \[ I_{a2} = \frac{V}{R_a} \cdot \frac{1 - x}{1 - xy} - \frac{E}{R_a} \]

The average armature current is:

\[ I_{a(\text{avg})} = \frac{\delta V - E}{R_a} \]

This is exactly what we'd get from DC analysis: \(V_{a(\text{avg})} = \delta V = E + I_a R_a\)

Figure 5: Steady-state armature current ripple between Ia1 and Ia2
Deep Dive — Current Ripple Approximation

For high switching frequencies (where \(T \ll \tau_a\)), we can linearize the exponentials and obtain a simple ripple formula:

Current Ripple (Approximate)
\[ \Delta I_a = I_{a2} - I_{a1} \approx \frac{V \cdot \delta(1-\delta)}{f \cdot L_a} \]

Maximum ripple occurs at \(\delta = 0.5\): \(\;\Delta I_{a,\max} = \dfrac{V}{4 f L_a}\)

This shows that doubling the switching frequency halves the current ripple, and more inductance also reduces ripple — hence the advantage of choppers over low-frequency thyristor converters.

Takeaway

The motoring current waveform is an exponential sawtooth between \(I_{a1}\) and \(I_{a2}\). The average current follows the simple DC relation \(I_a = (\delta V - E)/R_a\). Ripple decreases with higher frequency and higher inductance. For exam problems, you'll either solve the exact exponential equations or use the linear ripple approximation depending on whether \(T/\tau_a\) is small.

4. Regenerative Braking — Step-Up Chopper

▾
The Problem / The Why

When a motor needs to slow down, we want to recover the kinetic energy stored in the rotating mass rather than waste it as heat. In regenerative braking, the motor acts as a generator and feeds energy back to the DC supply. But the motor's back-EMF \(E\) at lower speeds is less than the supply voltage \(V\). We need a step-up chopper to boost the effective terminal voltage so current can flow back into the supply, enabling regeneration down to very low speeds.

The Intuition

Think of regenerative braking as "reverse motoring." During motoring, the supply pushes current into the motor. During regeneration, the motor (now a generator) pushes current back. The step-up chopper is the "voltage amplifier" that lets the generator's weak voltage overcome the supply voltage, like a lever that lets a small force lift a heavy weight.

Figure 6: Regenerative braking circuit using step-up chopper

Energy Storage Interval (\(0 \le t \le \delta T\))

Deep Dive — Switch G ON

When G is ON, the motor (generator) drives current through \(R_a\), \(L_a\), and the switch. The diode D is reverse-biased. Energy is stored in the inductance:

KVL (Energy Storage)
\[ R_a \, i_a + L_a \frac{di_a}{dt} = E \]

With \(i_a(0) = I_{a1}\):

\[ i_a(t) = \frac{E}{R_a}\left(1 - e^{-t/\tau_a}\right) + I_{a1} \, e^{-t/\tau_a} \]

At \(t = \delta T\): \(\;I_{a2} = \dfrac{E}{R_a}\left(1 - e^{-\delta T/\tau_a}\right) + I_{a1} \, e^{-\delta T/\tau_a}\)

Energy Transfer Interval (\(\delta T \le t \le T\))

Deep Dive — Switch G OFF

When G opens, diode D becomes forward-biased and current flows into the supply \(V\). Now KVL includes the supply voltage:

KVL (Energy Transfer)
\[ R_a \, i_a + L_a \frac{di_a}{dt'} + V = E \]

With \(i_a(0) = I_{a2}\):

\[ i_a(t') = \frac{E - V}{R_a}\left(1 - e^{-t'/\tau_a}\right) + I_{a2} \, e^{-t'/\tau_a} \]

At \(t' = (1-\delta)T\): \(\;I_{a1} = \dfrac{E - V}{R_a}\left(1 - e^{-(1-\delta)T/\tau_a}\right) + I_{a2} \, e^{-(1-\delta)T/\tau_a}\)

Note: Since \(E < V\), the term \((E-V)/R_a\) is negative, so the current decreases during this interval, as expected — energy is being delivered to the supply.

Deep Dive — Key Regeneration Relationships

From the step-up chopper analysis, the average motor terminal voltage during regeneration is:

Regenerative Braking Voltage
\[ V_a = (1 - \delta) \cdot V \]

The motor's voltage equation as a generator:

\[ V_a = E - I_a R_a \]

Combining:

\[ (1-\delta) V = E - I_a R_a \]

If \(I_a R_a\) is small: \(\;E \approx (1-\delta) V\), so \(\;\omega_m = \frac{(1-\delta)V}{K\phi}\)

Regenerated Power
\[ P_{\text{regen}} \approx V \cdot I_a \cdot (1-\delta) \]
Takeaway

Regenerative braking with a step-up chopper stores energy in the motor inductance during the ON period and transfers it to the supply during the OFF period. The effective terminal voltage is \((1-\delta)V\), which must be less than \(E\) for current to flow in the braking direction. By adjusting \(\delta\), we can control braking torque and recover energy down to low speeds.

5. Dynamic (Rheostatic) Braking

▾
The Problem / The Why

Regenerative braking requires the supply to accept the returned energy. But what if the supply cannot absorb energy (e.g., a battery that's fully charged, or the DC bus is at its voltage limit)? In that case, we need an alternative: dissipate the braking energy as heat in a resistor. This is dynamic braking (also called rheostatic braking). A chopper controls the effective braking resistance to regulate the braking torque smoothly.

The Intuition

Dynamic braking is like engine braking in a car — you convert kinetic energy to heat (in resistors instead of engine friction). The chopper acts as a "variable resistor" by adjusting how much of the time the braking resistor is connected.

Deep Dive — Dynamic Braking with Chopper
Figure 7: Dynamic braking with chopper-controlled braking resistor

The motor is disconnected from the supply and the armature is connected to a braking resistance \(R_b\) through a chopper. During the ON period, current circulates through the motor and switch G (storing energy in \(L_a\)). During the OFF period, current flows through \(R_b\), dissipating energy as heat.

Effective Braking Resistance
\[ R_e = (1-\delta) \cdot R_b \]
Average Braking Voltage
\[ V_b = V_{a(\text{avg})} = (1-\delta) \cdot I_a \cdot R_b \]

By varying \(\delta\) from 0 to 1, the effective resistance varies from \(R_b\) (maximum braking) to 0 (no braking), giving smooth control of braking torque.

Takeaway

Dynamic braking converts kinetic energy to heat in a resistor. The chopper duty ratio controls the effective braking resistance: \(R_e = (1-\delta)R_b\). This is used when the supply cannot accept regenerated energy, or as a backup when regenerative braking reaches its limits.

6. Composite Braking

▾
The Problem / The Why

In real applications like DC traction (trains, trams, electric vehicles), we need braking over the entire speed range. Regenerative braking is efficient but only works when the motor's back-EMF is high enough. At very low speeds, \(E\) is too small for regeneration. We need a strategy that combines multiple braking methods to cover the full speed range.

The Intuition

Composite braking is like a car with both regenerative and friction brakes. At high speed, the regenerative system recovers most of the energy. As speed drops and regeneration becomes weak, the friction brakes (dynamic braking) take over. Near standstill, mechanical brakes bring the vehicle to a complete stop. Each method covers the other's weakness.

Deep Dive — Composite Braking Strategy

A composite braking system combines regenerative and dynamic braking in a single circuit:

  • High speed: Regenerative braking active. The step-up chopper boosts the motor's back-EMF and returns energy to the supply. Duty ratio is adjusted to control braking torque.
  • Capacitor overvoltage protection: A braking resistor \(R_b\) and thyristor \(T_1\) are added in parallel. When the DC bus capacitor voltage exceeds the supply tolerance limit, \(T_1\) fires and diverts excess energy to \(R_b\).
  • Low speed: When back-EMF drops too low for effective regeneration, the circuit switches to pure dynamic braking. The chopper now controls the braking resistor directly.
  • Very low speed / standstill: Mechanical brakes engage for the final stop, since electromagnetic braking torque approaches zero.

Speed control during braking: Reduce \(V_a\) by changing the duty cycle while keeping \(E > V_a\) so that current flows in the braking direction.

Takeaway

Composite braking combines regenerative braking (energy-efficient, high speeds), dynamic braking (medium-to-low speeds), and mechanical braking (standstill) to provide smooth, controllable deceleration over the full speed range. This is the standard approach in DC traction systems.

7. Two-Quadrant Chopper Drives

▾
The Problem / The Why

So far, we've treated motoring (step-down chopper) and regenerative braking (step-up chopper) as separate circuits. In practice, we want a single circuit that can do both — smoothly transitioning between motoring and braking without switching hardware. This is the two-quadrant chopper.

The Intuition

A two-quadrant chopper is like a car's accelerator and regenerative braking pedal combined into one system. Press the "gas" (Q1 operates as step-down) and you motor forward. Lift off and "brake" (Q2 operates as step-up) and you recover energy back to the battery. The voltage across the motor stays positive, but the current direction reverses between motoring and braking.

Deep Dive — Two-Quadrant Circuit
Figure 8: Two-quadrant chopper (Class A + Class B combined). Operates in Quadrants I and IV.

The two-quadrant chopper consists of two switches (Q1, Q2) and two antiparallel diodes (D1, D2):

ModeActive DevicesQuadrantOperation
Motoring (Forward)Q1 (step-down), D2 (freewheel)I: +Va, +IaClass A chopper
Regenerative BrakingQ2 (step-up), D1 (to supply)IV: +Va, −IaClass B chopper

The transition between motoring and braking is seamless — no mechanical switching is needed. The controller simply changes which transistor is pulsed.

Takeaway

A two-quadrant chopper combines step-down (Q1, D2) and step-up (Q2, D1) in one half-bridge. It handles forward motoring and regenerative braking with positive voltage and bidirectional current. This is the minimum configuration for applications like electric vehicles where energy recovery during braking is desired.

8. Four-Quadrant Chopper Drives

▾
The Problem / The Why

Many industrial applications require full four-quadrant operation: forward motoring, forward braking (regeneration), reverse motoring, and reverse braking. Think of an elevator, a rolling mill, or a robotic arm — they all need to run and brake in both directions. The two-quadrant chopper only handles one direction of voltage. To get bidirectional voltage and current, we need the full H-bridge.

The Intuition

The four-quadrant chopper is an H-bridge — imagine the motor sitting in the middle of the letter "H," with a switch at each of the four corners. By choosing which diagonal pair of switches to close, we control both the direction and magnitude of the voltage applied to the motor, enabling full forward/reverse operation with regeneration in both directions.

Deep Dive — H-Bridge Configuration
Figure 9: Four-quadrant H-bridge chopper drive with all four operating quadrants active

The H-bridge uses four switches (Q1–Q4) with antiparallel diodes (D1–D4):

Switches ONMotor VoltageQuadrantOperation
Q1 + Q2+VaI (+V, +I): Forward motoringPositive voltage applied
Q3 + Q4−VaIII (−V, −I): Reverse motoringNegative voltage applied
D3 + D4−V back to supplyII (−V, +I): Forward regen. brakingCurrent forced to supply
D1 + D2+V back to supplyIV (+V, −I): Reverse regen. brakingCurrent forced to supply

By using PWM on the appropriate switch pair, the H-bridge can produce any average voltage from \(-V\) to \(+V\) and handle current in either direction, giving true four-quadrant operation.

Takeaway

The four-quadrant H-bridge chopper is the most versatile DC drive topology. It provides full bidirectional voltage and current control, enabling forward/reverse motoring and forward/reverse regenerative braking. This is the standard topology for servo drives, industrial positioning, and any application requiring full speed reversal with regeneration.

9. Practice Problems

▾
Problem 1

A separately excited DC motor is fed from a 200 V DC source through a step-down chopper operating at 1 kHz. The motor has \(R_a = 0.5\;\Omega\), \(L_a = 10\;\text{mH}\), and \(K\phi = 0.4\;\text{V}\cdot\text{s/rad}\). The motor draws an average current of 20 A. Find: (a) the duty ratio \(\delta\), (b) the motor speed in rpm, and (c) the peak-to-peak current ripple.

(a) Average voltage equation:

\(V_a = \delta \cdot V = E + I_a R_a\)

\(E = K\phi \cdot \omega_m\), so we need speed first... Actually, let's use the information given.

We know \(I_a = 20\) A, so the resistive drop is \(I_a R_a = 20 \times 0.5 = 10\) V.

We need another equation. Let's find \(E\) from the motor equation and duty ratio simultaneously:

\(\delta \cdot 200 = E + 10\), so \(E = 200\delta - 10\)

Wait — we need either speed or duty ratio as a given. Let's assume the problem intends that the motor runs at a speed such that \(I_a = 20\) A and we need to find \(\delta\) for a given speed. Since no speed is specified, let's find the duty ratio if the motor runs at 800 rpm.

At 800 rpm: \(\omega_m = 2\pi \times 800/60 = 83.78\) rad/s

\(E = 0.4 \times 83.78 = 33.51\) V

\(V_a = E + I_a R_a = 33.51 + 10 = 43.51\) V

\(\delta = V_a / V = 43.51 / 200 = \mathbf{0.2176}\)

(b) Speed: 800 rpm (as assumed above, or \(\omega_m = (V_a - I_a R_a)/(K\phi)\) for any given \(\delta\)).

(c) Current ripple:

\(\Delta I_a = \dfrac{V \cdot \delta(1-\delta)}{f \cdot L_a} = \dfrac{200 \times 0.2176 \times 0.7824}{1000 \times 0.01}\)

\(\Delta I_a = \dfrac{200 \times 0.1703}{10} = \dfrac{34.06}{10} = \mathbf{3.41\;\text{A}}\)

Problem 2

A DC motor (\(R_a = 1\;\Omega\), \(K\phi = 0.5\;\text{V}\cdot\text{s/rad}\)) is braking regeneratively using a step-up chopper connected to a 120 V DC supply. The motor speed is 1200 rpm and the average braking current is 15 A. Find: (a) the duty ratio, (b) the regenerated power.

(a) Finding the duty ratio:

Motor speed: \(\omega_m = 2\pi \times 1200/60 = 125.66\) rad/s

Back-EMF: \(E = K\phi \cdot \omega_m = 0.5 \times 125.66 = 62.83\) V

Generator voltage equation: \(V_a = E - I_a R_a = 62.83 - 15 \times 1 = 47.83\) V

Step-up chopper relation: \(V_a = (1-\delta) \cdot V\)

\(47.83 = (1-\delta) \times 120\)

\(1-\delta = 47.83/120 = 0.3986\)

\(\delta = 1 - 0.3986 = \mathbf{0.6014}\)

(b) Regenerated power:

\(P_{\text{regen}} = V \cdot I_a \cdot (1-\delta) = 120 \times 15 \times 0.3986 = \mathbf{717.5\;\text{W}}\)

Check

The motor generates \(P_{\text{gen}} = E \cdot I_a = 62.83 \times 15 = 942.5\) W. After \(I_a^2 R_a = 225\) W of copper losses, the net power to supply is \(942.5 - 225 = 717.5\) W. This matches!

Problem 3

A step-down chopper has \(V = 220\) V, \(T_{\text{on}} = 1\) ms, \(T_{\text{off}} = 3\) ms. The motor has \(R_a = 2\;\Omega\) and \(K\phi = 0.3\;\text{V}\cdot\text{s/rad}\). Find: (a) the chopping frequency, (b) the duty ratio, (c) the average output voltage, (d) the motor speed if \(I_a = 10\) A.

(a) Chopping frequency:

\(T = T_{\text{on}} + T_{\text{off}} = 1 + 3 = 4\) ms

\(f = 1/T = 1/0.004 = \mathbf{250\;\text{Hz}}\)

(b) Duty ratio:

\(\delta = T_{\text{on}}/T = 1/4 = \mathbf{0.25}\)

(c) Average output voltage:

\(V_a = \delta \cdot V = 0.25 \times 220 = \mathbf{55\;\text{V}}\)

(d) Motor speed:

\(V_a = E + I_a R_a\)

\(E = V_a - I_a R_a = 55 - 10 \times 2 = 35\) V

\(\omega_m = E/(K\phi) = 35/0.3 = 116.67\) rad/s

\(N = 60 \omega_m / (2\pi) = 60 \times 116.67 / 6.2832 = \mathbf{1114\;\text{rpm}}\)

Problem 4

For dynamic braking with a chopper, a DC motor (\(K\phi = 0.6\;\text{V}\cdot\text{s/rad}\), \(R_a = 0.5\;\Omega\)) is connected to a braking resistance \(R_b = 5\;\Omega\). The motor is running at 1500 rpm. If the duty ratio is 0.4, find: (a) the effective braking resistance, (b) the braking current, (c) the braking torque, and (d) the power dissipated in \(R_b\).

(a) Effective braking resistance:

\(R_e = (1-\delta) \cdot R_b = (1-0.4) \times 5 = 0.6 \times 5 = \mathbf{3\;\Omega}\)

(b) Braking current:

\(\omega_m = 2\pi \times 1500/60 = 157.08\) rad/s

\(E = K\phi \cdot \omega_m = 0.6 \times 157.08 = 94.25\) V

\(I_a = E/(R_a + R_e) = 94.25/(0.5 + 3) = 94.25/3.5 = \mathbf{26.93\;\text{A}}\)

(c) Braking torque:

\(T_b = K\phi \cdot I_a = 0.6 \times 26.93 = \mathbf{16.16\;\text{N}\cdot\text{m}}\)

(d) Power dissipated in \(R_b\):

\(P_{R_b} = I_a^2 \cdot R_e = 26.93^2 \times 3 = 725.2 \times 3 = \mathbf{2175.7\;\text{W}}\)

(The remaining power \(I_a^2 R_a = 362.6\) W is lost in the armature resistance.)

Problem 5

A step-down chopper supplies a separately excited DC motor from a 300 V DC source. The motor parameters are \(R_a = 0.8\;\Omega\), \(L_a = 20\;\text{mH}\), \(K\phi = 0.5\;\text{V}\cdot\text{s/rad}\). The chopper operates at 500 Hz with \(\delta = 0.6\). Find: (a) the average motor voltage, (b) if the motor drives a load requiring 25 A average current, find the motor speed, (c) the peak-to-peak current ripple, (d) \(I_{\max}\) and \(I_{\min}\).

(a) Average motor voltage:

\(V_a = \delta \cdot V = 0.6 \times 300 = \mathbf{180\;\text{V}}\)

(b) Motor speed:

\(E = V_a - I_a R_a = 180 - 25 \times 0.8 = 180 - 20 = 160\) V

\(\omega_m = E/(K\phi) = 160/0.5 = 320\) rad/s

\(N = 60 \times 320/(2\pi) = \mathbf{3056\;\text{rpm}}\)

(c) Peak-to-peak current ripple:

\(\Delta I_a = \dfrac{V \cdot \delta(1-\delta)}{f \cdot L_a} = \dfrac{300 \times 0.6 \times 0.4}{500 \times 0.02} = \dfrac{72}{10} = \mathbf{7.2\;\text{A}}\)

(d) Maximum and minimum current:

\(I_{\max} = I_{a(\text{avg})} + \Delta I_a / 2 = 25 + 3.6 = \mathbf{28.6\;\text{A}}\)

\(I_{\min} = I_{a(\text{avg})} - \Delta I_a / 2 = 25 - 3.6 = \mathbf{21.4\;\text{A}}\)

Note

Since \(I_{\min} = 21.4 > 0\), the current is continuous. If \(I_{\min}\) were negative (which is impossible physically since the diode prevents it), it would mean discontinuous conduction and the linear ripple formula would no longer apply.

Lecture Summary

▾
Big Picture

In this lecture, we covered the complete landscape of DC chopper drives:

  1. Chopper fundamentals: High-frequency switching of a DC source produces a variable average voltage. Higher frequency means smoother current (less ripple) and better dynamic response than phase-controlled converters.
  2. Step-down (Buck): \(V_a = \delta V\). Used for motoring (Quadrant I). Current ripple: \(\Delta I_a = V\delta(1-\delta)/(fL_a)\).
  3. Step-up (Boost): \(V_{out} = V_{in}/(1-\delta)\). Used for regenerative braking, boosting the motor's low back-EMF to exceed the supply voltage.
  4. Motoring analysis: Exact exponential solutions for current during duty and freewheeling intervals. Steady-state requires solving two simultaneous equations for \(I_{a1}\) and \(I_{a2}\).
  5. Regenerative braking: Step-up chopper stores energy in inductance during ON, transfers to supply during OFF. Terminal voltage: \(V_a = (1-\delta)V\).
  6. Dynamic braking: Energy dissipated in \(R_b\) with effective resistance \(R_e = (1-\delta)R_b\).
  7. Two-quadrant: Half-bridge combining Class A (motoring) and Class B (regeneration) for forward operation.
  8. Four-quadrant: Full H-bridge for complete forward/reverse motoring and braking.

These chopper topologies form the basis of modern DC drive systems, from small battery-powered robots to large industrial machines and electric vehicles.
