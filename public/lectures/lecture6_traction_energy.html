Section 3.3 • Electric Traction • kWh per tonne-km

⚡ What is Specific Energy Consumption?

▼
🟡 The Problem

Here's the scenario. You're an engineer at a railway company. The finance team walks in and says: "We need to know the electricity bill for running this train on this route. How many kilowatt-hours per trip?"

You know the train's speed profile. You know the route (gradients, distances). You know the motor and gearbox efficiencies. But how do you connect all these mechanical facts to an electricity bill?

That's exactly what Specific Energy Consumption (SEC) gives us — a single number that captures the energy efficiency of the entire train operation. It's how railway engineers compare routes, train designs, and operating strategies. It's the number that decides whether a railway line is profitable or bankrupt.

🔵 The Intuition

Think of SEC like fuel economy for a car, but normalized for weight. A car might get 8 litres per 100 km. But that doesn't account for whether the car is empty or carrying 5 passengers with luggage.

For trains, we normalize by both distance and weight. Why? Because a 200-tonne metro train using the same energy as a 2000-tonne freight train is not equally efficient. By dividing by both tonnes and kilometers, we get a fair comparison metric:

SEC = Energy consumed per tonne of train per km of travel

Lower SEC = more efficient operation = lower electricity bill = happier accountants.

🟢 The Deep Dive

Formal Definition

Specific Energy Consumption is the electrical energy consumed from the supply, measured in kWh, to move one tonne of train weight over one kilometre during a complete operating cycle.

Specific Energy Consumption \[\text{SEC} = \frac{E_{\text{electrical,net}}}{M \times D} \quad \left[\frac{\text{kWh}}{\text{tonne} \cdot \text{km}}\right]\]

Where:

  • \(E_{\text{electrical,net}}\) = net electrical energy drawn from the supply (kWh)
  • \(M\) = total train mass (tonnes)
  • \(D\) = total distance traveled in the cycle (km)
💡
Key insight The word "net" matters here. If the train can return energy to the grid during braking (regenerative braking), that energy is subtracted from what was consumed. Without regeneration, we only count the energy drawn from the supply during motoring phases.

Typical SEC Values

System TypeSEC (kWh/tonne-km)Notes
Metro / Suburban0.04 – 0.08Frequent stops, regen helps
Intercity Rail0.02 – 0.05Long cruise phases
High-Speed Rail0.03 – 0.06Aero drag dominates
Freight0.01 – 0.03Heavy, slow, efficient
🟣 Takeaway

SEC is the universal metric for traction energy efficiency. Units: kWh/(tonne·km). It accounts for everything — the motor losses, the gearbox losses, the route profile, and whether we can recover braking energy. Master this number, and you can put a price tag on any train journey.

⚗ Energy Flow: Supply to Wheel Rims

▼
🟡 The Problem

When we say "the train uses 500 kWh," where does all that energy actually go? Not all of it reaches the wheels. Every component in the chain — converter, motor, gearbox — eats a portion. We need to trace the energy from the supply all the way to the rail to understand the losses.

🔵 The Intuition

Imagine pouring water through a series of sieves. You start with a full bucket (electrical energy from the grid), but each sieve (converter, motor, gearbox) lets some water leak out. What arrives at the bottom (the wheels) is less than what you started with. The ratio of what arrives to what you started with is the overall efficiency \(\eta\).

Going backwards — during regenerative braking — the water flows upward through the same leaky sieves. So the energy returned to the grid is also reduced by \(\eta\).

🟢 The Deep Dive
Figure 1 — Energy flow from supply to wheel rims. Each stage introduces losses, reducing the useful energy delivered.

The Power Chain

At any instant, power flows through the chain:

\[P_{\text{in}} \xrightarrow{\eta_{\text{conv}}} P_{\text{motor}} \xrightarrow{\eta_{\text{motor}}} P_{\text{mech}} \xrightarrow{\eta_{\text{gear}}} P_{\text{out}}\]

The output power at the wheel rims is:

Power at Wheel Rims \[P_{\text{out}} = F_t \times v = \eta \cdot P_{\text{in}}\]

Where \(\eta = \eta_{\text{conv}} \times \eta_{\text{motor}} \times \eta_{\text{gear}}\) is the overall efficiency.

Integrating power over time gives energy:

Energy at Wheel Rims \[E_{\text{out}} = \int F_t \cdot v \, dt = \int F_t \, ds = \sum_{i} F_i \times D_i\]

That last form is key: for each phase of the journey where force \(F_i\) is roughly constant over distance \(D_i\), the mechanical energy is simply force times distance.

Going from Mechanical to Electrical

  • Motoring (drawing from supply): \(E_{\text{in}} = \dfrac{E_{\text{out,motoring}}}{\eta}\)
  • Regenerating (returning to supply): \(E_{\text{regen}} = E_{\text{out,braking}} \times \eta\)
⚠️
Notice the asymmetry! When motoring, we divide by \(\eta\) (we need more electrical energy than mechanical). When regenerating, we multiply by \(\eta\) (we recover less electrical energy than the mechanical energy at the wheels). Losses bite us both ways!
🟣 Takeaway

Energy at the wheels = Force × Distance. To find electrical energy: divide by \(\eta\) for motoring, multiply by \(\eta\) for regeneration. The overall \(\eta\) captures converter + motor + gearbox losses.

⚙ Force Components & Their Energy

▼
🟡 The Problem

We said energy = force × distance. But the tractive force isn't constant throughout a journey! The train accelerates, climbs hills, runs at constant speed, and brakes. In each phase, different force components are active. We need to break down the total force into its components and figure out which ones contribute energy in each phase.

🔵 The Intuition

Think of the tractive force as having three "layers" stacked on top of each other:

  1. Resistance \(F_r\) — Always present, always opposes motion. Like a tax you pay on every meter you travel.
  2. Gradient \(F_g\) — Only present on slopes. Going up? It's an extra burden. Going down? It's a gift (free force in your direction).
  3. Acceleration \(F_a\) — Only present when changing speed. Speeding up costs energy. Slowing down releases energy.

The motor must produce enough force to cover all active layers. During cruising on flat track, only resistance remains. During acceleration uphill, all three stack up.

🟢 The Deep Dive

The Three Force Components

Recall from Lecture 5, the total tractive effort is:

Total Tractive Effort \[F_t = F_a + F_g + F_r\]

Where:

ComponentFormulaWhen PresentSign
\(F_a\) — Acceleration \(277.8 \, M_e \, \alpha\) (N) Acc. & Dec. only + acc, − dec
\(F_g\) — Gradient \(9.81 \, M \, G\) (N) On slopes only + uphill, − downhill
\(F_r\) — Resistance \(r \, M\) (N) Always Always + (opposes motion)

Here \(\alpha\) is in km/h/s, \(M\) in tonnes, \(M_e = k \cdot M\) is the effective mass (accounting for rotational inertia), \(G\) is the gradient (as a fraction or ‰), and \(r\) is specific resistance in N/tonne.

Energy for Each Phase

For each phase \(i\) of the duty cycle:

Phase Energy \[E_i = F_i \times D_i \quad \text{(Joules)}\]

where \(F_i\) is the net tractive force during phase \(i\) and \(D_i\) is the distance covered (in metres for Joules, or in km if force is in kN to get kJ).

Distance Calculation from Speed-Time

Remember, distance = area under the speed-time curve:

  • Acceleration (0 to \(V_m\)): \(D_1 = \frac{1}{2} V_m t_1\)
  • Constant speed: \(D = V_m \times t\)
  • Deceleration (\(V\) to 0): \(D = \frac{1}{2} V \cdot t\)

(Convert km/h to m/s by dividing by 3.6, and time in seconds, to get distance in metres.)

Sign Convention: Motoring vs. Generating

🟡 Why This Matters

Getting the signs wrong is the single biggest source of errors in SEC calculations. Let's nail this down once and for all. When is the motor consuming energy, and when is it producing energy?

🟢 The Deep Dive
➕ Positive Force / Positive Energy — MOTORING

The motor drives the wheels. Electrical energy flows from supply to motor. The motor is a consumer.

This happens during: acceleration, climbing, cruising against resistance.

➖ Negative Force / Negative Energy — GENERATING

The load drives the motor. Electrical energy flows from motor back toward supply. The motor acts as a generator.

This happens in two situations:

  1. Steep downhill: Gravity force exceeds resistance → net force pushes the train forward without the motor. The motor must actually resist to maintain speed, acting as a generator.
  2. Deceleration (braking): The train's kinetic energy drives the wheels. The motor converts this into electrical energy.

What Happens to Regenerated Energy?

When the motor generates, the energy can be:

  • Returned to the grid (regenerative braking) — the most efficient option, used in modern inverter-fed systems
  • Stored in batteries or supercapacitors — used in some metro and hybrid systems
  • Dissipated in resistors (dynamic/rheostatic braking) — energy wasted as heat, but at least it provides controlled braking
🤔
Challenge for you: A train is going downhill on a gentle gradient. The resistance force is 30 N/tonne and the gravity component is 25 N/tonne. Is the motor in motoring or generating mode? Think about it before reading on!

Answer: Motoring! The gravity force (25 N/t) doesn't fully overcome resistance (30 N/t). The motor still needs to supply 5 N/tonne to maintain speed. The gradient only reduces the motor's burden — it doesn't eliminate it.
🟣 Takeaway

+E = motoring (supply feeds motor). −E = generating (motor feeds supply). A phase is "generating" only when the total net force is negative — meaning gravity or deceleration overpowers resistance. Always check the net force, not individual components!

📊 Duty Cycle Energy Analysis

▼
🟡 The Problem

A real train journey isn't just "go fast, then stop." It's a complex sequence: accelerate, cruise on flat, climb a hill, descend a hill, coast, and brake. Each phase has different forces, different distances, and different energy requirements. We need a systematic method to analyze the entire duty cycle.

🔵 The Intuition

Here's our game plan, nice and simple:

  1. Draw the speed-time diagram for the whole journey
  2. Identify each phase (acceleration, cruising, coasting, braking...)
  3. For each phase, calculate the distance (area under v-t curve)
  4. For each phase, calculate the net force (add up the active components)
  5. Energy for each phase = force × distance
  6. Classify each phase as motoring (+) or generating (−)
  7. Sum up, account for efficiency, and calculate SEC

It's bookkeeping. Careful, methodical bookkeeping. Let's set up the diagrams that make this systematic.

Systematic phase-by-phase analysis: identify the phase → calculate distance (area under v-t) → calculate net force (sum of active components) → compute energy (F × D) → classify as motoring or generating. This is the backbone of every SEC calculation.

⚡ Regenerative Braking

▼
🟡 The Problem

Every time a train brakes, an enormous amount of kinetic energy is destroyed. A 500-tonne train at 150 km/h carries about 430 MJ of kinetic energy — enough to power 40 homes for a day. Just throwing that away as heat in brake pads is an engineering crime. Can we recapture it?

🔵 The Intuition

Imagine a ball rolling downhill into a valley. To stop it at the bottom, you could put a wall in the way (friction brakes — energy becomes heat and noise) or you could use the ball's momentum to wind up a spring (regenerative braking — energy stored for later use).

In electric traction, the "spring" is the power grid itself. When the motor runs as a generator, it pumps electrical energy back into the supply line. Other trains on the same grid can use this energy immediately. It's beautifully efficient — but not free. The generator conversion has losses (multiply by \(\eta\)), so we don't get everything back.

🟢 The Deep Dive
Figure 5 — Regenerative braking energy flow. Kinetic energy passes through the motor (as generator) and converter back to the supply, with losses at each stage.

Energy Recovery Calculation

The energy available at the wheels during a braking phase is:

\[E_{\text{braking}} = |F_{\text{net}}| \times D_{\text{braking}}\]

The electrical energy actually returned to the supply is:

Regenerated Energy \[E_{\text{regen}} = |E_{\text{braking}}| \times \eta\]

Where \(\eta\) is the overall regeneration efficiency (motor + gearbox + converter).

🔎
Why can't we recover 100%?

Even with a perfect regeneration system, there are fundamental losses: copper losses in the generator windings, iron losses in the core, friction in the gearbox, switching losses in the converter. A typical overall regeneration efficiency is 70-85%. That means for every 100 kWh of braking energy, only 70-85 kWh makes it back to the grid.

🟣 Takeaway

Regenerative braking recovers \(E_{\text{regen}} = |E_{\text{braking}}| \times \eta\). Not all braking energy can be recovered — losses occur at every stage. But even partial recovery significantly reduces the electricity bill, especially for metro systems with frequent stops.

📈 The Complete SEC Calculation

▼
🟡 The Problem

We've assembled all the pieces. Now let's put them together into a complete, step-by-step recipe that you can apply to any duty cycle. This is the method you'll use in every exam problem and every real engineering calculation.

🟢 The Complete Method
Step 1 — Draw the speed-time diagram

Identify every phase: acceleration, cruising, gradient changes, coasting, braking. Mark the times and speeds.

Step 2 — Calculate distances

For each phase, find the distance from the area under the speed-time curve. Remember to convert units (km/h × seconds ÷ 3.6 = metres, or km/h × hours = km).

Step 3 — Calculate forces for each phase

Add up the active force components for each phase:

  • Acceleration: \(F = 277.8 \, M_e \, \alpha + 9.81 \, M \, G + r \, M\)
  • Constant speed: \(F = 9.81 \, M \, G + r \, M\) (no acceleration force)
  • Coasting: no electrical force needed
  • Braking: \(F = -277.8 \, M_e \, \beta + r \, M\) (deceleration opposes motion)
Step 4 — Calculate energy for each phase

\(E_i = F_i \times D_i\) (in watt-seconds if F in N and D in m)

Step 5 — Separate motoring and braking

\(E_{\text{motoring}} = \sum (\text{all positive } E_i)\)

\(E_{\text{braking}} = \sum (|\text{all negative } E_i|)\)

Step 6 — Calculate electrical energy

Electrical energy drawn from supply during motoring:

\[E_{\text{in}} = \frac{E_{\text{motoring}}}{\eta}\]

Electrical energy returned during regeneration:

\[E_{\text{regen}} = E_{\text{braking}} \times \eta\]

Net electrical energy consumed:

Net Electrical Energy \[E_{\text{net}} = E_{\text{in}} - E_{\text{regen}} = \frac{E_{\text{motoring}}}{\eta} - E_{\text{braking}} \times \eta\]

If there is no regeneration (e.g., dynamic braking only), then \(E_{\text{net}} = E_{\text{in}} = E_{\text{motoring}} / \eta\).

Step 7 — Calculate SEC

Convert joules to kWh (\(1 \text{ kWh} = 3.6 \times 10^6 \text{ J}\)):

Specific Energy Consumption \[\boxed{\text{SEC} = \frac{E_{\text{net}} \;(\text{Wh})}{M \;(\text{t}) \times D \;(\text{km}) \times 1000} \quad \text{kWh/(t} \cdot \text{km)}}\]

Or equivalently: \(\text{SEC} = \dfrac{E_{\text{net}} \;(\text{J})}{M \;(\text{t}) \times D \;(\text{km}) \times 3.6 \times 10^6}\)

⚠️
Unit conversion trap!

The most common error in SEC problems is unit confusion. Keep track carefully:

  • Forces in Newtons (N), distances in metres (m) → energy in Joules (J) = Watt-seconds (Ws)
  • 1 kWh = 3,600,000 J = 3.6 × 106 J
  • 1 Wh = 3,600 J
  • Speed conversion: 1 km/h = 1/3.6 m/s
🟣 Takeaway

Seven steps: Diagram → Distances → Forces → Energies → Separate ± → Efficiency correction → SEC. The net electrical energy is \(E_{\text{motoring}}/\eta - E_{\text{braking}} \times \eta\). Then divide by mass × distance. That's the complete recipe.

🔌 Induction Motor with Inverter in Traction

▼
🟡 The Problem

Historically, DC series motors dominated traction. But they can't easily regenerate energy. Modern systems use induction motors with inverters. Why did the entire industry switch, and how does this affect our energy calculations?

🔵 The Intuition

The DC series motor is like a one-way street — power flows from supply to wheels, period. The induction motor with an inverter is like a two-way highway. By changing the inverter's switching pattern, we can:

  • Drive the motor forward (motoring) — consuming energy
  • Drive the motor backward as a generator — returning energy
  • Control speed smoothly at any frequency
  • Operate in all four quadrants (forward/reverse, motoring/braking)

This four-quadrant capability is what makes regenerative braking practical and efficient in modern electric trains.

🟢 The Deep Dive

Key Advantages for SEC

FeatureDC Series MotorIM + Inverter
Regenerative braking Difficult / not practical Built-in, efficient
Speed control Resistor-based (lossy) Variable frequency (efficient)
Four-quadrant operation Needs complex switching Natural capability
Typical SEC reduction Baseline 20–40% lower
Maintenance Brushes, commutator wear Nearly maintenance-free

Impact on SEC Calculation

With IM + inverter systems, we can always assume regeneration is possible (unless stated otherwise). The SEC calculation uses the full formula:

\[\text{SEC} = \frac{E_{\text{motoring}}/\eta - E_{\text{braking}} \times \eta}{M \times D}\]

The regeneration term \(E_{\text{braking}} \times \eta\) directly reduces the electricity bill. In metro systems with stops every 1-2 km, this can save 20-40% of energy compared to non-regenerative systems.

🟣 Takeaway

Modern IM + inverter traction enables efficient regenerative braking, typically reducing SEC by 20-40%. The inverter provides four-quadrant operation — smooth speed control in both directions with seamless transitions between motoring and generating.

📝 Problem 7 — Uphill Journey SEC

▼
📚 Problem Statement ▼

A 500-tonne train climbs an up-gradient with \(G = 10\) (i.e., 10‰). The duty cycle has four phases:

  1. Acceleration at \(\alpha = 1.5\) km/h/s for 100 seconds
  2. Constant speed for 60 minutes
  3. Coasting for 3 minutes
  4. Dynamic braking at \(\beta = 2\) km/h/s to rest

Given: \(r = 30\) N/tonne, \(M_e = 1.1M\), \(\eta = 85\%\)

Find: (a) Average speed   (b) Specific energy consumption

✏️ Detailed Solution ▼
Step 1 — Basic Parameters

\(M = 500\) t, \(M_e = 1.1 \times 500 = 550\) t, \(G = 10\), \(r = 30\) N/t, \(\eta = 0.85\)

Step 2 — Maximum Speed

\(V_m = \alpha \times t_1 = 1.5 \times 100 = 150\) km/h = \(150/3.6 = 41.67\) m/s

Step 3 — Phase Times and Distances

Phase 1 (Acceleration): \(t_1 = 100\) s

\(D_1 = \frac{1}{2} \times 41.67 \times 100 = 2083.3\) m

Phase 2 (Constant speed): \(t_2 = 60 \times 60 = 3600\) s

\(D_2 = 41.67 \times 3600 = 150{,}000\) m = 150 km

Phase 3 (Coasting): \(t_3 = 3 \times 60 = 180\) s

During coasting, speed decreases from \(V_m\). Let's approximate the average speed during coasting. The deceleration during coasting is caused by resistance + gradient. The coasting deceleration:

\(a_{\text{coast}} = \frac{(F_g + F_r)}{277.8 \times M_e} = \frac{(9.81 \times 500 \times 10) + (30 \times 500)}{277.8 \times 550}\)

\(= \frac{49050 + 15000}{152790} = \frac{64050}{152790} = 0.419\) km/h/s

Speed drop = \(0.419 \times 180 = 75.5\) km/h

Speed at end of coasting = \(150 - 75.5 = 74.5\) km/h = 20.7 m/s

Average speed during coasting = \(\frac{150 + 74.5}{2} = 112.25\) km/h = 31.18 m/s

\(D_3 = 31.18 \times 180 = 5612\) m

Phase 4 (Braking): \(\beta = 2\) km/h/s, initial speed = 74.5 km/h

\(t_4 = 74.5 / 2 = 37.25\) s

\(D_4 = \frac{1}{2} \times (74.5/3.6) \times 37.25 = \frac{1}{2} \times 20.69 \times 37.25 = 385.4\) m

Step 4 — Total Distance and Average Speed

\(D_{\text{total}} = 2083.3 + 150000 + 5612 + 385.4 = 158{,}081\) m = 158.08 km

Total time = \(100 + 3600 + 180 + 37.25 = 3917.25\) s = 1.088 hours

(a) Average speed = \(\frac{158.08}{1.088} = \mathbf{145.3}\) km/h

Step 5 — Forces for Each Phase

Phase 1 (Acceleration up-gradient):

\(F_1 = 277.8 \times M_e \times \alpha + 9.81 \times M \times G + r \times M\)

\(= 277.8 \times 550 \times 1.5 + 9.81 \times 500 \times 10 + 30 \times 500\)

\(= 229{,}185 + 49{,}050 + 15{,}000 = 293{,}235\) N

Phase 2 (Constant speed up-gradient):

\(F_2 = 9.81 \times 500 \times 10 + 30 \times 500 = 49{,}050 + 15{,}000 = 64{,}050\) N

Phase 3 (Coasting): No electrical energy (supply disconnected)

Phase 4 (Dynamic braking): Energy dissipated in resistors (not returned to grid)

Step 6 — Energy Calculation

Phase 1: \(E_1 = F_1 \times D_1 = 293{,}235 \times 2{,}083.3 = 610.98 \times 10^6\) J

Phase 2: \(E_2 = F_2 \times D_2 = 64{,}050 \times 150{,}000 = 9{,}607.5 \times 10^6\) J

Phase 3: \(E_3 = 0\) (no electrical energy)

Phase 4: \(E_4 = 0\) for grid (dynamic braking — energy wasted in resistors)

Total motoring energy: \(E_{\text{motor}} = (610.98 + 9607.5) \times 10^6 = 10{,}218.5 \times 10^6\) J

Step 7 — Electrical Energy and SEC

Electrical energy from supply:

\(E_{\text{in}} = \frac{E_{\text{motor}}}{\eta} = \frac{10{,}218.5 \times 10^6}{0.85} = 12{,}021.8 \times 10^6\) J

Convert to kWh: \(E_{\text{in}} = \frac{12{,}021.8 \times 10^6}{3.6 \times 10^6} = 3{,}339.4\) kWh

(b) SEC = \(\frac{3339.4}{500 \times 158.08} = \frac{3339.4}{79040} = \mathbf{0.0423}\) kWh/(t·km)

💡
Observation: Note how Phase 2 (constant speed uphill) dominates the energy consumption. It accounts for about 94% of the total energy! This makes sense — the train climbs for a full hour. The acceleration phase, despite having a much larger force, covers a relatively short distance.

📝 Problem 9 — Regenerative Braking Energy

▼
📚 Problem Statement ▼

A 500-tonne train on a down-gradient of \(G = 8\) (8‰) decelerates from 150 km/h to 100 km/h in 50 seconds using regenerative braking.

Given: \(r = 40\) N/tonne, \(M_e = 1.1M\), \(\eta_{\text{regen}} = 80\%\)

Find: Energy returned to the supply line

✏️ Detailed Solution ▼
Step 1 — Deceleration Rate

\(\beta = \frac{V_1 - V_2}{t} = \frac{150 - 100}{50} = 1\) km/h/s

Step 2 — Forces Acting

Deceleration force (opposes motion, acts as braking):

\(F_d = 277.8 \times M_e \times \beta = 277.8 \times 550 \times 1 = 152{,}790\) N

Gravity force (downhill — helps motion, so it acts in the direction of travel):

\(F_g = 9.81 \times M \times G = 9.81 \times 500 \times 8 = 39{,}240\) N (in direction of motion)

Resistance force (always opposes motion):

\(F_r = r \times M = 40 \times 500 = 20{,}000\) N (opposing motion)

Step 3 — Net Force at Wheel Rims

Let's think carefully about the forces. Taking the direction of motion as positive:

  • Gravity: \(+39{,}240\) N (pushing train forward downhill)
  • Resistance: \(-20{,}000\) N (opposing motion)
  • Braking force needed: We need net deceleration of 1 km/h/s

The total braking force required at the wheels is:

\(F_{\text{brake}} = F_d - F_g + F_r = 152{,}790 - 39{,}240 + 20{,}000 = 133{,}550\) N

Wait — let me reconsider. The deceleration force \(F_d = 277.8 M_e \beta\) is the total inertial force needed. The motor must provide a braking force that, combined with resistance and accounting for gravity, produces this deceleration.

Motor braking force = \(F_d + F_r - F_g = 152{,}790 + 20{,}000 - 39{,}240 = 133{,}550\) N

This is the mechanical energy the motor absorbs at the wheels (acting as generator).

Step 4 — Distance During Braking

Average speed = \(\frac{150 + 100}{2} = 125\) km/h = 34.72 m/s

\(D = 34.72 \times 50 = 1{,}736.1\) m

Step 5 — Energy at Wheel Rims

\(E_{\text{wheels}} = F_{\text{brake}} \times D = 133{,}550 \times 1{,}736.1 = 231.8 \times 10^6\) J

Step 6 — Energy Returned to Supply

\(E_{\text{regen}} = E_{\text{wheels}} \times \eta = 231.8 \times 10^6 \times 0.80 = 185.4 \times 10^6\) J

Convert to kWh:

\(E_{\text{regen}} = \frac{185.4 \times 10^6}{3.6 \times 10^6} = \mathbf{51.5 \text{ kWh}}\)

🤔
Think about this:

Notice the gravity force reduces the motor's braking effort. On a downhill, gravity is already helping to push the train forward, so the motor doesn't need to absorb as much energy to slow it down. If the gradient were steeper, even less energy would be available for regeneration. And if the gradient were steep enough, you might need the motor to provide a driving force (motoring) even while decelerating!

📝 Problem 10 — Complex Multi-Phase SEC

▼
📚 Problem Statement ▼

A 500-tonne train completes a duty cycle with five phases:

  1. Acceleration at \(\alpha = 3\) km/h/s on level track for 1 minute
  2. Constant speed for 30 minutes on up-gradient \(G = 10\)
  3. Constant speed for 30 minutes on down-gradient \(G = 6\)
  4. Coasting for 5 minutes on level track
  5. Regenerative braking at \(\beta = 3\) km/h/s on level track to stop

Given: \(r = 30\) N/tonne, \(M_e = 1.1M\), \(\eta = 85\%\)

Find: Specific Energy Consumption

✏️ Detailed Solution ▼
Step 1 — Parameters

\(M = 500\) t, \(M_e = 550\) t, \(r = 30\) N/t, \(\eta = 0.85\)

Step 2 — Maximum Speed

\(V_m = \alpha \times t_1 = 3 \times 60 = 180\) km/h = 50 m/s

Step 3 — Phase-by-Phase Analysis

Phase 1: Acceleration on Level Track

\(t_1 = 60\) s, \(D_1 = \frac{1}{2} \times 50 \times 60 = 1{,}500\) m

\(F_1 = 277.8 \times 550 \times 3 + 0 + 30 \times 500 = 458{,}370 + 15{,}000 = 473{,}370\) N

\(E_1 = 473{,}370 \times 1{,}500 = 710.06 \times 10^6\) J (+Motoring)

Phase 2: Constant Speed, Up-Gradient G=10

\(t_2 = 30 \times 60 = 1{,}800\) s, \(D_2 = 50 \times 1{,}800 = 90{,}000\) m

\(F_2 = 9.81 \times 500 \times 10 + 30 \times 500 = 49{,}050 + 15{,}000 = 64{,}050\) N

\(E_2 = 64{,}050 \times 90{,}000 = 5{,}764.5 \times 10^6\) J (+Motoring)

Phase 3: Constant Speed, Down-Gradient G=6

\(t_3 = 1{,}800\) s, \(D_3 = 50 \times 1{,}800 = 90{,}000\) m

\(F_3 = -9.81 \times 500 \times 6 + 30 \times 500 = -29{,}430 + 15{,}000 = -14{,}430\) N

The force is negative! Gravity exceeds resistance — the motor acts as a generator.

\(E_3 = -14{,}430 \times 90{,}000 = -1{,}298.7 \times 10^6\) J (−Generating)

Phase 4: Coasting on Level Track

\(t_4 = 5 \times 60 = 300\) s. Supply disconnected.

Coasting deceleration = \(\frac{F_r}{277.8 \times M_e} = \frac{15{,}000}{277.8 \times 550} = 0.0982\) km/h/s

Speed drop = \(0.0982 \times 300 = 29.4\) km/h

Speed at end of coasting = \(180 - 29.4 = 150.6\) km/h = 41.83 m/s

Average speed = \(\frac{50 + 41.83}{2} = 45.92\) m/s

\(D_4 = 45.92 \times 300 = 13{,}775\) m

\(E_4 = 0\) (no electrical energy exchange)

Phase 5: Regenerative Braking on Level Track

Initial speed = 150.6 km/h = 41.83 m/s, \(\beta = 3\) km/h/s

\(t_5 = 150.6 / 3 = 50.2\) s

\(D_5 = \frac{1}{2} \times 41.83 \times 50.2 = 1{,}049.9\) m

Braking force: \(F_d = 277.8 \times 550 \times 3 = 458{,}370\) N (deceleration)

Motor braking force = \(F_d - F_r = 458{,}370 - 15{,}000 = 443{,}370\) N

(Resistance helps the braking, so motor absorbs less)

\(E_5 = -443{,}370 \times 1{,}049.9 = -465.5 \times 10^6\) J (−Generating)

Step 4 — Energy Summation

Motoring energy (at wheels):

\(E_{\text{motor}} = E_1 + E_2 = (710.06 + 5764.5) \times 10^6 = 6{,}474.56 \times 10^6\) J

Generating energy (at wheels):

\(E_{\text{gen}} = |E_3| + |E_5| = (1298.7 + 465.5) \times 10^6 = 1{,}764.2 \times 10^6\) J

Step 5 — Electrical Energy

Electrical energy consumed (motoring):

\(E_{\text{in}} = \frac{6{,}474.56 \times 10^6}{0.85} = 7{,}617.1 \times 10^6\) J = \(\frac{7617.1 \times 10^6}{3.6 \times 10^6} = 2{,}115.9\) kWh

Electrical energy returned (regeneration):

\(E_{\text{regen}} = 1{,}764.2 \times 10^6 \times 0.85 = 1{,}499.6 \times 10^6\) J = 416.5 kWh

Net electrical energy:

\(E_{\text{net}} = 2{,}115.9 - 416.5 = 1{,}699.4\) kWh

Step 6 — SEC Calculation

Total distance: \(D = 1500 + 90000 + 90000 + 13775 + 1049.9 = 196{,}325\) m = 196.33 km

\[\boxed{\text{SEC} = \frac{1699.4}{500 \times 196.33} = \frac{1699.4}{98{,}165} = \mathbf{0.0173} \text{ kWh/(t\(\cdot\)km)}}\]

💡
Analysis of the result:

This SEC of 0.0173 kWh/(t·km) is relatively low. Why? Two reasons:

  1. The down-gradient section (Phase 3) generates significant energy that is returned to the grid
  2. Regenerative braking (Phase 5) recovers additional energy

Together, regeneration reduced the energy bill by about \(\frac{416.5}{2115.9} \approx 20\%\). In a metro system with more frequent stops, this percentage would be even higher!

📚 Complete Summary & Takeaways

▼
📝 Complete Summary

1. SEC is the Universal Traction Energy Metric

SEC = kWh/(tonne·km). It normalizes energy consumption by both train weight and distance, enabling fair comparisons between different trains, routes, and operating strategies.

2. Energy at Wheels = Force × Distance

Break the journey into phases. For each phase: identify the active force components (\(F_a, F_g, F_r\)), calculate the distance, and compute energy. The sign of the net force determines whether the phase is motoring (+) or generating (−).

3. Efficiency Works Both Ways

Motoring: \(E_{\text{electrical}} = E_{\text{mechanical}} / \eta\) (we need MORE electrical than mechanical). Regenerating: \(E_{\text{returned}} = E_{\text{mechanical}} \times \eta\) (we recover LESS than the mechanical energy). Losses always work against us.

4. Regenerative Braking Saves 20-40%

Modern IM + inverter systems enable practical regenerative braking. The energy equation becomes: \(E_{\text{net}} = E_{\text{motoring}}/\eta - E_{\text{braking}} \times \eta\).

5. The 7-Step Method

Diagram → Distances → Forces → Phase Energies → Separate ± → Efficiency → SEC. Follow this systematically and you'll never go wrong.

Key Equations

\(\text{SEC} = \frac{E_{\text{net}}}{M \times D}\)

\(E_i = F_i \times D_i\)

\(E_{\text{net}} = \frac{E_{\text{mot}}}{\eta} - E_{\text{brk}} \times \eta\)

Common Pitfalls

Unit confusion (J vs kWh, m vs km). Forgetting \(M_e\) for acceleration. Wrong sign for downhill gradient. Forgetting coasting has zero electrical energy.
