Lecture 10

DC Motor Braking

Understanding dynamic, counter-current, and regenerative braking techniques for DC motors — how to safely decelerate machines and recover energy.

1. Introduction — Why Braking Matters

▾
The Problem / The Why

Elevators carrying passengers, electric trains approaching stations, cranes lowering heavy loads — all of these need to decelerate in a controlled manner. A motor that can only speed up is only half useful. The kinetic energy stored in a spinning motor and its load (\(\frac{1}{2}J\omega^2\)) doesn't disappear on its own — it must be converted into another form: heat in a resistor, energy returned to the grid, or friction heat in brake pads. The method we choose has massive implications for efficiency, safety, and equipment life.

The Intuition

Think of a bicycle going downhill. You have three choices: (1) Squeeze the brake pads — friction converts kinetic energy to heat (mechanical braking). (2) Use a dynamo/generator attached to the wheel to power your lights — you slow down by converting motion into electrical energy (dynamic braking). (3) If you had a magical battery, you could charge it while going downhill and use that energy later (regenerative braking). Electric drives give us all three options, and the last one is the most elegant.

Deep Dive — Energy Perspective

During deceleration, the motor's kinetic energy must go somewhere:

Kinetic Energy in Rotating System
\[ E_{\text{kinetic}} = \frac{1}{2} J \omega^2 \]

where \(J\) is the total moment of inertia (motor + load) and \(\omega\) is the angular speed. The braking method determines where this energy ends up:

Braking MethodEnergy DestinationEfficiency
MechanicalHeat in brake pads0% recovery
Dynamic (rheostatic)Heat in braking resistor0% recovery
Counter-current (plugging)Heat in resistor + supply energy wastedWorst — wastes extra energy
RegenerativeReturned to power supplyBest — energy recovered
Takeaway

Braking is not optional in real drives — it's a safety and performance requirement. The three electrical braking methods (dynamic, plugging, regenerative) each have different trade-offs in terms of energy efficiency, braking torque, and circuit complexity. Regenerative braking is the gold standard because it recovers energy, but not all situations allow it.

2. Mechanical Braking

▾
The Problem / The Why

Before electrical braking methods were developed, mechanical braking was the only option. Even today, mechanical brakes remain essential as a backup and for holding the motor at zero speed — something no electrical method can do reliably on its own.

The Intuition

Exactly like your car's disc brakes. Brake pads press against a rotating drum or disc, converting kinetic energy into heat through friction. Simple, reliable, but the energy is completely wasted. The brake pads also wear down over time, requiring maintenance.

Deep Dive

In mechanical braking:

  • Kinetic energy \(\rightarrow\) friction \(\rightarrow\) heat (entirely dissipated)
  • Brake pads wear out and need periodic replacement
  • Brake dust is generated (maintenance concern)
  • Braking torque is roughly constant regardless of speed
  • Can hold the load at zero speed (essential for elevators, hoists)

In practice, mechanical brakes are always used as the final stopping mechanism and as a parking brake. Electrical braking handles the bulk of deceleration (especially from high speeds), and mechanical braking takes over for the final stop and holding.

Takeaway

Mechanical braking is simple and provides holding torque at standstill, but wastes all kinetic energy as heat and requires maintenance. In modern drives, it is typically combined with electrical braking: electrical methods handle high-speed deceleration, mechanical brakes handle the final stop and holding.

3. Electrical (Self) Braking — Overview

▾
The Problem / The Why

Mechanical brakes wear out and waste energy. Can we use the motor itself to brake? Yes — by making the motor act as a generator, it converts kinetic energy into electrical energy, which is then either dissipated in a resistor or returned to the supply. This is "self braking" or "electrical braking."

Deep Dive — Three Methods

There are three distinct methods of electrical braking for DC motors:

MethodPrincipleEnergy Fate
Dynamic (Rheostatic)Motor disconnected from supply, armature connected to braking resistanceDissipated as heat in \(R_b\)
Counter-Current (Plugging)Armature voltage polarity reversed while motor still runningBoth KE and supply energy wasted as heat
RegenerativeMotor operates as generator, returning power to supplyReturned to the power source

Availability by Motor Type

Motor TypeDynamicCounter-CurrentRegenerative
Shunt / Sep. ExcitedYesYesYes
SeriesYesYesNo (impractical)
CompoundYesYesLimited

Notice that regenerative braking is practical only for separately excited (or shunt) motors, because we need independent control of the field to ensure \(E > V\). Series motors cannot easily achieve this because the field current depends on the armature current.

Takeaway

Electrical braking uses the motor as a generator to oppose rotation. Dynamic braking wastes energy in a resistor but is simple. Plugging is fast but wasteful. Regenerative braking recovers energy and is the most efficient, but requires the back-EMF to exceed the supply voltage and a source that can absorb power.

4. Dynamic Braking — Shunt / Sep. Excited Motor

▾
The Problem / The Why

You're running a conveyor belt and need to stop it. Simply cutting power lets the motor coast to a stop — which could take a long time with a heavy load. Dynamic braking provides a controlled, predictable deceleration by making the motor work against itself.

The Intuition

Imagine disconnecting a fan from the wall outlet and immediately connecting a light bulb across its terminals. The spinning fan (now acting as a generator) powers the light bulb, converting its kinetic energy into light and heat. The fan slows down because it's now doing work (generating electricity). Replace the light bulb with a resistor, and that's dynamic braking. The bigger the resistor, the less current flows, and the gentler the braking. The smaller the resistor, the harder the braking.

Figure 1: Dynamic braking circuit for a shunt/separately excited DC motor. The armature is disconnected from supply and connected to braking resistance Rb. Field excitation is maintained.

Deep Dive — Equations and Torque-Speed

When the motor is disconnected from the supply and connected to \(R_b\):

  • The back-EMF \(E = K\phi \cdot \omega\) drives current through the circuit
  • Field current is maintained independently (same direction, same magnitude)
  • The motor now acts as a generator
Braking Current
\[ I_a = \frac{E}{R_a + R_b} = \frac{K\phi \cdot \omega}{R_a + R_b} \]
Braking Torque
\[ T_{\text{brake}} = K\phi \cdot I_a = \frac{(K\phi)^2 \cdot \omega}{R_a + R_b} \]

Key observations:

  • Torque is proportional to speed: \(T \propto \omega\). This is a straight line through the origin on the T-\(\omega\) plot.
  • At high speed: Strong braking torque (good — this is when you need it most)
  • At zero speed: \(T = 0\). The motor cannot stop itself. You always need a mechanical brake for the final stop.
  • Smaller \(R_b\): Steeper slope — stronger braking at any given speed. But limited by maximum current rating.
  • Larger \(R_b\): Gentler braking. The slope becomes shallower.

Figure 2: Dynamic braking torque-speed characteristics. All lines pass through the origin — braking torque is zero at standstill.

Deep Dive — Power Dissipation

The power dissipated in the braking resistor during dynamic braking is:

Power Dissipated in Rb
\[ P = I_a^2 (R_a + R_b) = \frac{(K\phi)^2 \omega^2}{R_a + R_b} \]

This power decreases as the motor slows down (proportional to \(\omega^2\)). The total energy dissipated during braking from speed \(\omega_1\) to zero equals the initial kinetic energy:

Total Energy Dissipated
\[ E_{\text{dissipated}} = \frac{1}{2} J \omega_1^2 \]

The braking resistor must be rated to handle this energy over the braking duration. For frequent braking (like an elevator), the continuous power rating of \(R_b\) is a critical design parameter. Overheating the braking resistor is a real engineering concern.

Time to decelerate

From Newton's second law for rotation: \(J \frac{d\omega}{dt} = -T_{\text{brake}} = -\frac{(K\phi)^2}{R_a + R_b} \omega\)

This gives an exponential decay:

Speed During Dynamic Braking
\[ \omega(t) = \omega_0 \cdot e^{-t/\tau}, \quad \text{where } \tau = \frac{J(R_a + R_b)}{(K\phi)^2} \]

The time constant \(\tau\) tells us how quickly the motor decelerates. Smaller \(R_b\) means smaller \(\tau\) and faster braking. But the motor never quite reaches zero — it decays exponentially, approaching zero asymptotically.

Figure 3: Speed vs. time during dynamic braking. The speed decays exponentially with time constant τ. A mechanical brake is needed for the final stop.

Takeaway

Dynamic braking provides strong braking at high speeds but weakens as the motor slows down, reaching zero at standstill. It is simple and effective for deceleration, but always requires a mechanical brake for the final stop. The braking intensity is controlled by the value of \(R_b\). Speed decays exponentially with time constant \(\tau = J(R_a + R_b)/(K\phi)^2\).

5. Counter-Current Braking (Plugging) — Shunt Motor

▾
The Problem / The Why

What if you need to stop a motor very quickly? Dynamic braking gets weaker as the motor slows down. Sometimes you need aggressive, rapid deceleration — like an emergency stop on a machine tool or quickly reversing a rolling mill. Plugging provides the most aggressive electrical braking, but at a significant energy cost.

The Intuition

Imagine you're rowing a boat forward. Plugging is like suddenly rowing backward while the boat is still moving forward. Both the boat's forward momentum and your backward rowing effort create enormous stress. The boat decelerates brutally fast. In electrical terms, we reverse the armature voltage while the motor is still spinning — now both the supply voltage and the back-EMF push current in the same direction, creating a huge current and rapid deceleration.

Figure 3: Counter-current braking (plugging) for a shunt motor. The armature voltage is reversed. Both Va and E now drive current in the same direction.

Deep Dive — Analysis

When we reverse the armature voltage polarity while the motor is still spinning:

Plugging Current
\[ I_a = \frac{V_a + E}{R_a + R_{\text{ext}}} = \frac{V_a + K\phi \cdot \omega}{R_a + R_{\text{ext}}} \]

Notice the plus sign — \(V_a\) and \(E\) now add instead of opposing each other. This is why plugging current is much larger than normal running current.

Why external resistance is essential

Without \(R_{\text{ext}}\), the plugging current would be:

\[ I_a = \frac{V_a + E}{R_a} \]

At rated speed, \(E \approx V_a\), so \(I_a \approx 2V_a/R_a\) — which could easily be 40–50 times the rated current! The external resistance limits this to a safe value.

What happens at zero speed?

When the motor reaches zero speed, \(E = 0\), but the supply is still connected (reversed). The current is now \(I_a = V_a/(R_a + R_{\text{ext}})\), which will accelerate the motor in the reverse direction. Therefore:

Critical Warning

You must disconnect the supply at zero speed! If you forget, the motor will reverse and keep accelerating in the opposite direction. In applications where reversal is desired (like a rolling mill), this is actually useful. But for braking to a stop, the supply must be cut at \(\omega = 0\).

Energy analysis

Plugging is the most wasteful braking method:

  • The motor's kinetic energy is dissipated as heat in \(R_a + R_{\text{ext}}\)
  • The supply also provides additional energy (because \(V_a\) is doing work driving current through the circuit)
  • Total energy wasted = kinetic energy + energy drawn from supply

Figure 4: Torque-speed during plugging. The motor decelerates from operating speed through zero. Supply must be disconnected at zero speed to prevent reversal.

Deep Dive — Numerical Sense Check

Let's put numbers on why plugging is so extreme. Consider a motor with \(V_a = 220\) V, \(R_a = 0.5\;\Omega\), running at rated speed where \(E = 210\) V (typical: most of \(V_a\) appears as back-EMF).

Normal running current:

Motoring Current
\[ I_{a,\text{motor}} = \frac{V_a - E}{R_a} = \frac{220 - 210}{0.5} = 20 \text{ A} \]

Plugging current (without external resistance):

Plugging Current (no Rext)
\[ I_{a,\text{plug}} = \frac{V_a + E}{R_a} = \frac{220 + 210}{0.5} = 860 \text{ A} \]

That's 43 times the normal running current! The motor would be destroyed instantly. With \(R_{\text{ext}} = 4\;\Omega\):

Plugging Current (with Rext)
\[ I_{a,\text{plug}} = \frac{220 + 210}{0.5 + 4} = \frac{430}{4.5} = 95.6 \text{ A} \]

Now the current is about 4.8 times rated — high but manageable for a short braking transient.

Figure 5: Energy efficiency comparison of the three electrical braking methods.

Takeaway

Plugging provides very fast braking by reversing the armature voltage, but it is the most energy-wasteful method — it dissipates both the motor's kinetic energy and additional energy drawn from the supply. An external resistance is mandatory to limit the enormous plugging current. The supply must be disconnected at zero speed to prevent unintended reversal.

6. Dynamic Braking — Series Motor

▾
The Problem / The Why

Series motors are used in traction (trains, trams, electric vehicles) where high starting torque is essential. These motors also need braking — especially on downhill grades. But the series motor has a critical difference from the shunt motor: its field winding is in series with the armature. When we disconnect from the supply for dynamic braking, we need to be very careful about field current direction.

The Intuition

In a shunt motor, the field has its own independent supply — easy. In a series motor, the field current is the armature current. When the motor becomes a generator during dynamic braking, the current direction through the armature reverses (it's now flowing out of the generator). If the field is still in series with the armature, the field also reverses — and that means the generated EMF reverses too, which kills the braking effect. It's like a feedback loop that cancels itself out.

Figure 5: Dynamic braking for a series motor. The field winding connections must be reversed to maintain the correct EMF direction for braking.

Deep Dive — The Reversal Problem

Here is the critical issue with series motor dynamic braking:

  • During motoring: Current flows through field and armature in the same direction, producing flux \(\phi\) and back-EMF \(E = K\phi\omega\) that opposes the supply.
  • During generator mode (if you just connect \(R_b\)): Current direction reverses (flowing out of the "generator"). But the field is in series — so the field current also reverses, \(\phi\) reverses, and therefore \(E\) reverses too. The reversed \(E\) now tries to drive current in the original direction, contradicting our assumption. The result: no sustained braking.

The fix: Reverse either the field winding connections or the armature connections (but not both). This ensures that when the current reverses during generator operation, the field direction is maintained correctly, preserving the EMF polarity needed for braking.

Once the wiring is correct, the analysis is similar to the shunt motor dynamic braking case, except that \(\phi\) now varies with \(I_a\) (since it's a series motor), making the torque-speed relationship nonlinear.

Deep Dive — Why It Cancels Out

Let's trace the logic carefully. During motoring, let's say:

  • Current \(I_a\) flows in the positive direction through both field and armature
  • This creates flux \(\phi\) in the positive direction
  • The back-EMF is \(E = K\phi\omega\) in a direction that opposes \(V_a\) (opposing current)

Now, for dynamic braking, we disconnect the supply and connect \(R_b\). The motor acts as a generator. The EMF \(E\) tries to drive current through the circuit. But which direction?

Without reversing field connections (wrong):

  • EMF drives current in the direction determined by \(\phi\) and rotation
  • This new current passes through the series field, which now creates flux in the opposite direction
  • The reversed flux means the EMF reverses too
  • The reversed EMF tries to drive current the other way, which reverses the flux again...
  • Result: the system cannot settle into a stable state. The residual magnetism quickly dies out and braking ceases.

With field connections reversed (correct):

  • The EMF drives current through the circuit in one direction
  • Due to the reversed field connections, this current creates flux that supports the original direction
  • Self-reinforcing: the flux sustains the EMF, which sustains the current, which sustains the flux
  • Result: stable braking with the motor acting as a self-excited generator
Takeaway

Dynamic braking of a series motor requires reversing either the field or armature connections — not both. Without this reversal, the field and EMF both reverse, canceling out the braking effect entirely. This precaution is unique to series motors and is critical for traction applications.

7. Counter-Current Braking (Plugging) — Series Motor

▾
The Problem / The Why

For series motors in traction and industrial applications, sometimes you need rapid emergency braking, just as with shunt motors. But plugging a series motor has a subtle twist compared to the shunt case that catches many students off guard.

The Intuition

In a shunt motor, reversing the supply polarity reverses \(V_a\) but the field stays the same (independent supply). In a series motor, if you simply reverse the supply terminals, both the armature and the field currents reverse — because they are in series. Two reversals cancel out: the torque direction stays the same! That is: the motor keeps driving forward instead of braking. You haven't achieved anything.

Deep Dive

For a series motor, the torque is:

\[ T = K \phi \cdot I_a \]

Since \(\phi \propto I_a\) (series field), the sign of torque depends on \(I_a^2\) — which is always positive regardless of current direction. Reversing the supply polarity reverses both \(I_a\) and \(\phi\) simultaneously, so \(T\) remains in the same direction.

The correct approach: To plug a series motor, you must reverse either the field winding connections or the armature connections — not just the supply polarity. This ensures only one of the two (field or armature current) changes direction, producing the reverse torque needed for braking.

This principle applies whether the motor is fed from DC or AC (universal motor). It's the physical connections, not the supply polarity, that must change.

As with shunt motor plugging, an external resistance is essential to limit the enormous plugging current, and the supply must be disconnected at zero speed to prevent unintended reversal.

Takeaway

Simply reversing the supply on a series motor does NOT produce braking, because both field and armature reverse together, keeping torque in the same direction. You must reverse either the field or armature connections (not both, and not the supply alone). This applies to both DC series and AC universal motors.

8. Braking of DC Compound Motors

▾
The Problem / The Why

Compound motors have both shunt and series field windings, combining characteristics of both motor types. Braking them requires understanding how each field winding behaves during the different braking modes.

Deep Dive — Dynamic Braking

For dynamic braking of a compound motor:

  • Disconnect the armature from the supply and connect to braking resistance \(R_b\)
  • The shunt field remains connected to its supply (maintaining constant excitation)
  • The series field may need to be reversed, depending on whether the current direction through it during generation supports or opposes the shunt field
  • The goal is to maintain net flux in the correct direction for braking
Deep Dive — Counter-Current Braking

For plugging a compound motor:

  • The armature supply is reversed
  • The series field reverses automatically (it carries armature current, which reverses)
  • The shunt field remains unchanged (independent supply)
  • The net MMF (magnetomotive force) changes because the series contribution flips relative to the shunt contribution
  • In a cumulative compound motor: during normal operation, series and shunt MMFs add. During plugging, they oppose — the motor effectively becomes differentially compounded during braking
  • In a differential compound motor: the opposite occurs — it becomes cumulatively compounded during braking

This change in compounding affects the braking torque-speed characteristic and must be accounted for in the design of the braking circuit.

Deep Dive — Summary Table
Braking MethodShunt FieldSeries FieldNet Effect
Dynamic Stays connected to supply May need reversal Ensure net flux supports braking EMF
Plugging (cumulative) Unchanged Reverses automatically Becomes differentially compounded
Plugging (differential) Unchanged Reverses automatically Becomes cumulatively compounded

The key insight is that any braking operation that reverses armature current will also reverse the series field contribution. Since the shunt field is independent, only the series contribution flips. This fundamentally changes the motor's magnetic circuit behavior during braking, and the control system must account for it.

Takeaway

Compound motor braking requires careful attention to both field windings. The shunt field stays constant, but the series field reverses with armature current during plugging, changing the effective compounding type. Dynamic braking may require deliberate reversal of the series field to maintain the correct net flux direction.

9. Regenerative Braking of DC Motors

▾
The Problem / The Why

Dynamic braking and plugging both waste the motor's kinetic energy as heat. In an elevator that goes up and down all day, or a train that accelerates and decelerates constantly, this wasted energy adds up to enormous operating costs. What if we could send that energy back to the power grid? That's regenerative braking — and it's the reason modern electric vehicles can achieve significantly better energy efficiency than their internal combustion counterparts.

The Intuition

Think of regenerative braking like this: normally, the power grid pushes energy into the motor to make it spin. During regenerative braking, the motor pushes energy back to the grid. It's like a reversible pump — instead of consuming electricity to spin, the motor generates electricity from its rotation and feeds it back. For this to work, the motor must generate a voltage higher than the supply, so current flows "backward" into the source.

Deep Dive — Two Essential Conditions

Regenerative braking requires two conditions to be met simultaneously:

Condition 1: EMF Exceeds Supply Voltage
\[ E > V \]

When \(E > V\), the current reverses direction: \(I_a = (E - V)/R_a\), flowing out of the motor and into the supply. The motor is now a generator.

Condition 2: Source Can Absorb Power
\[ \text{Power source must accept returned energy (or other loads must consume it)} \]

If the supply cannot absorb energy (e.g., a simple diode rectifier), regeneration fails even if \(E > V\). The supply must be bidirectional (like a fully controlled converter) or other loads must be present on the same bus to absorb the regenerated power.

How to achieve E > V

Since \(E = K\phi \cdot \omega\), we can make \(E > V\) by:

  • Increasing speed (if the load drives the motor above its no-load speed — overhauling load)
  • Increasing field flux \(\phi\) (strengthening the field)
  • Decreasing \(V\) using a converter (reducing the supply voltage below the back-EMF)

Figure 6: During regenerative braking, E > V causes current to reverse, sending power back to the supply. The motor operates as a generator.

Deep Dive — Motoring vs. Generating Conventions

Understanding the sign conventions is essential for solving braking problems correctly:

During motoring (normal operation):

Motoring Convention
\[ V_a = E + I_a R_a \quad \Rightarrow \quad I_a = \frac{V_a - E}{R_a} > 0 \]

Current flows into the motor. Power is consumed: \(P = V_a I_a > 0\). The supply pushes current through the armature against the back-EMF.

During regenerative braking (generating):

Generating Convention
\[ E = V_a + I_a R_a \quad \Rightarrow \quad I_a = \frac{E - V_a}{R_a} > 0 \text{ (flows out)} \]

Current flows out of the motor into the supply. Power is returned: \(P = E I_a\) is generated and \(V_a I_a\) is absorbed by the supply. The back-EMF pushes current back into the source.

The transition happens when \(E\) crosses above \(V_a\). At exactly \(E = V_a\), the current is zero — the motor is at its no-load speed. Above this speed (or below this voltage), regeneration begins.

Figure 8: Four-quadrant operation of a DC motor showing where each braking mode operates in the voltage-current plane.

Regenerative Braking — Separately Excited Motor

Deep Dive — Speed Ranges

Below rated speed (using converter to reduce V)

In modern drive systems with a controllable converter, we can achieve regeneration at any speed:

  • Maintain rated field flux (\(\phi = \phi_{\text{rated}}\))
  • Reduce the converter output voltage \(V\) below the motor's back-EMF \(E = K\phi\omega\)
  • Current reverses: \(I_a = (E - V)/R_a\), flowing back into the supply
  • Braking torque: \(T = K\phi \cdot I_a\) (opposing rotation)

This is the most common method in modern variable-speed drives. The converter smoothly reduces \(V\) to control the braking torque.

Above rated speed (field weakening region)

When operating above base speed with field weakening:

  • Terminal voltage \(V\) is already at its rated maximum
  • The motor speed is high enough that \(E = K\phi\omega > V\) naturally
  • Must weaken the field to limit the braking current and torque
  • The back-EMF must not exceed the voltage rating of the converter

In traction (fixed supply voltage)

When the source voltage is fixed (as on a trolley bus or some trains):

  • Regeneration is only possible when the motor speed is high enough that \(E > V\)
  • This corresponds to speeds above the no-load speed for the given supply voltage
  • For speeds above rated, field weakening is necessary to prevent excessive current
  • At low speeds, dynamic braking must take over since \(E < V\)

Figure 7: Operating regions for a separately excited DC motor with regenerative braking. Below base speed, the converter reduces V. Above base speed, E > V naturally.

Practical Examples

The Intuition — Real-World Scenarios

Elevator going down with heavy load

The heavy load (passengers + elevator car) tries to accelerate the motor beyond its set speed. The motor's back-EMF rises above the supply voltage, and current reverses — the motor becomes a generator, feeding energy back to the grid. The elevator descends at controlled speed while actually returning energy. Some of this energy powers other elevators going up in the same building.

Train going downhill

Gravity tries to accelerate the train. The traction motors become generators, converting the train's potential energy into electrical energy returned to the overhead catenary. Other trains on the same line use this energy. On a long mountain descent, a train can return a significant fraction of the energy it consumed on the way up.

Electric vehicle decelerating

When the driver lifts off the accelerator or presses the brake, the drive reduces the voltage to the motor. The motor's back-EMF exceeds the (now-reduced) supply voltage, and regeneration begins. The kinetic energy charges the battery. This is why EVs achieve better city-driving efficiency than highway — more opportunities for regeneration in stop-and-go traffic.

Deep Dive — Composite Braking in Traction

In real traction systems, a combination of braking methods is used across the speed range:

Speed RangeBraking MethodReason
High speed (> base)RegenerativeE > V naturally, energy recovery
Medium speedRegenerative (with converter) or DynamicConverter reduces V, or switch to dynamic when regen not viable
Low speedDynamicE too low for regeneration
Near zeroMechanicalElectrical braking torque too weak

This combination — regenerative at high speed, dynamic at medium-to-low speed, and mechanical for final stop — is called composite braking or blended braking. It maximizes energy recovery while ensuring safe, complete stops.

Takeaway

Regenerative braking is the most energy-efficient braking method, returning kinetic energy to the power source. It requires \(E > V\) and a bidirectional power path. In modern drives, the converter controls \(V\) to enable regeneration at any speed. In traction, composite braking combines regen (high speed), dynamic (low speed), and mechanical (final stop) for optimal performance.

Design Decision Guide

When choosing a braking method for a drive application, consider these factors:

  • Frequency of braking: If the motor brakes often (elevator, train), regenerative braking pays for the extra converter cost through energy savings.
  • Speed of stop required: If an emergency stop is needed in the shortest time, plugging provides the fastest deceleration (at the cost of energy waste and equipment stress).
  • Energy cost: For applications with high energy prices or environmental concerns, regenerative braking is strongly preferred.
  • Supply type: If the supply cannot absorb power (e.g., diode rectifier), regenerative braking is not possible without additional hardware (like a braking chopper with dump resistor).
  • Motor type: Series motors effectively eliminate regenerative braking as an option.

Takeaway

Each braking method occupies a different region of the torque-speed plane. In practice, modern drives seamlessly blend regenerative braking (when possible) with dynamic braking (when regen is impractical) and mechanical braking (for final stop), optimizing for energy recovery while ensuring safe, reliable deceleration under all conditions.

Problem 10 — Regenerative Braking

A 220 V, 960 rpm, 90 A separately excited DC motor has \(R_a = 0.06\;\Omega\). An overhauling load applies a torque of \(T = 300\) N·m. Find the speed at which the motor operates under regenerative braking.

Step 1: Find \(K\phi\) from rated conditions

Rated speed: \(\omega_r = \frac{2\pi \times 960}{60} = 100.53\) rad/s

Rated back-EMF: \(E_r = V_a - I_a R_a = 220 - 90(0.06) = 220 - 5.4 = 214.6\) V

\(K\phi = \frac{E_r}{\omega_r} = \frac{214.6}{100.53} = 2.135\) V·s/rad

Step 2: Find the armature current from the load torque

During regenerative braking, the motor acts as a generator:

\(T = K\phi \cdot I_a\)

\(I_a = \frac{T}{K\phi} = \frac{300}{2.135} = 140.5\) A

Step 3: Apply the generator voltage equation

In regenerative braking (generator mode), \(E > V\):

\(E = V_a + I_a R_a = 220 + 140.5 \times 0.06 = 220 + 8.43 = 228.43\) V

(Note: In generator mode, \(V_a = E - I_a R_a\), which rearranges to \(E = V_a + I_a R_a\) since current flows out.)

Step 4: Find the speed

\(\omega = \frac{E}{K\phi} = \frac{228.43}{2.135} = 107.0\) rad/s

\(N = \frac{60 \times 107.0}{2\pi} = \mathbf{1021.5 \text{ rpm}}\)

Verification

The speed (1021.5 rpm) is above the rated speed (960 rpm), which makes sense — the overhauling load drives the motor faster than normal, pushing \(E\) above \(V\) and enabling regeneration. The current (140.5 A) is above rated (90 A), so in practice this condition might need current limiting.

Problem 11 — Dynamic Braking

The same motor as Problem 10 (220 V, 960 rpm, 90 A, \(R_a = 0.06\;\Omega\)) is used with an overhauling load that provides \(T = 300\) N·m. Dynamic braking is used to hold the motor at 600 rpm. Find the required external braking resistance \(R_b\).

Step 1: Use \(K\phi\) from Problem 10

\(K\phi = 2.135\) V·s/rad

Step 2: Find armature current from load torque

\(I_a = \frac{T}{K\phi} = \frac{300}{2.135} = 140.5\) A

Step 3: Find back-EMF at 600 rpm

\(\omega = \frac{2\pi \times 600}{60} = 62.83\) rad/s

\(E = K\phi \cdot \omega = 2.135 \times 62.83 = 134.1\) V

Step 4: Find the braking resistance

In dynamic braking, the armature is disconnected from supply and connected to \(R_b\). The back-EMF drives current through \(R_a + R_b\):

\(I_a = \frac{E}{R_a + R_b}\)

\(R_a + R_b = \frac{E}{I_a} = \frac{134.1}{140.5} = 0.954\;\Omega\)

\(R_b = 0.954 - 0.06 = \mathbf{0.894\;\Omega}\)

Note

The overhauling load maintains the motor at a constant 600 rpm by providing continuous driving torque. The dynamic braking circuit absorbs this energy in \(R_b\), holding the speed constant. The power dissipated in the braking resistor is \(P = I_a^2(R_a + R_b) = 140.5^2 \times 0.954 = 18.8\) kW — a substantial amount of heat that the resistor must handle continuously.

Lecture Summary

▾
Big Picture

In this lecture, we covered the complete landscape of DC motor braking:

  1. Mechanical braking: Simple friction-based — wastes energy as heat, but essential for holding at standstill.
  2. Dynamic (rheostatic) braking: Motor acts as generator into a resistor. Torque proportional to speed — strong at high speed, zero at standstill. Cannot stop the motor alone.
  3. Counter-current (plugging): Armature voltage reversed — most aggressive braking but wastes the most energy. Current \(I_a = (V_a + E)/(R_a + R_{\text{ext}})\). Must disconnect at zero speed.
  4. Regenerative braking: Energy returned to supply. Requires \(E > V\) and bidirectional power path. Most efficient method — standard in modern drives and electric vehicles.
  5. Series motor precautions: Must reverse field OR armature connections for both dynamic braking and plugging. Simply reversing the supply does nothing for a series motor.
  6. Composite braking: Real systems combine regen (high speed) + dynamic (medium speed) + mechanical (final stop) for optimal performance.

These braking techniques are directly tested in Sheet 3 problems. Master the equations and understand the energy flow direction for each method.
