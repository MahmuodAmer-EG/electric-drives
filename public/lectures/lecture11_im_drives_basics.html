Induction Motor Drives — Basics & Stator Voltage Control

Understanding induction motor types, equivalent circuit analysis, torque-speed characteristics, and stator voltage control as the simplest IM speed control method.

1. Induction Motor Types

▾
The Problem / The Why

We've spent several lectures on DC drives, and by now you know how beautifully controllable DC motors are — independent knobs for flux and torque. So why bother with induction motors? Because over 90% of all motors in the world are induction motors. They're cheap, rugged, maintenance-free (no brushes, no commutator), and they work directly from the AC grid. The challenge is that controlling their speed is much harder than for DC motors. Understanding the machine itself is the critical first step.

The Intuition

Think of an induction motor like a barbecue rotisserie driven by a rotating magnetic field. The stator (the stationary outer part) creates a magnetic field that spins at "synchronous speed." The rotor (the inner part) tries to follow, but it can never quite catch up — it always lags behind. That lag is called slip. If the rotor actually caught up to the rotating field, there would be no relative motion, no induced voltage in the rotor, no rotor current, and no torque. The slip is essential — it's the engine of the induction motor.

Deep Dive — IM Classifications

Induction motors come in three broad families:

TypeRotor ConstructionKey Feature
Squirrel Cage IM Rotor bars short-circuited by end rings — like a hamster cage Simplest, most rugged, lowest cost, 80%+ of all IMs. No external rotor access.
Wound Rotor IM Three-phase winding on rotor, brought out through slip rings External rotor resistance can be added. Better starting torque, speed control via rotor circuit. More expensive and needs maintenance (brushes).
Linear IM "Unrolled" rotary motor — flat stator produces traveling field Produces linear force instead of rotational torque. Used in maglev trains, conveyor systems, and sliding doors.

For drives, the squirrel cage motor is the dominant choice because of its low cost and robustness. The wound rotor motor is used in specialized applications where rotor circuit access provides a control advantage.

Figure 1: Cross-section of a squirrel cage induction motor. The rotor bars (orange) are short-circuited by end rings. The stator carries the three-phase winding (colored slots).

Takeaway

Induction motors are the workhorses of industry — simple, rugged, and cheap. The squirrel cage type dominates. The fundamental operating principle is electromagnetic induction: the stator's rotating field induces currents in the rotor, which interact with the field to produce torque. The rotor must always slip behind the field for this to work.

2. Special Squirrel Cage Designs

▾
The Problem / The Why

A standard squirrel cage motor has a fundamental trade-off: if you make the rotor resistance low, you get good running efficiency but terrible starting torque (and dangerously high starting current). If you make the resistance high, starting is great but running efficiency is poor. Engineers have developed clever rotor bar designs to get both — good starting and good running — out of a single motor.

a) Low Slip IM (s = 0.02 – 0.07)

The Intuition

This motor is designed for nearly constant speed. Low rotor resistance means minimal slip at rated load — the rotor almost keeps up with the rotating field. Think of it as a "tightly coupled" follower. The downside? At starting (when slip = 1), the low resistance means very high starting current and low starting torque. Speed control via voltage is limited to a narrow range.

  • Rotor resistance: Low \(R'_r\)
  • Operating slip: 2–7% at rated load
  • Starting torque: Poor (low \(R'_r\) gives low torque at \(s=1\))
  • Starting current: High (low impedance at standstill)
  • Efficiency: High at rated speed (low rotor copper losses = \(s \times P_g\))
  • Speed control range: Narrow via stator voltage
  • Best for: Constant-speed applications — pumps, compressors, fans at fixed speed

b) High Slip IM (s = 0.1 – 0.4)

The Intuition

The opposite philosophy: high rotor resistance makes the motor a strong starter with low starting current, but it operates at a much larger slip at rated load. It's like a "loosely coupled" follower — the rotor lags far behind the field. The high slip means more rotor copper loss (\(P_{cu} = s \cdot P_g\)), so efficiency is poor. But the wider speed range under voltage control and natural softness of the torque-speed curve make it ideal for fan and pump loads where torque is proportional to speed squared.

  • Rotor resistance: High \(R'_r\)
  • Operating slip: 10–40% at rated load
  • Starting torque: High (high \(R'_r\) maximizes torque near \(s=1\))
  • Starting current: Low
  • Efficiency: Poor at rated speed (high rotor losses)
  • Speed control range: Wide via stator voltage
  • Best for: Fan loads, blowers, centrifugal pumps (where \(T \propto \omega^2\))
  • Torque motor: A special high-slip design that can produce torque continuously at standstill — used in tape tensioners, winders, and positioning systems

c) Deep Bar IM

The Intuition

Here's an elegant trick: instead of choosing between high and low rotor resistance, make bars that automatically change their effective resistance depending on the situation. Deep bar rotors use narrow, deep rotor bars. At starting (slip = 1), the rotor frequency is high (equal to stator frequency), and the skin effect crowds the current to the top of the bar — only a fraction of the conductor cross-section carries current, so the effective resistance is high. At running (low slip = low rotor frequency), the skin effect disappears, current distributes uniformly across the full bar depth, and the effective resistance drops. The motor gets automatic "high resistance at start, low resistance at run."

Figure 2: Deep bar rotor — skin effect at starting concentrates current at the top (high effective resistance). At running, current distributes uniformly (low resistance).

d) Double Cage IM

The Intuition

The double cage motor takes the deep bar idea further by using two separate squirrel cage windings in the rotor. The upper cage (closer to the air gap) has high resistance and low leakage reactance. The lower cage (deeper in the rotor) has low resistance but high leakage reactance (because more flux leaks around it). At starting, the rotor frequency is high, so the lower cage has very high impedance (\(X = 2\pi f_{rotor} L\)) and most current flows through the upper cage (high R → good starting torque). At running, the rotor frequency is very low, the reactances become negligible, and current naturally flows through the low-resistance lower cage (good efficiency). Two cages, each optimized for its job.

Figure 3: Double cage rotor structure. The upper cage (high resistance) dominates at starting; the lower cage (low resistance) dominates at running.

Takeaway

The fundamental squirrel cage design trade-off — starting performance vs. running efficiency — is solved by clever rotor bar geometry. Deep bars use skin effect for automatic resistance variation. Double cages use two physically separate windings optimized for different operating conditions. Both achieve good starting torque without sacrificing running efficiency.

3. Per-Phase Equivalent Circuit

▾
The Problem / The Why

To analyze and control an induction motor drive, we need a mathematical model — just as we had the simple \(V_a = E_g + I_a R_a\) for the DC motor. The induction motor is more complex because energy transfers magnetically across an air gap (like a transformer). The per-phase equivalent circuit is our "blueprint" — it captures all the electromagnetic relationships in one circuit diagram that we can analyze with standard circuit theory.

The Intuition

Think of the IM as a transformer with a moving secondary. The stator is the primary winding. The rotor is the secondary winding — but it's spinning! The "trick" of the equivalent circuit is to refer everything to the stator side (just like we refer transformer secondary quantities to the primary). The rotor resistance appears as \(R'_r/s\), where \(s\) is the slip. This single term captures both the rotor copper loss and the mechanical power output. When the motor runs at low slip, \(R'_r/s\) is large (like a lightly loaded transformer), and when it's stalled (\(s=1\)), \(R'_r/s = R'_r\) (like a short-circuited secondary).

Deep Dive — Key Relationships
Synchronous Speed
\[ N_s = \frac{120f}{P} \quad \text{(rpm)}, \qquad \omega_s = \frac{4\pi f}{P} \quad \text{(rad/s)} \]

where \(f\) is the supply frequency (Hz) and \(P\) is the number of poles.

Slip
\[ s = \frac{N_s - N_r}{N_s} = \frac{\omega_s - \omega_r}{\omega_s} \]

Slip ranges from 0 (rotor at synchronous speed — no torque) to 1 (rotor at standstill).

Figure 4: Exact per-phase equivalent circuit of the induction motor (referred to stator side). The rotor resistance appears as R'r/s.

Deep Dive — Approximate Equivalent Circuit

For most drive analysis, we use the approximate equivalent circuit where the magnetizing branch \(jX_m\) is moved to the input terminals. This simplifies calculations enormously because the stator and rotor impedances are now in a simple series loop:

RMS Rotor Current (Approximate)
\[ I'_r = \frac{V_s}{\sqrt{\left(R_s + \dfrac{R'_r}{s}\right)^2 + (X_s + X'_r)^2}} \]

This is the key equation for torque analysis. Everything flows from knowing \(I'_r\).

Takeaway

The per-phase equivalent circuit converts the complex electromagnetic coupling of a 3-phase rotating machine into a simple circuit problem. The slip \(s\) in the denominator of \(R'_r/s\) is what makes the circuit "speed-dependent" — and that's what makes induction motor control both interesting and challenging.

4. Torque & Performance Equations

▾
The Problem / The Why

In DC drives, torque was simple: \(T = K\phi I_a\). In the induction motor, the torque-speed relationship is nonlinear and much richer. Understanding the torque equation — where maximum torque occurs, how starting torque depends on rotor resistance, and what the torque-speed curve looks like — is essential for choosing the right motor and the right control strategy.

a) Power Flow and Torque Derivation

The Intuition

Picture power flowing through the motor like water through a pipe with two leaks. Electrical power enters the stator, crosses the air gap as air gap power \(P_g\), then splits: a fraction \(s\) is lost as rotor copper loss (heat in the rotor), and the remaining fraction \((1-s)\) becomes useful mechanical power. The lower the slip, the more efficient the conversion. At synchronous speed (\(s=0\)), there would be zero rotor loss — but also zero torque. At standstill (\(s=1\)), all air gap power is wasted as heat — this is why stalled motors burn up!

Deep Dive — Power Relationships
Air Gap Power (per phase × 3)
\[ P_g = 3|I'_r|^2 \cdot \frac{R'_r}{s} \]
Rotor Copper Loss
\[ P_{cu,r} = 3|I'_r|^2 \cdot R'_r = s \cdot P_g \]
Developed Mechanical Power
\[ P_m = P_g - P_{cu,r} = P_g(1-s) = 3|I'_r|^2 \cdot R'_r \cdot \frac{(1-s)}{s} \]

The key insight: rotor copper loss is always \(s\) times the air gap power. This means efficiency is fundamentally limited by slip — a motor running at 5% slip wastes at least 5% of its air gap power as rotor heat.

Developed Torque
\[ T_d = \frac{P_g}{\omega_s} = \frac{3V_s^2 \cdot R'_r}{\omega_s \cdot s \cdot \left[\left(R_s + \dfrac{R'_r}{s}\right)^2 + (X_s + X'_r)^2\right]} \]

This is the complete torque equation for the induction motor. It depends on the slip \(s\), the supply voltage \(V_s\), the motor parameters (\(R_s, R'_r, X_s, X'_r\)), and the synchronous speed \(\omega_s\).

Figure 5: Power flow in an induction motor. Rotor copper loss = s × Pg, mechanical power = (1−s) × Pg.

b) Maximum Torque and Critical Slip

Deep Dive — Finding the Peak

To find the slip at which torque is maximum, we differentiate the torque equation with respect to \(s\) and set it to zero. The result:

Slip for Maximum Torque
\[ s_m = \frac{R'_r}{\sqrt{R_s^2 + (X_s + X'_r)^2}} \]

If stator resistance \(R_s\) is neglected (common approximation):

Slip for Maximum Torque (Rs neglected)
\[ s_m \approx \frac{R'_r}{X_s + X'_r} \]

Substituting back gives the maximum (breakdown) torque:

Maximum (Breakdown) Torque
\[ T_{mm} = \frac{3V_s^2}{2\omega_s \left(R_s + \sqrt{R_s^2 + (X_s + X'_r)^2}\right)} \]

With \(R_s\) neglected:

Maximum Torque (Rs neglected)
\[ T_{mm} = \frac{3V_s^2}{2\omega_s(X_s + X'_r)} \]

Critical observation: The maximum torque is independent of rotor resistance \(R'_r\)! Changing \(R'_r\) only moves the slip \(s_m\) at which the peak occurs, not the peak value itself. This is one of the most important results in induction motor theory.

Deep Dive — Starting Torque

At starting (\(s = 1\)):

Starting Torque
\[ T_{st} = \frac{3V_s^2 \cdot R'_r}{\omega_s \left[(R_s + R'_r)^2 + (X_s + X'_r)^2\right]} \]
Starting Current
\[ I_{st} = \frac{V_s}{\sqrt{(R_s + R'_r)^2 + (X_s + X'_r)^2}} \]

Notice that starting torque does depend on \(R'_r\). Increasing rotor resistance increases starting torque (up to a point) while also reducing starting current. This is exactly why wound rotor motors with external resistance have superior starting performance.

Starting Torque to Max Torque Ratio
\[ \frac{T_{st}}{T_{mm}} = \frac{2 s_m}{1 + s_m^2} \]

c) Normalized Torque Expression

Deep Dive — Universal Torque Curve

When we normalize the torque by the maximum torque (neglecting \(R_s\)), we get a beautifully simple expression:

Normalized Torque
\[ \frac{T_d}{T_{mm}} = \frac{2 \cdot s \cdot s_m}{s^2 + s_m^2} \]

This equation has some useful properties:

  • At \(s = s_m\): \(T_d/T_{mm} = 1\) (confirms the peak)
  • At \(s \ll s_m\): \(T_d \approx \frac{2T_{mm}}{s_m} \cdot s\) (torque is approximately linear with slip — this is the normal operating region)
  • At \(s \gg s_m\): \(T_d \approx \frac{2T_{mm} \cdot s_m}{s}\) (torque drops inversely with slip)

Linear approximation near synchronous speed:

Linear Region Approximation
\[ T_d \approx \frac{2T_{mm}}{s_m} \cdot s \]

This means the motor acts like a "spring" near synchronous speed: small changes in load cause proportional changes in slip. This is the stable operating region.

Figure 6: Effect of rotor resistance on the torque-speed characteristic. Increasing R'r shifts the peak to lower speeds but does NOT change Tmm.

Takeaway

The torque-speed curve of an IM has a peak (breakdown torque) that depends on \(V_s^2\) and the leakage reactance, but is independent of rotor resistance. Rotor resistance only shifts where the peak occurs. Near synchronous speed, the motor behaves linearly (torque proportional to slip). The region between synchronous speed and the peak torque slip is the stable operating region — if the load increases, the motor slows down just enough to produce more torque. Beyond the peak, the motor is unstable and will stall.

5. Squirrel-Cage Design Trade-offs

▾
The Problem / The Why

We now have the mathematical framework to understand exactly why squirrel cage motor design involves painful trade-offs — and why deep bar and double cage designs exist.

Deep Dive — The Fundamental Contradiction
PropertyLow R'rHigh R'r
Rated slipSmall (2–7%)Large (10–40%)
Running efficiencyHigh (low rotor losses)Poor (high rotor losses)
Starting torqueLowHigh
Starting currentHigh (dangerous)Low (safe)
Speed control range (voltage)NarrowWide
Breakdown torque slip\(s_m\) small (peak near sync speed)\(s_m\) large (peak at lower speed)

The resolution: Deep bar and double cage designs provide the "best of both worlds" — high effective resistance at starting (when rotor frequency is high) and low effective resistance at running (when rotor frequency is low). This is possible because impedance is frequency-dependent, and the rotor frequency is \(s \cdot f\).

Takeaway

You can't have good starting AND good running with a simple cage — you need clever bar geometry (deep bar or double cage) to exploit frequency-dependent impedance. This is one of the most elegant engineering solutions in all of electrical machines.

6. Speed Control Methods — Overview

▾
The Problem / The Why

Unlike the DC motor where speed control was straightforward (change \(V_a\) or \(\phi\)), the induction motor's speed depends on slip, supply frequency, and voltage in a nonlinear way. We have several "knobs" to turn, each with different trade-offs, cost, and complexity. Understanding the landscape of methods is essential before diving into each one.

Deep Dive — The Four Methods

From the synchronous speed and slip relationships:

\(N_r = N_s(1-s) = \frac{120f}{P}(1-s)\)

We can control speed by changing:

#MethodMotor TypeWhat ChangesComplexity
1 Stator voltage control Squirrel cage Slip changes (via torque reduction) Simple, low cost
2 Frequency control (V/f) Squirrel cage Synchronous speed changes Moderate (inverter needed)
3 Rotor resistance control Wound rotor Slip for given torque changes Simple but lossy
4 Slip power recovery Wound rotor Rotor energy recovered, not wasted Complex, efficient

In this lecture, we focus on Method 1: Stator Voltage Control. Frequency control (the most important method for modern drives) will be covered in Lecture 12.

Takeaway

IM speed can be controlled by changing voltage, frequency, or rotor resistance. Each method suits different applications. Voltage control is the simplest but most limited; frequency control is the most versatile and dominates modern practice.

7. Stator Voltage Control

▾
The Problem / The Why

What if we could control an IM's speed with just a simple AC voltage controller — no expensive inverter, no wound rotor, no external resistors? We can, using stator voltage control. The idea is simple: since torque is proportional to \(V_s^2\), reducing the voltage reduces the torque. If the load requires less torque at lower speed (like a fan), the motor settles at a lower speed. It's the cheapest possible speed control method for an IM. But it has serious limitations.

The Intuition

Imagine our torque-speed curve as a mountain. At full voltage, the mountain is tall (high \(T_{mm}\)). When we reduce voltage by a factor \(b\) (where \(b < 1\)), the mountain shrinks — its height drops to \(b^2\) of the original (because \(T \propto V^2\)). The peak position (at \(s_m\)) doesn't move — only the height changes. Now, if the load is a fan (torque increases with speed squared), the load curve intersects the shrunk mountain at a lower speed. That's our new operating point. The problem: if the mountain shrinks too much, there's no intersection at all, and the motor stalls.

Deep Dive — Mathematical Analysis

When the terminal voltage is reduced by a factor \(b\) (so the new voltage is \(bV_s\), where \(0 < b \le 1\)):

Torque at Reduced Voltage
\[ T_d = \frac{3R'_r \cdot (bV_s)^2}{\omega_s \cdot s \cdot \left[\left(R_s + \dfrac{R'_r}{s}\right)^2 + (X_s + X'_r)^2\right]} = b^2 \cdot T_{d,\text{rated}} \]

Key consequences:

  • Maximum torque: \(T_{mm,new} = b^2 \cdot T_{mm,rated}\) (drops quadratically!)
  • Slip for max torque: \(s_m\) stays the same (independent of voltage)
  • Speed range: Limited — especially for low-slip motors where the curve is steep
  • Best match: High-slip motors with fan/pump loads (\(T \propto \omega^2\))

Figure 7: Stator voltage control with a fan load. Reducing voltage (b < 1) shrinks the torque curve by b². The fan load curve intersects at progressively lower speeds.

Deep Dive — Why High-Slip Motors?

Consider two motors with the same breakdown torque but different slips:

  • Low-slip motor (\(s_m = 0.05\)): The torque curve is very steep near synchronous speed. Reducing voltage even a little causes the operating point to plunge to a much lower speed — or worse, the curve may not intersect the load at all. Useful speed range: maybe 5–10%.
  • High-slip motor (\(s_m = 0.3\)): The torque curve is much "softer" and spreads over a wide speed range. Reducing voltage causes a gradual speed reduction with many possible stable intersections. Useful speed range: 30–50% or more with fan loads.

This is why stator voltage control is always paired with high-slip motors and fan-type loads in practice.

Implementation of Stator Voltage Control

Deep Dive — Power Electronic Implementation

The reduced voltage is achieved using power electronic circuits:

TopologyDescriptionProsCons
3-Phase AC Voltage Controller 6 thyristors (anti-parallel pairs), one pair per phase. Controls RMS voltage by delaying firing angle. Simple, low cost, no DC link needed High harmonics, low input power factor at reduced speed, motor heating from harmonics
Voltage-Fed Inverter Rectifier + DC link + inverter. Voltage adjusted via DC link or PWM. Better waveform quality, can also control frequency More expensive, typically used for V/f control instead
PWM AC Controller IGBTs switch at high frequency to control effective voltage. Low harmonics, good power factor Higher cost than thyristor-based

Figure 8: Three-phase AC voltage controller for IM speed control. Anti-parallel thyristor pairs in each phase control the RMS voltage to the motor.

Deep Dive — Practical Considerations
  • Application domain: Low-power fans, blowers, centrifugal pumps (up to a few kW)
  • Harmonics: The phase-controlled thyristors produce significant current harmonics, causing additional motor heating and torque pulsations
  • Power factor: The input power factor decreases as the firing angle increases (similar to DC drive converters)
  • Efficiency: At reduced speeds, the motor operates at high slip, so rotor copper losses are significant (\(P_{cu} = s \cdot P_g\))
  • Motor derating: Due to harmonic heating, the motor must be derated (used below its nameplate power)
  • Soft starting: Even if speed control isn't needed, AC voltage controllers are widely used as soft starters — they ramp up the voltage gradually at starting to limit inrush current
Takeaway

Stator voltage control is the simplest and cheapest IM speed control method. It works by reducing the voltage (and thus the torque, by \(b^2\)) to create new operating points at lower speeds. It's practical only with high-slip motors and fan/pump loads. The main implementations are thyristor-based AC voltage controllers. The method is limited in range and efficiency, but unbeatable in simplicity for the right application. For wider speed ranges and constant-torque loads, we need frequency control (next lecture).

8. Worked Example

▾
Worked Example 1

Problem: A 3-phase, 4-pole, 50 Hz induction motor has the following per-phase equivalent circuit parameters (referred to stator): \(R_s = 0.5\;\Omega\), \(R'_r = 0.4\;\Omega\), \(X_s + X'_r = 4\;\Omega\). The motor is supplied at rated voltage \(V_s = 230\) V (phase). Find:

  1. The synchronous speed
  2. The slip for maximum torque
  3. The maximum (breakdown) torque
  4. The starting torque and starting current
  5. The ratio \(T_{st}/T_{mm}\)

Step 1: Synchronous speed

\[ N_s = \frac{120f}{P} = \frac{120 \times 50}{4} = 1500 \text{ rpm} \] \[ \omega_s = \frac{2\pi \times 1500}{60} = 50\pi = 157.08 \text{ rad/s} \]

Step 2: Slip for maximum torque

\[ s_m = \frac{R'_r}{\sqrt{R_s^2 + (X_s + X'_r)^2}} = \frac{0.4}{\sqrt{0.5^2 + 4^2}} = \frac{0.4}{\sqrt{16.25}} = \frac{0.4}{4.031} = 0.0993 \]

Step 3: Maximum torque

\[ T_{mm} = \frac{3V_s^2}{2\omega_s\left(R_s + \sqrt{R_s^2 + (X_s + X'_r)^2}\right)} \] \[ T_{mm} = \frac{3 \times 230^2}{2 \times 157.08 \times (0.5 + 4.031)} = \frac{158700}{1423.4} = 111.5 \text{ N·m} \]

Step 4: Starting torque and current (\(s = 1\))

\[ T_{st} = \frac{3V_s^2 \cdot R'_r}{\omega_s\left[(R_s + R'_r)^2 + (X_s + X'_r)^2\right]} \] \[ T_{st} = \frac{3 \times 230^2 \times 0.4}{157.08 \times [(0.5 + 0.4)^2 + 4^2]} = \frac{63480}{157.08 \times 16.81} = \frac{63480}{2640.1} = 24.0 \text{ N·m} \] \[ I_{st} = \frac{V_s}{\sqrt{(R_s + R'_r)^2 + (X_s + X'_r)^2}} = \frac{230}{\sqrt{0.81 + 16}} = \frac{230}{4.1} = 56.1 \text{ A} \]

Step 5: Ratio \(T_{st}/T_{mm}\)

\[ \frac{T_{st}}{T_{mm}} = \frac{2s_m}{1 + s_m^2} = \frac{2 \times 0.0993}{1 + 0.0993^2} = \frac{0.1986}{1.00986} = 0.197 \]

Check: \(T_{st}/T_{mm} = 24.0/111.5 = 0.215\). The slight discrepancy is because the formula \(2s_m/(1+s_m^2)\) assumes \(R_s\) neglected, while our calculation of \(T_{st}\) and \(T_{mm}\) included \(R_s\).

Note

The starting torque is only about 20% of the breakdown torque — typical for a low-slip motor. The starting current of 56 A is about 4–6 times rated current for a motor of this size, which is why we need soft starters or star-delta starting for larger motors.

Worked Example 2

Problem: The same motor drives a fan load where \(T_L \propto \omega_r^2\). At rated voltage, the motor runs at 1440 rpm. The stator voltage is reduced to 80% (b = 0.8). Find the new speed.

Step 1: Find rated operating point

Rated slip: \(s_1 = (1500 - 1440)/1500 = 0.04\)

Rated speed: \(\omega_1 = 2\pi \times 1440/60 = 150.8\) rad/s

Step 2: Find load constant

At the rated operating point, the motor torque equals the load torque. Using the approximate torque formula (since we're in the linear region, \(s \ll s_m\)):

Load: \(T_L = k_L \omega_r^2\), so \(k_L = T_1/\omega_1^2\)

Step 3: Use the voltage reduction relationship

At reduced voltage, the motor torque at slip \(s_2\) is:

\[ T_{motor} = b^2 \times T_{motor,rated}(s_2) \]

In the linear region: \(T \approx \frac{2T_{mm}}{s_m} \cdot s\), so:

\[ b^2 \cdot \frac{2T_{mm}}{s_m} \cdot s_2 = k_L \omega_{r2}^2 \]

Since at rated voltage: \(\frac{2T_{mm}}{s_m} \cdot s_1 = k_L \omega_{r1}^2\), we can write:

\[ b^2 \cdot s_2 \cdot \omega_{r1}^2 = s_1 \cdot \omega_{r2}^2 \]

With \(\omega_{r2} = \omega_s(1 - s_2)\) and \(\omega_{r1} = \omega_s(1 - s_1)\):

\[ b^2 \cdot s_2 \cdot (1 - s_1)^2 = s_1 \cdot (1 - s_2)^2 \] \[ 0.64 \cdot s_2 \cdot 0.9216 = 0.04 \cdot (1 - s_2)^2 \] \[ 0.5898 \cdot s_2 = 0.04 \cdot (1 - 2s_2 + s_2^2) \] \[ 0.5898\,s_2 = 0.04 - 0.08\,s_2 + 0.04\,s_2^2 \] \[ 0.04\,s_2^2 - 0.6698\,s_2 + 0.04 = 0 \]

Using the quadratic formula:

\[ s_2 = \frac{0.6698 \pm \sqrt{0.6698^2 - 4(0.04)(0.04)}}{2 \times 0.04} = \frac{0.6698 \pm \sqrt{0.4486 - 0.0064}}{0.08} \] \[ s_2 = \frac{0.6698 \pm 0.6651}{0.08} \]

Taking the smaller root (stable operating point):

\[ s_2 = \frac{0.6698 - 0.6651}{0.08} = \frac{0.0047}{0.08} = 0.0588 \]

\(N_2 = 1500 \times (1 - 0.0588) = 1500 \times 0.9412 = \mathbf{1412 \text{ rpm}}\)

Note

Reducing the voltage to 80% only reduced the speed from 1440 to 1412 rpm — a change of only 28 rpm (about 2%). This illustrates the limited speed range of stator voltage control with a low-slip motor. With a high-slip motor, the speed reduction would be much more significant.

9. Practice Problems (Sheet 6)

▾
Problem 1

A 3-phase, 6-pole, 50 Hz wound-rotor induction motor has \(R_s = 0.5\;\Omega\), \(R'_r = 0.4\;\Omega\), and \(X_s + X'_r = 3.5\;\Omega\) (per phase). The motor is supplied at 400 V line-to-line (Y-connected). Find:

  1. The synchronous speed
  2. The slip for maximum torque
  3. The maximum torque
  4. The starting torque

Step 1: Phase voltage and synchronous speed

\[ V_s = \frac{400}{\sqrt{3}} = 230.9 \text{ V (phase)} \] \[ N_s = \frac{120 \times 50}{6} = 1000 \text{ rpm}, \quad \omega_s = \frac{2\pi \times 1000}{60} = 104.72 \text{ rad/s} \]

Step 2: Slip for maximum torque

\[ s_m = \frac{R'_r}{\sqrt{R_s^2 + (X_s + X'_r)^2}} = \frac{0.4}{\sqrt{0.25 + 12.25}} = \frac{0.4}{3.536} = 0.1131 \]

Step 3: Maximum torque

\[ T_{mm} = \frac{3 \times 230.9^2}{2 \times 104.72 \times (0.5 + 3.536)} = \frac{159,945.4}{845.4} = 189.2 \text{ N·m} \]

Step 4: Starting torque (\(s = 1\))

\[ T_{st} = \frac{3 \times 230.9^2 \times 0.4}{104.72 \times [(0.9)^2 + (3.5)^2]} = \frac{63,978.2}{104.72 \times 13.06} = \frac{63,978.2}{1367.6} = 46.8 \text{ N·m} \]
Verification

\(T_{st}/T_{mm} = 46.8/189.2 = 0.247\). Using the formula: \(2s_m/(1+s_m^2) = 2(0.1131)/(1+0.0128) = 0.2262/1.0128 = 0.223\). Close (difference due to \(R_s\) not being neglected in the direct calculation).

Problem 2

For the motor in Problem 1, external resistance is added to the rotor circuit to make the starting torque equal to the maximum torque. Find the required total rotor resistance (referred to stator).

Approach: For \(T_{st} = T_{mm}\), we need \(s_m = 1\) at starting. That means:

\[ s_m = \frac{R'_{r,total}}{\sqrt{R_s^2 + (X_s + X'_r)^2}} = 1 \] \[ R'_{r,total} = \sqrt{R_s^2 + (X_s + X'_r)^2} = \sqrt{0.25 + 12.25} = 3.536\;\Omega \]

External resistance needed (referred to stator):

\[ R'_{ext} = R'_{r,total} - R'_r = 3.536 - 0.4 = 3.136\;\Omega \]
Note

The external resistance is about 8 times the internal rotor resistance! This dramatically changes the torque-speed curve — the peak now occurs at standstill. This is the whole point of wound rotor motors: external resistance provides complete control over the starting characteristic. After starting, the external resistance is gradually shorted out to restore normal running performance.

Problem 3

A 3-phase, 4-pole, 50 Hz IM drives a fan load (\(T_L \propto \omega_r^2\)). At rated voltage, the motor operates at 1450 rpm with a torque of 50 N·m. The voltage is reduced to 70% (\(b = 0.7\)). Neglecting \(R_s\), with \(s_m = 0.08\), find the new operating speed.

Step 1: Initial conditions

\[ N_s = 1500 \text{ rpm}, \quad s_1 = \frac{1500 - 1450}{1500} = 0.0333 \] \[ \omega_{r1} = 2\pi \times 1450/60 = 151.8 \text{ rad/s} \]

Step 2: Set up equilibrium at new voltage

Using the relationship derived in Worked Example 2:

\[ b^2 \cdot s_2 \cdot (1 - s_1)^2 = s_1 \cdot (1 - s_2)^2 \] \[ 0.49 \cdot s_2 \cdot (0.9667)^2 = 0.0333 \cdot (1 - s_2)^2 \] \[ 0.4578\,s_2 = 0.0333(1 - 2s_2 + s_2^2) \] \[ 0.4578\,s_2 = 0.0333 - 0.0666\,s_2 + 0.0333\,s_2^2 \] \[ 0.0333\,s_2^2 - 0.5244\,s_2 + 0.0333 = 0 \] \[ s_2^2 - 15.74\,s_2 + 1 = 0 \] \[ s_2 = \frac{15.74 - \sqrt{247.7 - 4}}{2} = \frac{15.74 - 15.61}{2} = 0.065 \]

\(N_2 = 1500(1 - 0.065) = \mathbf{1403 \text{ rpm}}\)

Speed reduction: \(1450 - 1403 = 47\) rpm (about 3.2%)

Note

Even a 30% voltage reduction only produces about 3% speed change with this low-slip motor. For meaningful speed control, we'd need a high-slip motor design.

Lecture Summary

▾
Big Picture

In this lecture, we built the foundation for induction motor drives:

  1. IM types: Squirrel cage (dominant, cheap, rugged) vs. wound rotor (rotor access for control) vs. linear (for linear motion).
  2. Special designs: Low-slip (efficient running), high-slip (good starting, wide voltage control range), deep bar and double cage (best of both worlds via frequency-dependent impedance).
  3. Equivalent circuit: The per-phase model with \(R'_r/s\) captures all electromagnetic relationships. The rotor current and torque are derived from simple circuit analysis.
  4. Torque-speed characteristic: Nonlinear curve with breakdown torque \(T_{mm} \propto V_s^2/(X_s + X'_r)\) independent of rotor resistance. Rotor resistance only shifts \(s_m\).
  5. Key relationships: \(P_{cu,r} = s \cdot P_g\), \(P_m = (1-s) \cdot P_g\) — efficiency is fundamentally limited by slip.
  6. Stator voltage control: Simplest method (\(T \propto V^2\)), limited range, best with high-slip motors and fan loads. Implemented with thyristor AC voltage controllers.

Next lecture: Frequency control (V/f) — the most powerful and versatile IM speed control method, which changes synchronous speed directly and maintains full torque capability across the speed range.
