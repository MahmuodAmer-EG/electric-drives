DC Drives — Three-Phase Converters & Chopper Drives

From high-power industrial converters to nimble chopper-fed drives for EVs and transit systems.

Why Do We Need Three-Phase Converters?

🟡 The Problem

In Lecture 7, we explored single-phase converter drives—great for small motors up to about 15 kW. But what happens when we need to drive a 500 kW mill motor, a 1 MW hoist, or a massive rolling-mill drive? Single-phase supplies simply cannot deliver that kind of power cleanly. We'd draw enormous pulsating currents from one phase, create massive torque pulsations, and annoy every electrical engineer in the building with the harmonics we'd inject back into the grid.

🔵 The Intuition

Think of it like this: a single-phase supply is like one person pushing a merry-go-round—the push comes once per revolution, so the motion is jerky. A three-phase supply is like three people evenly spaced around the merry-go-round, each pushing in turn. The result? Smoother, more powerful, and more continuous torque delivery. That's exactly why three-phase converter drives exist: smoother DC output, higher power capability, and lower harmonic distortion.

🟢 Key Advantages of Three-Phase Over Single-Phase
  • Higher power ratings: up to 1500 kW with a full bridge, vs. 15 kW for single-phase.
  • Lower ripple: the DC output has a ripple frequency of 3f or 6f (vs. f or 2f for single-phase), meaning smaller and cheaper filters.
  • Better power factor: current drawn from the grid is more balanced.
  • Reduced torque pulsations: smoother motor operation, less mechanical stress.

3-Phase Half-Wave Converter

🟡 The Problem

We want the simplest possible three-phase controlled rectifier. Can we just use three thyristors—one per phase—and call it a day? Let's find out what that buys us and what it costs.

🔵 The Intuition

Imagine each phase of the three-phase supply "takes a turn" powering the DC motor. At any moment, whichever phase has the highest voltage (after we allow for the firing delay angle \(\alpha\)) wins and conducts through its thyristor. The other two phases sit idle. This natural "competition" among phases produces a DC output that's the envelope of the three phase voltages—but only the positive parts we've selected.

Figure 1: Three-phase half-wave converter — three thyristors, one per phase, with neutral return.
🟢 Deep Dive — Analysis

Let \( V_m \) be the peak phase voltage and \( V_L \) the RMS line-to-line voltage. The average output voltage is:

\[ V_a \;=\; \frac{3\sqrt{3}}{2\pi}\,V_m\,\cos\alpha \;=\; 1.17\,V_L\,\cos\alpha \]

Key characteristics:

  • Power rating: Suitable for loads up to 40 kW.
  • Quadrant operation: One quadrant only (\(+V_a\), \(+I_a\)).
  • Ripple frequency: 3× supply frequency (3f = 150 Hz for a 50 Hz supply).
  • Disadvantage: High harmonic content in supply current, DC component in supply (unbalanced), requires neutral connection.
  • Application: Limited—mostly small industrial drives where simplicity outweighs harmonic concerns.
Figure 2: Half-wave converter — single-quadrant (Q I) operation.
🟣 Takeaway

The 3-phase half-wave converter is the simplest three-phase option—just three thyristors. It gives us a DC output proportional to \(\cos\alpha\), good for up to 40 kW. But it injects DC into the supply and produces high harmonics, so it's rarely used in modern drives.

3-Phase Semi-Converter (Half-Controlled Bridge)

🟡 The Problem

We want a bridge configuration for higher power (no neutral needed), but full thyristor bridges are expensive. Can we use a mix of thyristors and diodes to get a cheaper solution that still does the job for medium-power one-quadrant drives?

🔵 The Intuition

Here's the trick: we put thyristors on the top half of the bridge (so we have firing angle control) and plain diodes on the bottom half (they conduct freely). The thyristors control when current starts flowing, and the diodes provide the freewheeling path. It's like having a "half-throttle" approach—cheaper than a fully controlled bridge, and we still get variable DC output. The diodes also provide a natural freewheeling path, which prevents negative output voltage. That means we're stuck in one quadrant, but for many applications that's perfectly fine.

Figure 3: Three-phase semi-converter — T1, T3, T5 (thyristors, top) and D4, D6, D2 (diodes, bottom).
🟢 Deep Dive — Analysis

The average output voltage for the semi-converter:

\[ V_a \;=\; \frac{3\,V_{m(L)}}{2\pi}\,(1 + \cos\alpha) \;=\; \frac{3\sqrt{2}\,V_L}{2\pi}\,(1 + \cos\alpha) \]

where \( V_{m(L)} = \sqrt{2}\,V_L \) is the peak line voltage. Note how the \((1+\cos\alpha)\) factor means:

  • At \(\alpha = 0^\circ\): \(V_a = \frac{3V_{m(L)}}{\pi}\) (maximum, same as uncontrolled diode bridge).
  • At \(\alpha = 180^\circ\): \(V_a = 0\) (fully reduced).
  • Output voltage is always positive (no inversion possible).

Key characteristics:

  • Power rating: Up to 115 kW.
  • Quadrant operation: One quadrant (\(+V_a\), \(+I_a\))—the diodes prevent voltage reversal.
  • Freewheeling: Diodes provide natural freewheeling, which improves the power factor compared to a full converter at large firing angles.
  • Ripple frequency: 3f (150 Hz for 50 Hz supply).
🟣 Takeaway

The semi-converter is the "budget bridge"—half the thyristors of a full converter, plus free freewheeling from the diodes. Good for one-quadrant drives up to 115 kW where you only need to motor, not brake regeneratively.

3-Phase Full Converter (Fully-Controlled Bridge)

🟡 The Problem

We need a drive that can handle up to 1500 kW and can reverse the DC voltage for regenerative braking—think of a crane lowering a heavy load, or an electric train decelerating. The semi-converter can't reverse voltage. We need full control.

🔵 The Intuition

Now we replace all six devices with thyristors. This gives us complete firing angle control on both the positive and negative halves of each cycle. The magical consequence: by increasing \(\alpha\) beyond 90°, the average output voltage goes negative while current still flows in the same direction. That means the motor absorbs negative power—it's actually pumping energy back into the supply. That's regenerative braking, and it's two-quadrant operation.

Figure 4: Fully-controlled six-thyristor bridge — T1, T3, T5 (top) and T4, T6, T2 (bottom).
🟢 Deep Dive — Analysis

The average output voltage for the full bridge converter:

\[ V_a \;=\; \frac{3\sqrt{6}}{\pi}\,V_\ph\,\cos\alpha \;=\; \frac{3\sqrt{2}}{\pi}\,V_L\,\cos\alpha \;=\; 2.34\,V_L\,\cos\alpha \]

For an uncontrolled bridge (\(\alpha=0\)):

\[ V_{a0} = 2.34\,V_L \]

Two-Quadrant Operation

This is where things get exciting:

  • \(0 \le \alpha < 90^\circ\): \(V_a > 0\) → Motoring (Quadrant I). Power flows from supply to motor.
  • \(90^\circ < \alpha \le 180^\circ\): \(V_a < 0\) → Regenerative braking (Quadrant IV, since \(I_a\) stays positive but \(V_a\) reverses). Power flows from motor back to supply.

Key characteristics:

  • Power rating: Up to 1500 kW—this is the workhorse of industrial DC drives.
  • Ripple frequency: 6f (300 Hz for 50 Hz supply)—much smoother than half-wave or semi.
  • Applications: Rolling mills, hoists, paper machines, mine winders, large fans and pumps.
Figure 5: Full converter — two-quadrant (Q I + Q IV) operation.
🟣 Takeaway

The fully-controlled bridge is the king of industrial DC drives. Six thyristors give us two-quadrant operation (motoring and regenerative braking) at power levels up to 1500 kW with a clean 6-pulse DC output. When \(\alpha > 90^\circ\), voltage reverses and the motor becomes a generator feeding energy back to the grid.

3-Phase Dual Converter

🟡 The Problem

A full converter gives us two quadrants, but what about applications that need true four-quadrant operation—motoring and braking in both directions of rotation? Think of a steel rolling mill that must accelerate and decelerate a massive slab in both directions, or an elevator that goes up and down while braking smoothly.

🔵 The Intuition

The solution is beautifully simple in concept: take two full converters and connect them back-to-back across the motor. Converter 1 handles positive current, Converter 2 handles negative current. Between them, they can produce any combination of positive/negative voltage and positive/negative current—all four quadrants. The key constraint linking them: their firing angles must always satisfy \(\alpha_1 + \alpha_2 = 180^\circ\), so the average voltages of the two converters are equal and opposite. Without this constraint, a massive circulating current would flow between the two bridges.

Figure 6: Dual converter — two fully-controlled bridges connected back-to-back across the motor.
🟢 Deep Dive — Analysis

Each converter individually produces:

\[ V_{a1} = 2.34\,V_L\,\cos\alpha_1, \qquad V_{a2} = 2.34\,V_L\,\cos\alpha_2 \]

The constraint \(\alpha_1 + \alpha_2 = \pi\) ensures \(V_{a1} = -V_{a2}\), preventing circulating currents.

  • Quadrant I (motoring forward): Conv 1 active, \(\alpha_1 < 90^\circ\). \(+V_a, +I_a\).
  • Quadrant II (regen braking, reverse rotation): Conv 2 active, \(\alpha_2 < 90^\circ\). \(-V_a, +I_a\)... wait, let's be more precise: by switching which converter carries the current, we can achieve all four V-I combinations.
  • Quadrant III (motoring reverse): Conv 2 active with reversed current path.
  • Quadrant IV (regen braking forward): Conv 1 with \(\alpha_1 > 90^\circ\).

Key characteristics:

  • Power rating: Up to 1500 kW (same as full converter, but with four-quadrant capability).
  • Cost: 12 thyristors total (two full bridges), plus complex control logic.
  • Applications: Reversing rolling mills, mine winders, elevators, test benches.
Figure 7: Dual converter — full four-quadrant operation.
🟣 Takeaway

The dual converter is the ultimate AC-to-DC drive topology. Two full bridges connected back-to-back give us complete four-quadrant operation: motoring and regenerative braking in both directions. The price? Twelve thyristors and a constraint that \(\alpha_1 + \alpha_2 = 180^\circ\).

Chopper-Controlled DC Drives — Introduction

🟡 The Problem

So far, we've been converting AC to controlled DC. But what if we already have a DC supply? Batteries in an electric vehicle, a DC bus from a solar array, or a DC traction supply in a metro system. We still need to control the motor speed, and we certainly don't want to waste energy in resistors like it's 1920. We need a way to efficiently vary a DC voltage—enter the DC chopper.

🔵 The Intuition

Imagine you have a garden hose (the DC supply) and you want to water your plants gently instead of blasting them. You could partially close the tap (that's a resistor—wasteful). Or you could rapidly flick the tap fully on and off, on and off. When it's on, full pressure. When it's off, no flow. The average water flow depends on what fraction of the time the tap is open. That's exactly what a chopper does: it switches the full DC supply on and off very rapidly, and the average output voltage is the fraction of on-time multiplied by the supply voltage.

The key insight: no energy is wasted in the switching process (ideally). The switch is either fully on (zero voltage drop, no loss) or fully off (zero current, no loss). Compare this to a resistor, which always dissipates heat.

The Chopper Principle

🟢 Deep Dive

A DC chopper is a high-speed electronic switch (typically a power MOSFET, IGBT, or thyristor with forced commutation) that chops the DC supply into a train of pulses. The motor's inductance acts as a natural filter, smoothing the current into a relatively steady value.

Fundamental Relations

\[ V_a = \delta \times V_s \]

where:

  • \(\delta\) = duty ratio = \(\dfrac{T_\on}{T_\on + T_\off} = T_\on \times f\)
  • \(f\) = chopping frequency = \(\dfrac{1}{T_\on + T_\off}\)
  • \(V_s\) = DC supply voltage
  • \(V_a\) = average voltage applied to the motor

Since \(0 \le \delta \le 1\), we get \(0 \le V_a \le V_s\). By varying \(\delta\), we smoothly control the motor voltage from zero to full supply.

Figure 9: Chopper output — rectangular pulses at voltage Vs. Average output Va = δ × Vs.

Class A Chopper (Step-Down / Buck)

🔵 The Intuition

The Class A chopper is the simplest and most common configuration. It's a "step-down" converter: the output voltage is always less than or equal to the supply. We use it for the most common task—motoring: controlling the speed of a DC motor in the forward direction by adjusting the duty ratio.

Figure 10: Class A chopper circuit — switch CH, freewheeling diode Dfw, and DC motor (Ra, La, Eg).
🟢 Deep Dive — Motor Equations with Chopper

When the chopper is ON, the supply voltage \(V_s\) is applied to the motor. When OFF, the freewheeling diode \(D_{fw}\) provides a path for the current to circulate through the motor (the inductor keeps current flowing). The average motor voltage:

\[ V_a = \delta \cdot V_s = E_g + I_a R_a \]

Since the back-EMF relates to speed:

\[ E_g = K\phi\,\omega_m \]

Solving for motor speed:

\[ \boxed{\omega_m = \frac{\delta\,V_s - I_a R_a}{K\phi}} \]

And the motor torque:

\[ T = K\phi\,I_a \]

So by varying \(\delta\) from 0 to 1, we smoothly control the motor speed from (almost) zero to the speed corresponding to full supply voltage. The operation is in one quadrant: \(+V_a, +I_a\).

🟣 Takeaway

The Class A chopper is elegant in its simplicity: one switch, one freewheeling diode, and you get smooth, efficient speed control of a DC motor from a DC supply. The motor speed is directly proportional to the duty ratio \(\delta\).

Current Ripple Analysis

🟡 The Problem

We said the motor inductance "smooths" the current. But how smooth is smooth? The current still rises during the ON period and falls during the OFF period, creating a ripple. Excessive ripple means higher peak currents (stressing the switch and motor), more heating, and more torque pulsations. We need to quantify this ripple and know how to minimize it.

🔵 The Intuition

Think of the motor inductor like a water tank. During the ON time, the chopper fills it faster than the motor drains it—the current (water level) rises. During the OFF time, only the motor is draining it—the current falls. The ripple is the difference between the peak and trough. More inductance = bigger tank = smaller fluctuation. Higher chopping frequency = shorter fill/drain cycles = also smaller fluctuation.

Figure 11: Armature current ripple — current rises during Ton and falls during Toff. The ripple ΔIa = Imax − Imin.
🟢 Deep Dive — Ripple Equations

The peak-to-peak current ripple is:

\[ \Delta I_a = \frac{(\delta\,V_s - E_g)(1 - \delta)}{f \cdot L_a} = \frac{V_s\,\delta\,(1 - \delta)}{f \cdot L_a} \]

(The second form uses the fact that \(\delta V_s - E_g = I_a R_a\) and, for the ripple derivation, we consider the voltage across the inductor during the ON time.)

Maximum Ripple Condition

The ripple is maximized when the product \(\delta(1-\delta)\) is maximized. Taking the derivative and setting it to zero:

\[ \frac{d}{d\delta}[\delta(1-\delta)] = 1 - 2\delta = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad \boxed{\delta = 0.5} \]

So maximum ripple occurs at 50% duty ratio. The maximum ripple value is:

\[ \Delta I_{a,\max} = \frac{V_s}{4\,f\,L_a} \]

Peak and Minimum Currents

\[ I_{a,\max} = I_{a,\avg} + \frac{\Delta I_a}{2}, \qquad I_{a,\min} = I_{a,\avg} - \frac{\Delta I_a}{2} \]

If \(I_{a,\min} < 0\), the current becomes discontinuous—it drops to zero during part of the off-time. This changes the motor behavior and requires separate analysis.

How to Reduce Ripple

  • Increase chopping frequency \(f\): Higher frequency = smaller ripple. Modern IGBTs allow frequencies of 5–20 kHz.
  • Increase inductance \(L_a\): Add an external inductor in series if the motor's armature inductance is too small.
  • Avoid \(\delta = 0.5\) if possible: Operate at higher or lower duty ratios for reduced ripple.
🟣 Takeaway

Current ripple is \(\Delta I_a = V_s\,\delta(1-\delta)/(f\,L_a)\). It's worst at \(\delta=0.5\) and can be tamed by increasing the chopping frequency or adding inductance. Always check that \(I_{a,\min} > 0\) to ensure continuous conduction.

Regenerative Braking with Chopper

🟡 The Problem

When an electric vehicle decelerates or a metro train approaches a station, the motor's kinetic energy needs to go somewhere. We could dump it into resistors as heat (dynamic braking), but that's wasteful. Can we send it back to the DC supply—recharging the battery or feeding it back to the DC bus? That's regenerative braking, and it saves energy (10–30% in metro systems!).

🔵 The Intuition

During regenerative braking, the motor acts as a generator. Its back-EMF \(E_g\) is now the "source" trying to push current back into the DC supply. But here's the challenge: the back-EMF might be lower than the supply voltage \(V_s\). So we need to "boost" it. We use the chopper in a different configuration—essentially a step-up (boost) converter—where the motor's inductance stores energy during the ON time and releases it at higher voltage to the supply during the OFF time.

Figure 12: Regenerative braking chopper (Class B) — energy flows from motor back to the DC supply.
🟢 Deep Dive — Regenerative Braking Analysis

During regenerative braking:

  • The motor acts as a generator: \(E_g > V_a\).
  • When the chopper switch CH is ON: The motor current circulates through the switch and inductor, building up energy in \(L_a\).
  • When CH turns OFF: The inductor forces current through diode D into the supply. The voltage across the motor terminals exceeds \(V_s\) (boost action), and energy is returned.

The average voltage relationship becomes:

\[ V_a = (1 - \delta)\,V_s \quad \text{or equivalently} \quad E_g = K\phi\,\omega_m \]

The key point: in step-up (regenerative) mode, as \(\delta\) increases, \(V_a\) decreases, and more current flows from the motor back to the supply. The speed at which regenerative braking is effective:

\[ \omega_m = \frac{(1-\delta)\,V_s + I_a R_a}{K\phi} \]

Power returned to the supply:

\[ P_{\text{regen}} = V_s \times I_s = E_g \times I_a - I_a^2 R_a \]
🟣 Takeaway

Regenerative braking with a chopper is like running the motor "backwards" energetically—kinetic energy converts to electrical energy and flows back to the supply. It requires the back-EMF to exceed the chopper output voltage, and uses the motor's own inductance as an energy storage element in a boost-converter action. In metro systems, this technique saves 10–30% of traction energy.

Practice Problems (Sheet 5)

Problem 1: Duty Ratio for Desired Speed ▼
Given

A DC motor: 220 V, 24 A, 1000 rpm, \(R_a = 2\;\Omega\). Fed from a chopper with \(V_s = 230\;\text{V}\) at \(f = 500\;\text{Hz}\). Find the duty ratio \(\delta\) for 500 rpm at 1.2 times rated torque.

🟢 Solution
  1. Find \(K\phi\) from rated conditions:
    At rated: \(E_g = V_a - I_a R_a = 220 - 24 \times 2 = 172\;\text{V}\)
    \(\omega_{\text{rated}} = \frac{2\pi \times 1000}{60} = 104.72\;\text{rad/s}\)
    \(K\phi = \frac{E_g}{\omega_m} = \frac{172}{104.72} = 1.643\;\text{V·s/rad}\)
  2. Find current at 1.2× rated torque:
    Since \(T = K\phi \cdot I_a\), and torque is proportional to current (for constant flux):
    \(I_a = 1.2 \times 24 = 28.8\;\text{A}\)
  3. Find back-EMF at 500 rpm:
    \(\omega_{500} = \frac{2\pi \times 500}{60} = 52.36\;\text{rad/s}\)
    \(E_g = K\phi \cdot \omega_{500} = 1.643 \times 52.36 = 86.0\;\text{V}\)
  4. Find required \(V_a\):
    \(V_a = E_g + I_a R_a = 86.0 + 28.8 \times 2 = 143.6\;\text{V}\)
  5. Find duty ratio:
    \(\delta = \frac{V_a}{V_s} = \frac{143.6}{230} = \boxed{0.624}\)
Problem 3: Armature Current from Chopper Timing ▼
Given

DC motor with \(R_a = 2\;\Omega\), chopper from 220 V DC. \(T_\on = 15\;\text{ms}\), \(T_\off = 10\;\text{ms}\), \(K\phi = 0.4\;\text{V·s/rad}\). Find \(I_a\) at 1400 rpm.

🟢 Solution
  1. Find duty ratio:
    \(\delta = \frac{T_\on}{T_\on + T_\off} = \frac{15}{15+10} = 0.6\)
  2. Find average voltage:
    \(V_a = \delta \cdot V_s = 0.6 \times 220 = 132\;\text{V}\)
  3. Find back-EMF:
    \(\omega_m = \frac{2\pi \times 1400}{60} = 146.61\;\text{rad/s}\)
    \(E_g = K\phi \cdot \omega_m = 0.4 \times 146.61 = 58.64\;\text{V}\)
  4. Find armature current:
    \(I_a = \frac{V_a - E_g}{R_a} = \frac{132 - 58.64}{2} = \boxed{36.68\;\text{A}}\)
Problem 12: Ripple, Imax, and Imin ▼
Given

Motor fed from 200 V chopper at 500 Hz. \(T = 35\;\text{Nm}\), \(\omega = 1000\;\text{rpm}\), \(R_a = 0.15\;\Omega\), \(L_a = 10\;\text{mH}\), \(K\phi = 1.5\;\text{V·s/rad}\). Find \(I_{\max}\), \(I_{\min}\), and the ripple.

🟢 Solution
  1. Find average current from torque:
    \(I_a = \frac{T}{K\phi} = \frac{35}{1.5} = 23.33\;\text{A}\)
  2. Find back-EMF:
    \(\omega_m = \frac{2\pi \times 1000}{60} = 104.72\;\text{rad/s}\)
    \(E_g = 1.5 \times 104.72 = 157.08\;\text{V}\)
  3. Find required Va and duty ratio:
    \(V_a = E_g + I_a R_a = 157.08 + 23.33 \times 0.15 = 160.58\;\text{V}\)
    \(\delta = \frac{V_a}{V_s} = \frac{160.58}{200} = 0.803\)
  4. Find current ripple:
    \(\Delta I_a = \frac{V_s \cdot \delta(1-\delta)}{f \cdot L_a} = \frac{200 \times 0.803 \times 0.197}{500 \times 0.01}\)
    \(\Delta I_a = \frac{200 \times 0.1582}{5} = \frac{31.64}{5} = \boxed{6.33\;\text{A}}\)
  5. Find peak and minimum currents:
    \(I_{\max} = I_a + \frac{\Delta I_a}{2} = 23.33 + 3.17 = \boxed{26.50\;\text{A}}\)
    \(I_{\min} = I_a - \frac{\Delta I_a}{2} = 23.33 - 3.17 = \boxed{20.17\;\text{A}}\)
    Since \(I_{\min} > 0\), the current is continuous.
Problem 14: Regenerative Braking ▼
Given

230 V, 1000 rpm, 20 A motor. \(R_a = 1\;\Omega\), \(L_a = 50\;\text{mH}\). Regenerative braking at 600 Hz with \(\delta = 0.5\). Calculate the speed and power returned to the supply.

🟢 Solution
  1. Find \(K\phi\) from rated conditions:
    \(E_{g,\text{rated}} = 230 - 20 \times 1 = 210\;\text{V}\)
    \(\omega_{\text{rated}} = 104.72\;\text{rad/s}\)
    \(K\phi = \frac{210}{104.72} = 2.006\;\text{V·s/rad}\)
  2. Find the average voltage during regeneration:
    In regenerative (step-up) chopper mode: \(V_a = (1-\delta)\,V_s = (1-0.5) \times 230 = 115\;\text{V}\)
  3. Find the motor speed during regeneration:
    During regenerative braking, \(E_g = V_a + I_a R_a\) (note: current flows out of the motor, so the \(I_a R_a\) drop adds to \(V_a\) to give \(E_g\)).
    Assuming rated current for simplicity: \(I_a = 20\;\text{A}\)
    \(E_g = 115 + 20 \times 1 = 135\;\text{V}\)
    \(\omega_m = \frac{E_g}{K\phi} = \frac{135}{2.006} = 67.30\;\text{rad/s}\)
    \(n = \frac{60 \times 67.30}{2\pi} = \boxed{642.6\;\text{rpm}}\)
  4. Find power returned to the supply:
    \(P_{\text{regen}} = E_g \cdot I_a - I_a^2 R_a = 135 \times 20 - 20^2 \times 1\)
    \(P_{\text{regen}} = 2700 - 400 = \boxed{2300\;\text{W} = 2.3\;\text{kW}}\)

Grand Summary

🟣 What We Covered

In this lecture, we've covered the full spectrum of DC drive topologies:

  • Three-phase converters for high-power AC-to-DC conversion: half-wave (40 kW), semi-converter (115 kW), full converter (1500 kW, two-quadrant), and dual converter (1500 kW, four-quadrant).
  • DC choppers for efficient DC-to-DC speed control: the Class A (step-down) chopper for motoring, and the Class B (step-up) chopper for regenerative braking.
  • Current ripple analysis: how to calculate it, when it's worst (\(\delta = 0.5\)), and how to minimize it (increase \(f\) or \(L_a\)).

These dominate modern industry because AC motors are cheaper, more robust, and maintenance-free compared to DC motors. But controlling them is harder—that's where the fun begins!
