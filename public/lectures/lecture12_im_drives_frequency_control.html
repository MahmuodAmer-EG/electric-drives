Lecture 12

IM Drives — Frequency Control & Rotor Resistance Control

Exploring scalar V/F control of induction motors, the three operating regions, rotor resistance methods for wound-rotor machines, and slip power recovery drives.

1. Frequency Control — Introduction

▾
The Problem / The Why

An induction motor's synchronous speed is \(N_s = 120f/P\). If we want to change speed smoothly and efficiently, the most powerful lever we have is the supply frequency. Unlike rotor resistance or voltage control, frequency control does not waste energy as heat — it directly changes where the motor "wants" to run. This is why virtually every modern industrial drive is a variable-frequency drive (VFD).

The Intuition

Think of an induction motor like a runner on a treadmill. The supply frequency sets the treadmill speed (synchronous speed). The runner always lags a little behind (slip). If you want the runner to go faster, you speed up the treadmill — that's frequency control. But if you just speed up the treadmill without adjusting the incline (voltage), the runner will stumble (flux drops, motor weakens). That's why we keep V/f constant.

Deep Dive — Two Categories of Frequency Control

Once we decide to control frequency, there are two fundamentally different philosophies:

MethodPrincipleComplexityPerformance
Scalar (V/F) Control Adjust voltage and frequency magnitudes together, maintaining V/f ratio Simple Good for steady-state, fans, pumps
Vector (Field-Oriented) Control Decompose stator current into flux & torque components, control independently — like a DC motor Complex (requires rotor position/flux estimation) Excellent dynamic response, servo applications

This lecture focuses on scalar V/F control. Vector control will be covered in future lectures. Scalar control is simpler and sufficient for a huge range of applications — any load where you don't need fast torque transients (fans, pumps, conveyors, compressors).

Takeaway

Frequency control is the most efficient and most widely used speed control method for induction motors. Scalar V/F control keeps things simple by maintaining the voltage-to-frequency ratio, preserving airgap flux and torque capability across a wide speed range.

2. The Constant V/F Principle

▾
The Problem / The Why

If we just change the frequency without touching the voltage, the airgap flux changes — and with it, the motor's entire torque capability. At low frequency with full voltage, the core saturates and draws excessive magnetizing current. At high frequency with the same voltage, the flux drops and the motor becomes weak. We need a rule that keeps the motor "healthy" across all frequencies.

The Intuition

The back-EMF of the motor is like the "voltage used to maintain the magnetic field." The back-EMF equation is \(E = 4.44 \cdot f \cdot T_{ph} \cdot k_w \cdot \Phi_m\). If we want to keep the flux \(\Phi_m\) constant (and therefore keep the motor's torque capability constant), then as \(f\) changes, \(E\) must change proportionally. Since \(V \approx E\) (neglecting stator impedance), we get the constant V/f rule.

Deep Dive — The Math Behind V/F

Starting from the back-EMF equation:

Back-EMF Equation
\[E = 4.44 \cdot f \cdot T_{ph} \cdot k_w \cdot \Phi_m\]

Since \(T_{ph}\) (turns per phase) and \(k_w\) (winding factor) are fixed machine parameters:

Constant Flux Condition
\[\frac{E}{f} = 4.44 \cdot T_{ph} \cdot k_w \cdot \Phi_m = \text{constant}\]

This means: if we keep E/f constant, the airgap flux stays constant. And since \(V \approx E\) when stator impedance is small, the practical rule becomes keep V/f constant.

Now consider the referred rotor current in the equivalent circuit:

Rotor Current (Referred to Stator)
\[I'_r = \frac{E}{\sqrt{(R'_r/s)^2 + X'^2_r}}\]

where \(X'_r = 2\pi f L'_r\) is the rotor leakage reactance. Notice that \(X'_r\) also depends on frequency, but the slip frequency is what matters for the rotor circuit, and the ratio works out.

Torque Expression Under V/F Control

The Problem / The Why

We've established the flux rule, but what does it actually do for the torque? We need a torque expression that shows how frequency control affects the torque-speed curve — and crucially, whether the maximum torque is preserved.

The developed torque of an induction motor (per phase, three-phase total) is:

Developed Torque (Stator Impedance Neglected)
\[T_d = \frac{3E_{\text{base}}^2 \cdot (R'_r/s)}{\omega_s \left[(R'_r/s)^2 + X'^2_r\right]}\]

where \(\omega_s = 2\pi f\) is the synchronous angular frequency and \(E_{\text{base}}\) is the back-EMF at the operating frequency (which scales with \(f\) when V/f is maintained).

Setting \(dT_d/ds = 0\) to find the slip at maximum torque:

Slip at Maximum Torque (Rs Neglected)
\[s_m = \frac{R'_r}{X'_r}\]

And the maximum torque itself:

Maximum Torque
\[T_{\max} = \frac{3E_{\text{base}}^2}{2\omega_s X'_r}\]
The Key Insight

Look carefully at \(T_{\max}\). Both \(E_{\text{base}}^2\) and \(\omega_s \cdot X'_r\) are proportional to \(f^2\) and \(f \cdot f\) respectively when V/f is held constant. The \(f\) dependences cancel out! This means \(T_{\max}\) stays constant regardless of frequency — as long as V/f is maintained. This is the whole point of V/F control.

We can express the torque in a convenient normalized form:

Normalized Torque
\[\frac{T_d}{T_{\max}} = \frac{2 \cdot s \cdot s_m}{s^2 + s_m^2}\]
Takeaway

Under constant V/f control (with stator impedance neglected), the maximum torque \(T_{\max}\) is independent of frequency. The entire family of torque-speed curves at different frequencies has the same peak torque value — the curves just shift along the speed axis. This gives us constant torque capability from zero up to rated speed.

Figure 1: Family of torque-speed curves under V/F control. Below rated frequency, Tmax remains constant. Above rated frequency, Tmax decreases (flux weakening).

3. Three Operating Regions

▾
The Problem / The Why

A motor doesn't operate the same way at all speeds. Below rated speed, we can freely boost both voltage and frequency. Above rated speed, the voltage hits its ceiling (the inverter or supply can't go higher). This creates distinct operating regions, each with its own rules for torque, power, and flux. Understanding these regions is essential for sizing a drive and predicting motor behavior.

Region 1: Constant Torque (f ≤ frated)

The Intuition

In this region, we're below the motor's rated frequency. We increase speed by increasing \(f\), and we increase \(V\) proportionally to keep V/f constant. Since flux is constant and current can be at rated value, the motor can produce its full rated torque at any speed in this region.

Deep Dive
  • Voltage: Increases linearly with frequency (\(V/f = \text{const}\))
  • Flux: Constant (maintained by V/f ratio)
  • Maximum torque: Constant (independent of frequency)
  • Power: Increases linearly with speed, since \(P = T \cdot \omega\) and \(T\) is constant
  • Current: Can be at rated value

At low frequencies, voltage boost is needed to compensate for stator resistance (discussed in Section 4).

Region 2: Constant Power (f > frated)

The Intuition

Now the motor is above its rated speed. We've run out of voltage — the inverter can't supply more than rated voltage. So we keep increasing \(f\) while voltage stays at \(V_{\text{rated}}\). The V/f ratio drops, meaning flux weakens. It's exactly like field weakening in a DC motor. The motor can still produce rated power, but since \(\omega\) is increasing, the torque must decrease: \(T = P/\omega\).

Deep Dive
  • Voltage: Stays at \(V_{\text{rated}}\) (can't exceed)
  • Frequency: Increases above \(f_{\text{rated}}\)
  • Flux: Decreases (V/f drops, so \(\Phi \propto V/f\) falls)
  • Current: Maintained at rated value
  • Power: Approximately constant (\(P = V \cdot I \approx \text{const}\))
  • Torque: Decreases inversely with speed: \(T \propto 1/\omega\)
  • Slip: Increases gradually as frequency rises

Region 3: Breakdown Torque Region (High Frequency)

The Intuition

Push the frequency high enough in the flux-weakening region, and eventually the slip reaches its maximum value \(s_m\). The motor is now operating at the tip of its torque-speed curve. Any further increase in frequency causes the torque to drop even faster — the motor can no longer sustain constant power.

Deep Dive
  • Slip: At maximum value \(s_m\)
  • Torque: Equals breakdown torque, which decreases as frequency increases further
  • Power: No longer constant — decreases with increasing frequency
  • Practical limit: This region is usually avoided in normal operation; drives are sized so the load torque stays within Regions 1 and 2

Figure 2: The three operating regions of V/F control. Region 1: constant torque (power rises). Region 2: constant power (torque falls). Region 3: both torque and power decrease.

Takeaway

Below rated speed, the motor operates at constant torque (V/f maintained). Above rated speed, voltage is capped at rated value, flux weakens, and the motor transitions to constant power operation. This dual-region behavior — constant torque below base speed, constant power above — is identical in concept to DC motor armature voltage + field weakening control.

4. Effect of Stator Impedance

▾
The Problem / The Why

So far we assumed \(V \approx E\), ignoring the voltage drop across the stator resistance \(R_s\) and leakage reactance \(X_s\). At rated frequency, this drop is a small fraction of the supply voltage. But at low frequencies, the supply voltage is low (because V/f = const), while the stator resistance drop \(R_s \cdot I_s\) stays the same. A 5 V drop that's negligible at 200 V supply becomes very significant at 20 V supply. This kills the flux and torque at low speeds.

The Intuition

Imagine you're filling a swimming pool through a long hose. At high water pressure (high frequency/voltage), the friction in the hose is a minor loss. But at low pressure (low frequency/voltage), that same friction can reduce the flow to a trickle. The stator resistance is the "hose friction" — at low frequencies, it steals a disproportionate share of the voltage, starving the airgap of flux.

Deep Dive — Voltage Boost Strategies

The actual terminal voltage must be:

Voltage with Boost Compensation
\[V = \left(\frac{V}{f}\right) \cdot f + \Delta V\]

where \(\Delta V\) is the boost voltage added to compensate for the stator impedance drop.

Different boost strategies exist:

StrategyDescriptionPros/Cons
Constant Boost Add a fixed \(\Delta V\) at all frequencies below a threshold Simple to implement; can cause excessive flux at light load / low speed
Linear Boost \(\Delta V\) decreases linearly with frequency, zero at rated \(f\) Better match; still a compromise
Nonlinear / Current-Dependent Boost \(\Delta V = R_s \cdot I_s\) (measured or estimated) Best accuracy; requires current feedback

Figure 3: V/f characteristic with voltage boost at low frequencies. Above rated frequency, voltage is capped at Vrated.

Takeaway

At low frequencies, stator resistance drop is significant relative to the supply voltage, causing flux and torque to sag. Voltage boost compensates for this. The best approach uses current-dependent boost (\(\Delta V = R_s I_s\)), but even a simple fixed or linear boost greatly improves low-speed torque.

5. Variable Frequency Sources

▾
The Problem / The Why

The V/F control principle requires a three-phase AC source with adjustable voltage and frequency. The utility grid provides fixed 50 or 60 Hz. We need power electronic converters to bridge this gap. The choice of converter topology affects cost, performance, harmonics, and the motor's operating behavior.

Deep Dive — Converter Topologies
TopologyHow It WorksCharacteristics
Voltage Source Inverter (VSI) Rectifier → DC bus (with capacitor) → 3-phase inverter using IGBTs/MOSFETs Most common. PWM controls both voltage and frequency. Stiff DC bus voltage. Good harmonic performance with high switching frequency.
Current Source Inverter (CSI) Controlled rectifier → DC link inductor → thyristor inverter Stiff DC link current. Used in high-power applications. Inherent regeneration capability. Needs commutation capacitors.
Cycloconverter Direct AC-to-AC conversion using thyristors (no DC link) Output frequency limited to ~1/3 of input. Used for very high power, low speed (cement mills, ship propulsion). Less common today.

Figure 4: Voltage Source Inverter (VSI) drive block diagram. The most common variable frequency drive topology.

Takeaway

The Voltage Source Inverter (VSI) is by far the most common topology for modern VFDs. It converts grid AC to DC (rectifier), smooths it (DC bus capacitor), then synthesizes variable-frequency, variable-voltage AC using PWM switching (inverter). This gives complete control over both voltage and frequency.

6. Rotor Resistance Control (Wound Rotor IM)

▾
The Problem / The Why

Not all induction motors are squirrel-cage. Wound-rotor induction motors have accessible rotor windings brought out through slip rings. This gives us a unique control handle: we can add external resistance in the rotor circuit. While inefficient (slip power is dissipated as heat), this method is simple, well-proven, and still used in applications like cranes, hoists, and some industrial drives where starting torque requirements are severe.

The Intuition

Adding resistance to the rotor circuit is like adding drag to the runner on our treadmill. The runner (rotor) slows down more relative to the treadmill (synchronous speed), meaning higher slip. Crucially, the maximum force the runner can exert doesn't change — it just happens at a lower speed. The torque-speed curve slides to the left, but the peak doesn't shrink.

Deep Dive — Mathematical Analysis

With external resistance \(R'_e\) added to the rotor circuit (referred to stator), the slip at maximum torque becomes:

Slip at Maximum Torque with External Resistance
\[s_m = \frac{R'_r + R'_e}{\sqrt{R_s^2 + (X_s + X'_r)^2}}\]

If stator resistance \(R_s\) is neglected:

Simplified
\[s_m = \frac{R'_r + R'_e}{X_s + X'_r}\]

Key observations:

  • Adding \(R'_e\) increases \(s_m\) — the peak torque shifts to higher slip (lower speed)
  • The value of \(T_{\max}\) is unchanged — it depends only on the machine parameters, not on rotor resistance
  • The speed range for a given load torque widens with more resistance
  • At \(s_m = 1\) (standstill), maximum starting torque is obtained

When referring external resistance to the stator side (important for calculations with turns ratio):

Referred Resistance with Turns Ratio
\[R'_e = a^2 \cdot R_{e,\text{actual}}\]

where \(a = N_{\text{stator}} / N_{\text{rotor}}\) is the stator-to-rotor turns ratio.

Figure 5: Effect of external rotor resistance on the torque-speed curve. The peak shifts to higher slip (lower speed), but Tmax remains the same.

Static Rotor Resistance Control

The Problem / The Why

Traditional rotor resistance control uses bulky three-phase variable resistors (rheostats). These are difficult to adjust precisely and continuously. Modern static rotor resistance control replaces them with a diode bridge rectifier, a DC chopper, and a single DC resistance — giving smooth, electronically controlled resistance variation.

Deep Dive — Static Circuit

The rotor three-phase AC is rectified by a 3-phase diode bridge into DC. A DC chopper (transistor switch) controls the effective resistance seen by the DC side:

Equivalent External Resistance Per Phase
\[R_e = \frac{1}{2} R \cdot (1 - k)\]

where \(R\) is the DC resistance and \(k\) is the duty ratio of the DC chopper (0 to 1).

  • \(k = 0\): Chopper always off → \(R_e = R/2\) (maximum resistance, lowest speed)
  • \(k = 1\): Chopper always on → \(R_e = 0\) (zero external resistance, normal operation, highest speed)

The relationship between DC link current \(I_d\) and AC rotor current \(I_r\):

DC to AC Current Relationship
\[I_d = \sqrt{\frac{3}{2}} \cdot I_r\]

Advantages over mechanical rheostats:

  • Continuously variable (smooth speed control)
  • More precise and repeatable
  • Can be used in closed-loop control systems
  • No moving contacts (longer life, less maintenance)
  • Faster response

Figure 6: Static rotor resistance control. 3-phase diode bridge rectifies rotor current; DC chopper controls effective resistance.

Takeaway

Static rotor resistance control replaces bulky rheostats with a diode bridge + chopper + DC resistance. The duty ratio \(k\) provides smooth, continuous control of the equivalent rotor resistance: \(R_e = 0.5R(1-k)\). This is much more precise and suitable for closed-loop control than mechanical rheostats.

7. Slip Power Recovery Control

▾
The Problem / The Why

Rotor resistance control wastes energy. The slip power \(P_{\text{slip}} = s \cdot P_{\text{airgap}}\) is dissipated as heat in the external resistance. At high slip (low speed), this can be a huge fraction of the total power. What if we could recover that slip power and put it to useful work instead of wasting it? That's exactly what slip power recovery drives do.

Kramer Drive (Static Kramer)

The Intuition

Instead of burning the slip power in a resistor, the Kramer drive routes it to a DC motor that is mechanically coupled to the induction motor shaft. The DC motor converts the electrical slip power back into useful mechanical power, adding its torque to the shaft. The total mechanical output is IM torque + DC motor torque, and the speed is controlled by adjusting the DC motor's field current.

Deep Dive
  • Rotor AC power is rectified to DC by a diode bridge
  • DC power feeds a DC motor coupled to the IM shaft
  • Total shaft torque = IM torque + DC motor torque
  • Speed controlled by the DC motor's field current
  • Advantage: Slip power is recovered as useful mechanical work, not wasted as heat
  • Drawback: Requires an auxiliary DC motor (extra cost, maintenance, inertia)

Figure 7: Kramer drive. Slip power from rotor is rectified and feeds a DC motor coupled to the same shaft.

Scherbius Drive (Static Scherbius)

The Intuition

The Scherbius drive takes the idea further: instead of feeding slip power to a DC motor, it inverts the DC back to AC and feeds it back to the grid through a transformer. No auxiliary motor needed — the slip power is simply returned to the electrical supply. This is more elegant and requires less hardware than the Kramer approach.

Deep Dive
  • Rotor AC → diode bridge → DC link → line-commutated inverter → transformer → grid
  • Slip power is recovered to the supply mains (not wasted)
  • No auxiliary DC motor needed (simpler, lighter, less maintenance)
  • With diode bridge on rotor side: only works for speeds below synchronous (sub-synchronous only)
  • Replace diode bridge with fully-controlled thyristor bridge → extends to above synchronous speed
  • This becomes the doubly-fed induction machine (DFIM)

Figure 8: Scherbius drive. Slip power is rectified, inverted, and fed back to the grid through a transformer.

Takeaway

Slip power recovery drives (Kramer and Scherbius) address the fundamental inefficiency of rotor resistance control. The Kramer drive converts slip power to useful mechanical work via a DC motor. The Scherbius drive is more elegant, feeding slip power back to the grid. With a fully-controlled converter on the rotor side, the Scherbius concept evolves into the doubly-fed induction machine.

8. Doubly-Fed Induction Machine (DFIM)

▾
The Problem / The Why

The Scherbius drive with a diode rectifier only works below synchronous speed. What if we need operation above and below synchronous speed? And what if we want the machine to behave like a synchronous machine — running at a speed independent of load? The doubly-fed induction machine (DFIM) achieves this by using a bidirectional AC/DC/AC converter between the rotor and the grid.

The Intuition

Think of the DFIM as an induction motor with a "speed tuner" connected to its rotor. By injecting power into the rotor at the right frequency and phase, we can make the rotor's magnetic field rotate at whatever speed we need to keep the stator field synchronized with the grid. Below synchronous speed, we extract power from the rotor. Above synchronous speed, we inject power into the rotor. Either way, the machine speed can be controlled independently of the load.

Deep Dive
Operating ModeSpeedRotor Power FlowSlip
Sub-synchronous \(N < N_s\) Power extracted from rotor → grid \(s > 0\)
Synchronous \(N = N_s\) DC excitation (zero rotor frequency) \(s = 0\)
Super-synchronous \(N > N_s\) Power injected into rotor from grid \(s < 0\)

Key features of the DFIM:

  • The converter handles only slip power (a fraction of total power), so it can be much smaller than a full-rated converter
  • Acts like a synchronous machine: speed is independent of load torque
  • Widely used in wind turbines (Type 3 wind turbine generator)
  • Allows variable-speed operation with reduced converter rating (typically ±30% of rated power)
  • Can control both active and reactive power independently

Figure 9: Doubly-fed induction machine. A partial-rated bidirectional converter connects rotor to grid, enabling variable-speed operation both below and above synchronous speed.

Takeaway

The DFIM is the natural evolution of the Scherbius drive. By using a bidirectional AC/DC/AC converter on the rotor, it can operate above and below synchronous speed, acting like a variable-speed synchronous machine. Since the converter only handles slip power (a fraction of total), it's smaller and cheaper than a full-rated converter. This makes the DFIM the dominant choice for modern wind turbines and some high-power industrial drives.

9. Lecture Summary

▾
Big Picture

In this lecture, we covered the major speed control techniques for induction motors beyond simple voltage control:

  1. V/F (Scalar) Control: The workhorse of modern drives. By maintaining constant V/f, we preserve airgap flux and keep \(T_{\max}\) constant across a wide speed range. Three operating regions emerge: constant torque (below base speed), constant power (above base speed), and breakdown torque (very high frequencies).
  2. Stator Impedance Effects: At low frequencies, \(R_s I_s\) drop is significant — voltage boost is needed to maintain flux and torque.
  3. Variable Frequency Sources: The VSI (voltage source inverter) is the dominant topology, using a rectifier + DC bus + PWM inverter.
  4. Rotor Resistance Control: For wound-rotor motors. Adding external resistance shifts \(T_{\max}\) to lower speeds without changing its value. Static control (diode bridge + chopper) is more precise than rheostats.
  5. Slip Power Recovery:
    • Kramer drive: slip power feeds a DC motor on the same shaft
    • Scherbius drive: slip power returned to the grid via an inverter
  6. DFIM: The ultimate evolution — bidirectional converter on rotor enables variable-speed operation above and below synchronous speed with a partial-rated converter. The backbone of modern wind energy.

Next, we will explore vector (field-oriented) control, which takes the sophistication one step further by decoupling torque and flux control, achieving DC-motor-like dynamic performance from an AC induction machine.

10. Worked Examples

▾
Example 1 — V/F Control: Finding Speed at Reduced Frequency

A 3-phase, 4-pole, 50 Hz induction motor has a rated slip of 4%. The motor is controlled by a V/F drive. Find the motor speed when the supply frequency is reduced to 30 Hz, assuming the same slip.

Step 1: Find synchronous speed at rated frequency

\(N_{s1} = \frac{120 \times 50}{4} = 1500 \text{ rpm}\)

Step 2: Find rated motor speed

\(N_{r1} = N_{s1}(1 - s) = 1500(1 - 0.04) = 1440 \text{ rpm}\)

Step 3: Find synchronous speed at 30 Hz

\(N_{s2} = \frac{120 \times 30}{4} = 900 \text{ rpm}\)

Step 4: Find motor speed at 30 Hz (same slip)

Under constant V/f control, if the load torque is the same, the slip remains approximately the same:

\(N_{r2} = N_{s2}(1 - s) = 900(1 - 0.04) = \mathbf{864 \text{ rpm}}\)

Why Same Slip?

With V/f constant, the entire torque-speed curve shifts proportionally. If the load torque hasn't changed, the operating slip stays the same. The motor just "tracks" the new synchronous speed with the same percentage lag.

Example 2 — Maximum Torque Under V/F Control

A 3-phase, 60 Hz, 6-pole induction motor has the following equivalent circuit parameters (referred to stator): \(R'_r = 0.3\;\Omega\), \(X'_r = 1.2\;\Omega\), \(X_s = 1.0\;\Omega\). The rated line voltage is 460 V. Neglect \(R_s\) and magnetizing branch. Calculate: (a) The slip at maximum torque. (b) The maximum torque at rated frequency. (c) Whether \(T_{\max}\) changes when the frequency is reduced to 40 Hz with V/f maintained.

(a) Slip at maximum torque

With \(R_s\) neglected:

\(s_m = \frac{R'_r}{X_s + X'_r} = \frac{0.3}{1.0 + 1.2} = \frac{0.3}{2.2} = \mathbf{0.1364}\) (13.64%)

(b) Maximum torque at 60 Hz

The synchronous speed: \(\omega_s = 2\pi \times 60 = 376.99\) rad/s (electrical), or mechanically \(\omega_{s,mech} = 376.99 / (P/2) = 376.99/3 = 125.66\) rad/s

Phase voltage: \(V_{ph} = 460/\sqrt{3} = 265.6\) V

Since we neglect \(R_s\): \(E \approx V_{ph} = 265.6\) V

\(T_{\max} = \frac{3 E^2}{2 \omega_{s,mech} (X_s + X'_r)}\)

\(T_{\max} = \frac{3 \times 265.6^2}{2 \times 125.66 \times 2.2} = \frac{3 \times 70543}{552.9} = \frac{211630}{552.9} = \mathbf{382.8 \text{ N}\cdot\text{m}}\)

(c) Does \(T_{\max}\) change at 40 Hz?

At 40 Hz with V/f maintained:

  • New voltage: \(V_{40} = V_{60} \times (40/60) = 460 \times 2/3 = 306.7\) V (line), \(V_{ph} = 177.1\) V
  • New synchronous speed: \(\omega_{s,mech,40} = 2\pi \times 40 / 3 = 83.78\) rad/s
  • New reactances: \(X_{s,40} = 1.0 \times (40/60) = 0.667\;\Omega\), \(X'_{r,40} = 1.2 \times (40/60) = 0.8\;\Omega\)

\(T_{\max,40} = \frac{3 \times 177.1^2}{2 \times 83.78 \times (0.667 + 0.8)} = \frac{3 \times 31364}{2 \times 83.78 \times 1.467} = \frac{94093}{245.8} = \mathbf{382.8 \text{ N}\cdot\text{m}}\)

Verification

The maximum torque is identical at 40 Hz and 60 Hz! This confirms the key V/F control principle: when E/f is maintained constant, \(T_{\max}\) is preserved. The \(f^2\) in the numerator (from \(E^2 \propto f^2\)) cancels with the \(f^2\) in the denominator (from \(\omega_s \cdot X \propto f \cdot f\)).

Example 3 — Rotor Resistance Control: Finding External Resistance for Target Speed

A 3-phase, 4-pole, 50 Hz wound-rotor induction motor has \(R'_r = 0.5\;\Omega\) (referred to stator) and drives a constant-torque load. At rated conditions, the motor runs at 1440 rpm. Find the external resistance (referred to stator) needed to reduce the speed to 1200 rpm. Neglect stator impedance.

Step 1: Synchronous speed and rated slip

\(N_s = 120 \times 50 / 4 = 1500\) rpm

\(s_1 = (1500 - 1440)/1500 = 60/1500 = 0.04\)

Step 2: New slip at target speed

\(s_2 = (1500 - 1200)/1500 = 300/1500 = 0.2\)

Step 3: Use the constant-torque condition

For constant torque, and neglecting stator impedance, the rotor current must remain the same. From the torque equation:

\(T = \frac{3V^2(R'_r + R'_e)/s}{\omega_s[(R'_r + R'_e)/s]^2 + (X_s + X'_r)^2]}\)

For small slip (operating below \(s_m\)), the torque is approximately proportional to \(s/(R'_r + R'_e)\). For constant torque:

\(\frac{s_1}{R'_r} = \frac{s_2}{R'_r + R'_e}\)

Step 4: Solve for \(R'_e\)

\(R'_r + R'_e = R'_r \times \frac{s_2}{s_1} = 0.5 \times \frac{0.2}{0.04} = 0.5 \times 5 = 2.5\;\Omega\)

\(R'_e = 2.5 - 0.5 = \mathbf{2.0\;\Omega}\) (referred to stator)

Physical Check

We needed to increase the total rotor resistance by a factor of 5 (from 0.5 to 2.5) to increase the slip by a factor of 5 (from 0.04 to 0.20). This makes physical sense: for constant torque, the slip is proportional to the total rotor resistance.

Example 4 — Rotor Resistance with Turns Ratio

The wound-rotor motor in Example 3 has a stator-to-rotor turns ratio of \(a = 2\). Find the actual external resistance per phase to be connected to the rotor slip rings.

We found \(R'_e = 2.0\;\Omega\) (referred to stator). To find the actual rotor-side resistance:

\[R'_e = a^2 \cdot R_{e,\text{actual}}\]

\(R_{e,\text{actual}} = \frac{R'_e}{a^2} = \frac{2.0}{2^2} = \frac{2.0}{4} = \mathbf{0.5\;\Omega \text{ per phase}}\)

Key Point

Always be careful about which side a resistance is referred to. The equivalent circuit uses stator-referred values, but physical resistors are connected on the rotor side. The conversion uses \(a^2\), which can make a big difference when the turns ratio is not unity.

Example 5 — Static Rotor Resistance: Finding Duty Ratio

A wound-rotor IM uses static rotor resistance control with a DC resistance \(R = 8\;\Omega\). The motor requires an equivalent external resistance of \(R_e = 2\;\Omega\) per phase for a desired speed. Find the required duty ratio \(k\).

Using the static resistance formula:

\(R_e = \frac{1}{2} R (1 - k)\)

\(2 = \frac{1}{2} \times 8 \times (1 - k)\)

\(2 = 4(1 - k)\)

\(1 - k = 0.5\)

\(k = \mathbf{0.5}\) (50% duty ratio)

Verification

At \(k = 0.5\): \(R_e = 0.5 \times 8 \times (1 - 0.5) = 0.5 \times 8 \times 0.5 = 2\;\Omega\). Correct.

Limits: At \(k = 0\), \(R_e = 4\;\Omega\) (max). At \(k = 1\), \(R_e = 0\;\Omega\) (normal). Our value of 2 is right in the middle, consistent with 50% duty.

Example 6 — Voltage Boost Calculation

A V/F controlled IM has \(R_s = 2\;\Omega\) and draws rated current \(I_s = 10\) A. The rated V/f ratio is 460 V / 60 Hz = 7.67 V/Hz. Find the required terminal voltage at 5 Hz to maintain rated flux.

Step 1: Ideal voltage at 5 Hz (no boost)

\(V_{\text{ideal}} = (V/f) \times f = 7.67 \times 5 = 38.3\) V (line)

Step 2: Stator resistance voltage drop

\(\Delta V = R_s \cdot I_s = 2 \times 10 = 20\) V

Step 3: Required terminal voltage with boost

\(V_{\text{terminal}} = V_{\text{ideal}} + \Delta V = 38.3 + 20 = \mathbf{58.3 \text{ V}}\)

Why This Matters

Without boost, the airgap voltage would be only \(38.3 - 20 = 18.3\) V instead of the needed 38.3 V — the flux would be less than half of rated! The 20 V boost is absolutely critical at this low frequency. Notice that at rated frequency (460 V supply), the same 20 V drop is only 4.3% of supply — negligible. But at 5 Hz it's 52% of the ideal voltage — devastating without compensation.

Example 7 — Constant Power Region: Torque at Twice Rated Speed

An IM rated at 50 Hz develops 100 N·m at rated speed. Using V/F control, the frequency is increased to 100 Hz (voltage stays at rated). Find the available torque at 100 Hz, assuming operation in the constant power region.

Step 1: Rated power

At 50 Hz, synchronous speed \(\omega_{s1} = 2\pi \times 50 / (P/2)\). Let's use the ratio approach.

Since frequency doubles: \(\omega_{s2} = 2\omega_{s1}\)

Step 2: In the constant power region

\(P_{\text{rated}} = T_1 \times \omega_{s1} \approx 100 \times \omega_{s1}\)

At 100 Hz: \(T_2 = P_{\text{rated}} / \omega_{s2} = P_{\text{rated}} / (2\omega_{s1}) = T_1 / 2\)

\(T_2 = 100/2 = \mathbf{50 \text{ N}\cdot\text{m}}\)

Rule of Thumb

In the constant power region, torque is inversely proportional to speed. Double the speed, half the torque. This is the same trade-off as in DC motor field weakening.

Example 8 — Normalized Torque Equation

An IM has \(s_m = 0.15\). Using the normalized torque equation, find the developed torque (as a fraction of \(T_{\max}\)) when the motor operates at slip \(s = 0.05\).

Using the normalized torque formula:

\(\frac{T_d}{T_{\max}} = \frac{2 s \cdot s_m}{s^2 + s_m^2}\)

\(\frac{T_d}{T_{\max}} = \frac{2 \times 0.05 \times 0.15}{0.05^2 + 0.15^2} = \frac{0.015}{0.0025 + 0.0225} = \frac{0.015}{0.025} = \mathbf{0.6}\)

So the motor produces 60% of its maximum torque at this operating point.

Sanity Check

The slip (0.05) is one-third of \(s_m\) (0.15), so we're on the "left side" of the torque-speed curve (stable operating region), well below the peak. Getting 60% of \(T_{\max}\) is reasonable — the curve rises steeply from zero slip and is already quite high at \(s/s_m = 1/3\).

Problem 1

A 3-phase, 6-pole, 50 Hz induction motor operates with V/F control. At rated conditions, the motor runs at 960 rpm. (a) Find the rated slip. (b) Find the motor speed when the supply frequency is 35 Hz (same slip). (c) If the rated torque is 80 N·m, find the power developed at both frequencies.

(a) Rated slip:

\(N_s = 120 \times 50/6 = 1000\) rpm

\(s = (1000 - 960)/1000 = 0.04\) (4%)

(b) Speed at 35 Hz:

\(N_{s,35} = 120 \times 35/6 = 700\) rpm

\(N_{r,35} = 700(1 - 0.04) = \mathbf{672 \text{ rpm}}\)

(c) Power at each frequency:

At 50 Hz: \(P_{50} = T \times \omega = 80 \times 2\pi \times 960/60 = 80 \times 100.53 = \mathbf{8042 \text{ W}}\)

At 35 Hz: \(P_{35} = 80 \times 2\pi \times 672/60 = 80 \times 70.37 = \mathbf{5630 \text{ W}}\)

Power scales with speed (as expected in the constant-torque region).

Problem 2

A wound-rotor IM has \(R'_r = 0.4\;\Omega\) and \(X_s + X'_r = 2.0\;\Omega\) (all referred to stator). Neglect \(R_s\). (a) Find \(s_m\) with no external resistance. (b) Find the external resistance \(R'_e\) needed to achieve \(s_m = 1\) (maximum torque at standstill). (c) Does \(T_{\max}\) change?

(a) \(s_m = R'_r/(X_s + X'_r) = 0.4/2.0 = \mathbf{0.2}\)

(b) For \(s_m = 1\):

\(s_m = (R'_r + R'_e)/(X_s + X'_r)\)

\(1 = (0.4 + R'_e)/2.0\)

\(R'_e = 2.0 - 0.4 = \mathbf{1.6\;\Omega}\)

(c) No! \(T_{\max} = 3V^2/[2\omega_s(X_s + X'_r)]\). It depends only on voltage and reactances, not on rotor resistance. \(T_{\max}\) is unchanged.

Problem 3

A static rotor resistance controller uses \(R = 10\;\Omega\). Find: (a) The range of equivalent external resistance \(R_e\) achievable. (b) The duty ratio for \(R_e = 1.5\;\Omega\). (c) If the motor rotor current is \(I_r = 15\) A, find the DC link current.

(a) Range of \(R_e\):

At \(k = 0\): \(R_e = 0.5 \times 10 \times 1 = 5\;\Omega\) (maximum)

At \(k = 1\): \(R_e = 0.5 \times 10 \times 0 = 0\;\Omega\) (minimum)

Range: \(\mathbf{0 \leq R_e \leq 5\;\Omega}\)

(b) Duty ratio for \(R_e = 1.5\;\Omega\):

\(1.5 = 0.5 \times 10 \times (1 - k)\)

\(1 - k = 0.3\)

\(k = \mathbf{0.7}\)

(c) DC link current:

\(I_d = \sqrt{3/2} \times I_r = \sqrt{1.5} \times 15 = 1.225 \times 15 = \mathbf{18.37 \text{ A}}\)

Problem 4

An IM driven by a V/F controller operates at 30 Hz. The motor parameters are \(R_s = 1.5\;\Omega\), rated V/f = 380 V / 50 Hz = 7.6 V/Hz. The rated stator current is 12 A. (a) Find the ideal terminal voltage at 30 Hz. (b) Find the required voltage boost. (c) Find the actual terminal voltage needed.

(a) Ideal voltage at 30 Hz:

\(V_{\text{ideal}} = 7.6 \times 30 = \mathbf{228 \text{ V (line)}}\)

(b) Voltage boost (current-dependent):

\(\Delta V = R_s \times I_s = 1.5 \times 12 = \mathbf{18 \text{ V}}\)

(c) Actual terminal voltage:

\(V_{\text{actual}} = 228 + 18 = \mathbf{246 \text{ V}}\)

For comparison, at 50 Hz the boost fraction is \(18/380 = 4.7\%\) (small). At 30 Hz it's \(18/228 = 7.9\%\) (more significant). At 10 Hz it would be \(18/76 = 23.7\%\) (critical).
